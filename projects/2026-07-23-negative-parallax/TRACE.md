### Provisional 1 — Bailer-Jones 2015 — CONFIRMED at source, stronger than expected

One load-bearing full-text extraction spent (arXiv PDF 1507.02105v2, two chunk retrievals
against the same document; the academic-paper tool's PDF converter is broken in this
environment — `libxcb.so.1` missing — so the shared extraction budget carried this read).
Verbatim, now on record:

> "An astrometric reduction can indeed produce a negative parallax, because the 'measured'
> parallax is the result of reducing a set of noisy angular measurements. It corresponds to
> the parallactic displacement being in the opposite direction on the sky from that expected
> due to the movement of the observer along the baseline. It does not correspond to a
> negative distance, because r > 0 by definition."

> "This is bad, as negative parallaxes are valid measurements."
(said of the naive 1/ϖ mode estimator, whose posterior under an improper uniform prior puts
the mode at r = ∞ for non-positive parallaxes)

> "when we use a prior, negative or zero parallaxes provide information in agreement with
> our intuition: they are just noisy measurements which happened to end up non-positive.
> They imply the distance is likely to be large (up to the constraint imposed by prior
> knowledge), and the range of probable solutions depends on the size of the parallax error"

From the abstract (verbatim): estimating distance "is not trivial once the fractional
parallax error is larger than about 20%, which will be the case for about 80% of stars in
the Gaia catalogue." And from the worked experiments: the naive estimator that must reject
non-positive parallaxes ends, at high fractional error, having "had to reject over 95% of
the data."

### Provisional 3 — "DR3 fraction-negative by magnitude/type" — WITHDRAWN as posed, superseded by a two-regime finding

The construct assumed a documented single figure. The discipline does not carve the
population that way. What the primaries actually hold:

- **Rybizki et al. 2021** (MNRAS, DOI 10.1093/mnras/stab3588, abstract read at source): a
  neural "astrometric fidelity" classifier for EDR3, trained on "an extensive sample of
  manifestly bad astrometric solutions, with parallax that is negative at > 4.5 sigma" —
  i.e. the discipline's operational definition of *manifestly bad* is: negative by more
  than the solution's own claimed uncertainty can excuse.
- **Fabricius et al. 2021**, A&A 649, A5 (catalogue validation, read at aanda.org full
  HTML): 3.04 million EDR3 sources with parallax_over_error < −5 are "clearly spurious"
  (against 192.21 million with parallax_over_error > +5, giving an ≈1.6% estimate of
  spurious solutions hiding *among the positives*); assumption stated verbatim: "We can
  reasonably assume that a disturbance giving rise to a negative (spurious) parallax, for
  example image parameters affected by duplicity or crowding, could just as well have
  produced a spurious solution with a positive parallax and with roughly the same
  probability." And the recommendation, verbatim: "When evaluating parallaxes for a
  particular sample of sources, where only positive parallaxes are selected, we therefore
  recommend to also select a similar sample, but with negative parallaxes in order to
  evaluate the likely fraction of spurious results."
- **Gaia DR3 documentation** (§14.1.2, read at source,
  https://gea.esac.esa.int/archive/documentation/GDR3/Catalogue_consolidation/chap_cu9val/sec_cu9val_introduction/ssec_cu9val_intro_astro_accuracy.html):
  "Spurious solutions (objects with very negative parallaxes) have been significantly
