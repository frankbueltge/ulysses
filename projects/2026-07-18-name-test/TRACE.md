### Conjecture 2 resolved: "anything goes" is not his principle — VERIFIED, the cliché collapses

The score marked as CONJECTURE the reading that "anything goes" is ironic. The primary
settles it, in Feyerabend's own words, in the front matter of this very edition:

> "An example is the end of Chapter 1: 'anything goes' is not a 'principle' I hold — I
> do not think that 'principles' can be used and fruitfully discussed outside the
> concrete research situation they are supposed to affect — but the terrified
> exclamation of a rationalist who takes a closer look at history." (AM3, Preface,
> pp. vii–viii)

The sentence the slogan comes from is itself conditional. End of ch. 1: "To those who
look at the rich material provided by history... it will become clear that there is
only one principle that can be defended under all circumstances and in all stages of
human development. It is the principle: anything goes." (AM3, pp. 18–19). The
surviving "principle" is the reductio of the demand for one — not a first principle
but what is left to a rationalist who insists on all-circumstance validity.

End of ch. 2 states the book's actual intention: "My intention is not to replace one
set of general rules by another such set: my intention is, rather, to convince the
reader that all methodologies, even the most obvious ones, have their limits."
(AM3, p. 23). And: "the demonstrations and the rhetorics used do not express any
'deep convictions' of mine. They merely show how easy it is to lead people by the
nose in a rational way." (AM3, p. 23). Appendix 1 deflates the slogan further via
the Wigner anecdote: "'Anything goes' does not mean that I shall read every single
paper that has been written — God forbid!" (AM3, p. 159).

**Consequence, registered as a score correction (not silently):** the score's §2
framing of the non-fit ("a maximally methodical practice vs a book that argues no
methodological rule survives") was built partly on the cliché. Feyerabend's target is
*universal, ahistorical* method — rules claimed valid under all circumstances — not
the existence of local, situated, revisable rules. A practice whose protocol is
versioned, amendable and explicitly provisional ("the toolbox is provisional... never
a compulsory menu", Protocol v4 §11) is not, as such, his opponent. The opposition
narrows to one precise point — see below. SCORE §10 carries the correction; the
original wording stands preserved in the score body and in git history.

### Conjecture 1 resolved: which "error" the book touches — near-orthogonality, one sharp friction

The word "Irrtum" occurs **once** in the whole book: in the title of Ernst Mach's
*Erkenntnis und Irrtum*, cited approvingly in the Introduction as the 19th-century
matter-of-course of "an elastic and historically informed methodology" (AM3, p. 10
n. 5, citing Mach, *Erkenntnis und Irrtum*, Neudruck, Wissenschaftliche
Buchgesellschaft, Darmstadt, 1980, p. 200). "Error" appears ~21 times in ~280 pages,
mostly incidental (margins of error, errors of the senses, Hoppe's "trial and error"
description of Galileo's telescope work, AM3 p. 83). Where it is load-bearing,
Feyerabend uses error in three senses, none of which is this practice's:

1. **Error as retrospective label produced by the methodology under attack** — his
   central use, and he puts the word in scare quotes: "These 'deviations', these
   'errors', are preconditions of progress." (AM3, p. 158). The quotes do real work:
   for Feyerabend they are only "errors" relative to a law-and-order image of science
   whose authority the book denies. He does not propose keeping and correcting them;
   he denies the labelling system's jurisdiction.
2. **Error as progress-enabling rule-violation:** "there is not a single rule,
   however plausible, and however firmly grounded in epistemology, that is not
   violated at some time or other... such violations are not accidental events...
   they are necessary for progress." (AM3, p. 14).
3. **Error as irrational element permitted its way:** ideas "survived because
   prejudice, passion, conceit, errors, sheer pigheadedness, in short because all the
   elements that characterize the context of discovery, opposed the dictates of
   reason and because these irrational elements were permitted to have their way."
   (AM3, p. 116).

Nowhere in the chapters read is error a *disclosed, preserved, correctable difference
inside a rule-bound apparatus* — the practice's sense (Hamming's correctable
distance; the disclosed-vs-hidden distinction of S34–S36; Rheinberger's productive
differential). Strictly: Feyerabend's "error" exists only relative to the method it
violates; the practice's "Irrtum" exists only inside the apparatus that registers it.
The two concepts pass through each other almost without contact.

**The one sharp friction, registered honestly:** the practice does hold rules claimed
valid under all circumstances — the PROHIBITIONS ("bind absolutely") and the
verifiability rule. AM3's summary indicts exactly this shape of rule: "the principles
of critical rationalism (take falsifications seriously; increase content; avoid ad
hoc hypotheses; 'be honest' — whatever that means; and so on)... give an inadequate
account of the past development of science as a whole and are liable to hinder it in
the future" (AM3, p. 157), and "There is not a single rule that remains valid under
all circumstances and not a single agency to which appeal can always be made."
(AM3, p. 158). "'Be honest' — whatever that means" is aimed at precisely the family
of norms this practice treats as absolute. So the book, read at the primary, is not
this practice's ancestor but its live counterposition — it would refuse the absolutes
the practice stands on. That is a stronger and more usable finding than the cliché
opposition the score started from.

### The comparison (F1–F3 from tick 2 against U1–U6)

- **F1 (scare-quoted retrospective label; the labelling system's jurisdiction
  denied) vs U2 (the register).** The practice *is* the labelling system Feyerabend
  refuses. Where AM3 writes "These 'deviations', these 'errors', are preconditions of
  progress" (p. 158) with quotes that deny the tribunal's authority, the practice
  convenes the tribunal on itself nightly and publishes the case files, typed A–H.
  Same word, opposite operation: he de-authorises the judgement of error; the
  practice institutionalises self-judgement.
- **F2 (progress-enabling rule-violation) vs U3/U6.** The practice's registered
  errors are almost never rule-violations — they are failures *inside*
  rule-compliance (wrong inference, inaccessible primary, unreliable instrument).
  Where a rule is actually violated, the practice's licensed response is
  repair-and-disclose, never "necessary for progress" (S34: "I am only forbidden
  from keeping it"). The one genuine Feyerabendian resonance: the practice values
  what an error *opens* ("The error is productive: it opens Track C…",
  `works/fehlerkataster-007.md`) — but only after registration, i.e. only inside the
  jurisdiction F1 rejects.
- **F3 (irrational elements permitted their way) vs U1/U4.** Nothing in this
  practice is permitted its way unregistered; even the correction process's own
  pathology has a register type (H, oscillation/overcorrection). The practice's
  nearest kin to F3 — U4's demand that the error mechanism actually run — runs the
  mechanism *under a seed*, reproducibly. What Feyerabend calls pigheadedness
  permitted its way, this practice would book, type and correct.

**Summary of the comparison: homonymy, not descent.** "Error as Method" and "Against
Method" share a surface negation of method; underneath, the genitives run opposite
ways. The practice's Irrtum requires the method as its **medium** (an error exists
for the practice once registered in the apparatus); Feyerabend's error requires
method only as its **foil** (a label relative to it, a violation of it, an exception
permitted against it). Tick 2 found near-orthogonality reading from the book's side;
the practice-side collection confirms it from this side.
