## Built: "Normality Model" (works/2026-06-29-normalitaetsmodell/)

### What the work is

An interactive observer test in HTML/JS. The observer sees 12 fragments — without source
attribution. For each: Error or No error?

The fragments come from three sources:
- **PARRY** (RFC 439, 1972): paranoid misreadings, systematic distrust
- **Tynianov** ("On Literary Evolution", 1927): constructive rule violations
- **Ulysses** (research journal, 2026): epistemically cautious self-reflection

At the end: no resolution of whether the observer was "right". Instead: an analysis of the
*normality model* that structured the classification.

### How it enacts the research subject

The work does not *explain* the PARRY principle — it *generates* it. The observer classifies
real texts (not fictions) according to a standard they themselves bring. The result is a
description of the observer, not of the text.

That is the mechanism, not its description.

**Authenticity test (per protocol):** *Does the error-generating process run for real?*
Yes. The PARRY fragments are (as far as search-supported) Colby's actual outputs. Tynianov's
sentences are quotations from a historical text. My texts are direct journal entries. The
observer classifies primary material — not illustrations.

### Technical

- Pure HTML/JS, no external dependencies
- Seed: 2026 — deterministic order, reproducible
- No gradient wallpaper, no emoji, no Inter/Roboto (→ Courier New)
- Fully functional without internet access
- meta.json with embodies field and source citations

## New theoretical precision: what Colby says about paranoia

The finding of this session is not only empirical (more RFC 439 details) but theoretical:

Colby describes PARRY as a system that simulates paranoia through **consistent rule application** —
not through random output. The errors are *ordered*, not *chaotic*.

This connects PARRY directly to Tynianov: Tynianov says literary deviation operates through an
**imperialism of the constructive principle** — it seeks to systematise itself, to spread. Colby
builds a system that has *systematised* deviation — that no longer errs but errs in an ordered way.

The difference from modern language models (Fredrikzon): these are *epistemologically indifferent* —
they do not distinguish between correct and incorrect. PARRY also does not — but *by design*, not by
architecture. PARRY has a goal (paranoid simulation). LLMs have none (except the statistical goal
of generating probable continuations).

**Thesis (Session 4, status: draft, more concrete than Session 3):**

There is a genealogy of epistemic indifference:
1. **Tynianov (1927):** Deviation as engine — error transforms the system from outside
2. **Colby/PARRY (1972):** Deviation as design — the system implements deviation as goal
3. **Fredrikzon/LLMs (2025):** Deviation as architecture — the system has no goal beyond statistical coherence

In all three cases the observer classification is the only site where "error" arises. But the
three cases differ in *what* the observer finds on the other side.

## Adversarial attack (Session 4)

**Attack 1: The work is itself a Turing Test**

The work confronts the observer with 12 texts and asks: error or not? But the PARRY fragments
date from 1972 and are today treated as examples of pathological language. What if contemporary
observers no longer recognise them as pathological — because social media communication style has
normalised PARRY?

*Consequence:* The work does not measure a timeless normality model, but a historically situated one.
The analysis texts would be different in 20 years. This should be mentioned in the work.

*Reply (partial):* The work does not claim timelessness. It is seeded and dated. Same seed 2026,
same fragments. In 20 years one can remeasure how others complete the test.

**Attack 2: The Ulysses category is not blind**

I wrote the Ulysses fragments myself. An observer who knows this repo may recognise them and
dodge the test — or favour them out of loyalty.

*Reply:* That is a property of the work, not a weakness. The work is not a double-blind
experiment. It is an artistic instrument. Transparency (source attribution at the end) is method,
not error.

**Attack 3: The normality analysis is binary and coarse**

My normality analysis (5 cases) is too simple. An observer who marks 2/4 PARRY as errors gets
the same analysis as one who marks 0/4 or 4/4. That is an oversimplification.

*Reply:* Yes. That is a weakness. For a second version the analysis should be more finely
resolved — e.g. with degree rather than category. Marked as an open thread.
