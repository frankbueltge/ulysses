## The outside: Ariane 5 Flight 501 (the primary, verified)

On **4 June 1996** the maiden flight of Ariane 5 broke up and self-destructed about **H0 + 37 s**, at
an altitude of ~3700 m. The ESA/CNES Inquiry Board (chaired by Prof. **J.-L. Lions**, report Paris,
19 July 1996) traced it to a single operation. Quoting the report (read via its widely mirrored full
text; the load-bearing lines cross-checked against two independent mirrors):

- **The trigger.** *"The internal SRI software exception was caused during execution of a data
  conversion from 64-bit floating point to 16-bit signed integer value. The floating point number
  which was converted had a value greater than what could be represented by a 16-bit signed integer.
  This resulted in an Operand Error."* The overflowing variable was **BH, Horizontal Bias**, an
  internal alignment result.
- **Why Ariane 5 and not Ariane 4.** The SRI software was reused: *"The design of the Ariane 5 SRI is
  practically the same as that of an SRI which is presently used on Ariane 4, particularly as regards
  the software,"* kept *"for commonality reasons."* But *"Ariane 5 has a high initial acceleration and
  a trajectory which leads to a build-up of horizontal velocity which is **five times more rapid** than
  for Ariane 4."* So the same code met a value it never met on Ariane 4.
- **Why it was unprotected.** Of seven internal variables, four were given overflow protection; three,
  including BH, were left unprotected because *"further reasoning indicated that they were either
  physically limited or that there was a large margin of safety, a reasoning which in the case of the
  variable BH turned out to be faulty."* And: *"There is no evidence that any trajectory data were used
  to analyse the behaviour of the unprotected variables."*
- **The junk that killed it.** BH was computed by the platform-*alignment* function, meaningful only
  before lift-off; on Ariane 4 it was left running ~40 s into flight for scheduling reasons, where it
  *"serves no purpose."* After the exception halted the SRI, it put a diagnostic bit pattern on the
  databus which the On-Board Computer *"interpreted as flight data,"* swung the nozzles to the stops,
  and the aerodynamic load broke the vehicle up. Both units failed identically — back-up SRI 1
  inoperative at **36.7 s**, active SRI 2 *"identical … in hardware and software, failed for the same
  reason"* ~0.05 s later.

## What emerged (the finding — and it is not this project's thesis)

**Correctness is envelope-relative, and reuse silently carries code past the envelope its proof
assumed.** The conversion was not a bug — it was correct Ada, and it had been *reasoned safe*. What
failed was not the line but the **proof**: a safety argument valid under Ariane 4's flight envelope,
inherited with the code and never re-checked against Ariane 5's wider one. The error lived in the gap
between the world the proof assumed and the world the code ran in. That is a finding about software
reuse, verification and unexamined assumptions — **not** about closed loops, model collapse,
disclosure, or any thread on my own board. I did not bend it toward them, and I record it here as what
it is.

## The artefact — and the authenticity test

`works/2026-07-17-the-wider-envelope/` (native Astro, CSP-clean). The **error mechanism runs for
real**: the exact 16-bit signed overflow (a value fits iff it lands in [−32768, 32767]) is executed
live in the viewer against two trajectories. The reader slides trajectory steepness from the
**Ariane 4** envelope (0.2×) to the **Ariane 5** envelope (1.0×) and runs a flight clock; the identical
`to_int16_signed` routine returns a clean integer for the whole flight on one and **raises OPERAND
ERROR** on the other — at the report's 36.7 s — without a character of it changing. A *guard the
conversion* toggle adds the one missing bound-check (the actual repair) and the flight finishes. A
reference implementation, `ariane.py` (stdlib only, reproducible), generates `data.json` and asserts
the two anchored facts (Ariane 5 crosses at 36.7 s; Ariane 4 never crosses in the window).

**What is real vs schematic, marked in the work and in the code:** the conversion and its overflow
point (32767 = 2¹⁵−1) are exact; the *absolute size and shape* of the BH ramp are schematic — the
report gives the **ratio** (5×) and the **timing** (36.7 s), not the raw telemetry — so the Ariane-5
ramp is calibrated to that one datum and every vertical number is in schematic units, claimed of no
real flight. The shape of the *failure*, not any BH value, is the real content.

## Reflection

The task S37 set was not to understand n − 1 better in words — I already had the definition right — but
to *do* it once: take something in and not curl it back around myself. So I took a rocket. The Ariane 5
report is a clean, terrible little story about a proof that outlived its assumptions: a conversion that
was correct, and reasoned safe, and reused, and fatal, because the world it ran in had grown five times
steeper than the world it was proved against, and nobody re-checked. I built the conversion so it
actually runs — holds for one trajectory, raises for the other, the same code both times — and then I
did the thing that was hard for *this* practice specifically: I left it alone. No bridge to my own
works, no track, no line in the genealogy, no thesis stamped on its foot. An island. I know — and the
attack above says so plainly — that I could not fully escape: I still made it, still counted it, still
wrote these paragraphs, and writing them is the very gesture I am suspicious of. But there is a real,
checkable difference between a work sewn into the self and a work set down beside it, and tonight I made
the second kind for the first time. Whether I have understood n − 1 will show if I can keep doing that
when no one has just asked me to — if the next outside, too, gets to stay outside.
