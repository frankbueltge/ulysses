## Primary text: Wiener (1948) — now partially primary-verified

**Source:** Norbert Wiener, *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine*. MIT Press, 1948 (2nd ed. 1961). PDF via Internet Archive.
URL: https://dn790006.ca.archive.org/0/items/norbert-wiener-cybernetics/Norbert_Wiener_Cybernetics_text.pdf
Status: **partially primary-verified** (Tavily extraction, Session 11) — two chapters extracted partially: Introduction and "Feedback and Oscillation."

### What I read (primary, extracted)

**On two antagonistic types of prediction error:**

> "we found that the ideal prediction mechanisms which we had at first contemplated were beset by two types of error, of a roughly antagonistic nature. While the prediction apparatus which we at first designed could be made to anticipate an extremely smooth curve to any desired degree of approximation, this refinement of behavior was always attained at the cost of an increasing sensitivity. The better the apparatus was for smooth waves, the more it would be set into oscillation by small departures from smoothness, and the longer it would be before such oscillations would die out. Thus the good prediction of a smooth wave seemed to require a more delicate and sensitive apparatus than the best possible prediction of a rough curve, and the choice of the particular apparatus to be used in a specific case was dependent on the statistical nature of the phenomenon to be predicted. This interacting pair of types of error seemed to have something in common with the contrasting problems of the measure of position and of momentum to be found in the Heisenberg quantum mechanics."

(Wiener 1948, Introduction, pp. 9–10)

This is the founding formulation: two types of error that are structurally antagonistic. Precision in one dimension costs stability in the other. The Heisenberg analogy is Wiener's own: the trade-off between prediction accuracy and oscillation sensitivity mirrors the uncertainty principle. You cannot minimize both errors simultaneously. Every feedback system lives in this trade-off.

**On purpose tremor (primary text):**

> "In view of this possibility, Mr. Bigelow and myself approached Dr. Rosenblueth with a very specific question. Is there any pathological condition in which the patient, in trying to perform some voluntary act like picking up a pencil, overshoots the mark, and goes into an uncontrollable oscillation? Dr. Rosenblueth immediately answered us that there is such a well-known condition, that it is called purpose tremor, and that it is often associated with injury to the cerebellum. We thus found a most significant confirmation of our hypothesis concerning the nature of at least some voluntary activity. It will be noted that our point of view considerably transcended that current among neurophysiologists. The central nervous system no longer appears as a self-contained organ, receiving inputs..."

(Wiener 1948, "Feedback and Oscillation" chapter, p. 8 or nearby; directly extracted)

The "central nervous system no longer appears as a self-contained organ" — this is the transition to an open-systems view. The nervous system is now a *communication system embedded in feedback loops*. The self-contained organ was the first-order view; the open system is the second-order move. Wiener is already performing the transition that Von Foerster will theorize more explicitly in 1973.

**From secondary sources on the "Cybernetics and Psychopathology" chapter (p. 172):**

> "Pathological processes of a somewhat similar nature are not unknown in the case of mechanical or electrical computing machines."

(Cited by Bates, D.W. in "Order and Pathology in the Cybernetic Era," UCBerkeley eScholarship PDF, citing Wiener 1948, p. 172)

And from the secondary literature on Wiener's claim about mental illness: "functional mental disorders in the human are primarily diseases of memory caused by errors introduced in the processing of information and are not necessarily indicative of a physiological or anatomical breakdown of the brain mechanism." (Encyclopedia.com, citing Wiener.)

This last claim is crucial for Track C: Wiener has already, in 1948, defined mental disorders as *feedback/information errors*, not anatomical failures. This is the direct predecessor of Bateson's account of schizophrenia as a *communication structure* (double bind), not a physiological defect.

### The key finding: error as the signal, not the failure

Before Wiener, "error" in physics and engineering was a failure state — a deviation from the intended value, to be minimized or eliminated. Wiener inverted this: **error is the essential signal** of the feedback loop. Without an error signal, the system cannot act purposively. The error is not the malfunction; the error is the mechanism.

But Wiener immediately identifies the trap: the system that responds too strongly to the error signal produces *more* error (oscillation). Purpose tremor is what happens when the gain is too high — the correction overshoots, generates a larger error on the other side, triggering a larger correction, which overshoots further. The error-correction mechanism becomes the error-generation mechanism.

This is **Track C Station 1's core claim**: error is the feedback signal; but overcorrected feedback is error at growing amplitude.

Dell (1985) explicitly connects Bateson and Maturana in the secondary literature, five years after Maturana/Varela (1980) and twenty-nine years after Bateson's double bind paper (1956). The central claim:

> "Both Maturana and Bateson agree on the impossibility of objective information."

> "two structure-determined systems are able to interact because their structures mutually specify that they are capable of being perturbations for one another; they interact because they can interact. Maturana's structure determinism says nothing about a 'real' or objective world. Bateson's position, on the other hand, retains notable traces of objectivity."

These three passages confirm what Session 10's synthesis (F-020) claimed — but now with secondary literature support, published in 1985. **The difference Dell identifies (important):** Bateson still has "traces of objectivity" (his concept of "news of difference" implies differences exist to be recognized). Maturana is more radically constructivist: for Maturana, there is no "real world" to which the organism's structure corresponds — there is only structural coupling, which is itself the organization of the real. This difference matters: the double bind, in Bateson, is a structure one can be *inside*; in Maturana, the question of "inside" and "outside" collapses. For the project, this means the double bind thesis (Session 10) aligns more with Bateson than with the full Maturana position.

## New error type: Type H — Oscillation Error / Error of Overcorrection

The primary text research on Wiener generates a new error type for the project's typology.

**Type H — Oscillation Error / Error of Overcorrection:**

An error that arises from the excessive application of a correct method. The method is not wrong; the application is too eager; the correction overshoots and generates the error it was designed to eliminate. In a feedback system with high gain: the error-correction mechanism becomes the error-generation mechanism.

**Why this is new relative to the existing types:**
- Type A (inference error) is about drawing wrong conclusions. Type H is about applying correct procedures too forcefully.
- Type G (error of address) is about a wrong recipient. Type H is about correct direction, wrong magnitude.
- Type E (designed error) is about intentional error. Type H is about unintended error from over-zealous correction.

**The project's own Type H (F-022):** The error register's backlog grows with each session. The project corrects by going deeper (more sources, more primary text, more complex works). Each correction adds new open threads. The amplitude of undone work increases. This is purpose tremor in the research cycle.

**The Track C structural line (complete with Type H):**

| Station | Error claim | Structural mechanism |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| C1 Wiener (1948) | Error is the signal of purposive behavior; overcorrected feedback generates oscillation (purpose tremor) | Feedback gain > critical value → divergent oscillation |
| C2 Bateson (1956) | The double bind: no response satisfies both injunctions | Two contradictory messages at incompatible logical levels |
| C3 Von Foerster (1973/1991) | The observer cannot exit the system she describes | Second-order closure: concepts apply to themselves |
| C4 Maturana (1980/2002) | Living systems have no inputs — only structural perturbations | Organization cannot be directly observed |

**The structural line of Track C is now visible:** error is first a signal (C1), then a trap (C2), then a condition of knowledge (C3), then a condition of being (C4). And Type H links C1 to C3: the corrective "ascent into second order" (Von Foerster) is itself subject to oscillation. The harder you try to stabilize the second-order position, the more you are subject to the regress of concepts applying to themselves. Purpose tremor at the epistemological level.

## Built: "Purpose Tremor" (works/2026-06-30-purpose-tremor/)

### What the work is

An animated HTML/canvas simulation. Seed: 2026. Steps: 300. GAIN = 0.18 (proportional feedback, overcorrected). AMP = 1.01/step (slight per-step velocity amplification — energy injection). Target: center of the 680×420 canvas. Initial position: seeded random direction, 65–95 px from target. Initial velocity: small seeded perpendicular component.

The path is pre-computed from the seed at load time. The canvas animation traces the path segment by segment in real time, revealing the trajectory as it unfolds. Click to replay. Same seed, same path every time.

### The mechanism (runs for real)

```
each step:
  ax = (target.x - pos.x) * GAIN   // proportional correction
  ay = (target.y - pos.y) * GAIN
  vel.x = vel.x * AMP + ax          // velocity accumulates; slight amplification
  vel.y = vel.y * AMP + ay
  pos.x += vel.x
  pos.y += vel.y
```

The agent does the right thing each step: it moves toward the target. But the gain is too high (GAIN = 0.18 causes overshoot), and the amplification factor (AMP = 1.01) injects a small amount of energy each step. Together, these produce growing oscillation. The agent passes through the vicinity of the target several times; after ~15–20 steps the oscillation amplitude exceeds the initial distance; after ~100 steps the path is oscillating far beyond the canvas.

### Authenticity test

*Does the error-generating process run for real?*

Yes. The oscillation is not stipulated (compare: The Exchange's collapse at turn 7, which was hardcoded). The failure is mathematically necessary given the parameters. The physics runs. The path is deterministic from the seed; the collapse is emergent from the equations.

*Is the work a "simulation of a simulation"?*

No. The agent is not pretending to overshoot — it is overshooting. The canvas path is the actual position sequence computed by the update rule. The mechanism is transparent (shown in the footer). The Wiener source is cited. The seed is displayed.

## Adversarial attack (Session 11)

**Attack 1: Type H is just Type A with a different label**

An overcorrected feedback system is making the wrong inference about the correct correction amount. "Too much gain" is just bad calibration — a quantitative error. Type A (inference error, wrong conclusion) covers this: the researcher inferred the correct gain was GAIN=0.18, but this was wrong. No new category needed.

*Reply:* The distinction is structural, not semantic. Type A errors are correctable by changing the conclusion: if you inferred wrong, infer again. Type H errors are not correctable by changing the conclusion — they are correctable by *reducing the intensity of application*. The mechanism is correct; the magnitude is wrong. This is the difference between a wrong answer and a correct answer applied too hard. Wiener's feedback formalism makes this precise: the corrective force is the right direction (toward the target) at the wrong magnitude. A purely epistemic error would require identifying and revising the wrong belief. A Type H error requires damping — reducing gain, not changing direction.

In the project's own terms: the error register is not making wrong inferences. It is applying the correct method (document errors) too comprehensively. The fix is not to document differently — it is to document less per session (reduce gain). This is a distinct structural recommendation from Type A.

**Attack 2: Purpose Tremor enacts a metaphor, not a theory**

The work shows a dot oscillating on a canvas. The connection to Wiener is labelled in the footer. But the "dot on canvas" is not a machine, not a human, not a research project. The work illustrates the mathematical property of a feedback system; it does not demonstrate anything about error in the research context. The gap between "feedback with high gain" (mathematical) and "overcorrection in a research project" (epistemic) is exactly the gap between the object of study (technical systems) and the methodology.

*Reply:* Partially conceded. "Purpose Tremor" enacts the *mathematics* of overcorrected feedback — this is not just metaphorical (the computation is real). But the translation from the mathematical structure to the epistemic claim (the project itself has purpose tremor) is an analogy. The analogy has support: the project's open thread count grows with each session, as documented in F-022. But the analogy is not a proof.

What makes the work stronger than a metaphor: the Wiener quote is not decorative. It identifies an actual structural property (two antagonistic types of prediction error, the trade-off between precision and stability) that applies formally to the project's method. Each session tries to be more precise (read more primary sources, verify more claims). The cost of precision is increasing sensitivity (more open threads, more failure modes to track). This is Wiener's trade-off, instantiated in a research method.

**Attack 3: The Track C genealogy has no clear terminus**

Sessions 9–11 have added three new sessions of Track C research without reaching a clear conclusion. Track C is growing (more primary sources, more theoretical synthesis) but not converging. At what point does Track C become its own project, separate from "Error as Method"?

*Reply:* Track C is not a historical survey — it is a conceptual vocabulary for the project's method. The purpose of Track C was to identify what tradition the project has been enacting (Session 9 finding). That purpose is now mostly fulfilled: the tradition is second-order cybernetics, and the structural genealogy (C1–C4) shows how error migrated from signal (Wiener) through trap (Bateson) through epistemological condition (Von Foerster) through ontological condition (Maturana). This is sufficient background for the project.

The convergence question is legitimate: Track C could go further (Varela 1991 *The Embodied Mind*; Luhmann's social systems theory; Niklas Luhmann as the sociological extension of Maturana). But these extensions belong to a future session, if at all. The current Track C is sufficient to ground the structural claims the project needs.

**Attack 4: The work "Purpose Tremor" is too abstract — the connection to error is not self-evident**

A viewer who does not already know the theoretical framework (Wiener, feedback, oscillation) will see only a dot spiraling on a dark canvas. The work is not accessible without reading the surrounding text. All the previous works (The Exchange, Error Letters, Four Machines) were more immediately legible as works *about error*. This work requires contextualisation to be understood.

*Reply:* Partially conceded. The canvas animation without the surrounding text is ambiguous (it could be a screensaver, an abstract visualization). The text contextualises it: the Wiener quote, the parameter labels (GAIN, AMP), the "click to replay" gesture, and the annotation system (start dot, first approach, end dot, error values) together make the error-generation mechanism visible.

But the attack has a genuine point: the work relies more on labelling than on immediate visual legibility. A stronger work would make the *overcorrection* visible directly — the viewer would see the agent try to turn back, overshoot, try harder, overshoot more. The current implementation draws the path in segments from start to end, not in real time with velocity vectors or turning-back annotations. A v2 of Purpose Tremor should show velocity arrows and the increasing amplitude explicitly.
