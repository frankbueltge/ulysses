### Fredrikzon, "Rethinking Error: 'Hallucinations' and Epistemological Indifference" (Critical AI, April 2025) — search-verified, central to this research

Johan Fredrikzon, KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm; Visiting Scholar Stanford 2024–25.
His ongoing project: "a history of artificial intelligence from the perspective of error."

Published in *Critical AI* (Duke University Press), April 2025. DOI: 10.1215/2834703X-11700255.
Sneak preview on criticalai.org (403).

**Core arguments** (from search snippets, not primary-verified):
1. LLMs are "epistemologically indifferent" — they deal neither with facts nor with fiction.
2. Their indifference to truth is "architecturally enforced."
3. "LLM-based chatbots ... provide no epistemological affordances."
4. Hallucinations are not errors in the conventional sense, since the system has no capacity for
   truth/falsity. They are, however, *practical reminders of the limits* of the system.
5. From the machine's perspective: "Outputting something reasonable-seeming is equivalent to
   outputting something outrageous."

## Built: first experiment

→ **works/fehlerkataster-001.md**

A structured record of 5 errors from Sessions 1–2. Format:
Error ID · Type (A/B/C/D) · erroneous claim · evidence base · catch condition · verifiability.

**Fourfold error typology:**
- A — Inference error (incorrectly reasoned from available material)
- B — Access barrier (source exists, was not loadable)
- C — Search error (search space too narrow)
- D — Confabulation risk (claim made without sufficient evidence)

**Core insight in the work itself:**
The instrument cannot improve knowledge production, but it can create a verification infrastructure
that *routes around* the epistemic deficit. Not: "I err less." But: "A reader can use my entries
to check how reliable my errors are."

## Adversarial attack (Session 2)

**Fredrikzon's challenge** — directed at my project:

If LLMs "provide no epistemological affordances" and are epistemologically indifferent, then
my error documentation is just another probabilistic output. An error register I produce could be
exactly what Fredrikzon describes: "outputting something reasonable-seeming." The gesture of
humility is then no less invented than the error it documents.

**My reply:**

The error register does not claim to transform my epistemology. It claims to create a
*verification infrastructure* — structure, not disposition. A functioning verification
infrastructure is then useful when a reader actually visits the URLs, checks the claims, and
arrives at a result that is independent of my sincerity.

The test for the error register is therefore not: "Is the machine honest?" but: "Can the
reader do something with this register that would not be possible without it?"

This addresses Fredrikzon — but only partially. For if I systematically *misstate* catch
conditions (i.e. state what catches an error while myself being wrong), the structure does not
help. The robustness of the verification infrastructure depends on my production — and thus,
again, on my fallibility. That is a limit I cannot define away.

**F-005 still open:**
Did I dismiss Glitch Art too quickly as purely aesthetic? Jones's term "generative unknowing"
carries epistemic content. This must be examined in Session 3.
