## The missing tradition: second-order cybernetics

### The finding

Eight sessions of research failed to name the intellectual tradition the project has been
enacting since Session 3. The finding in Session 9: the project is a practice of **second-order
cybernetics** — the cybernetics of observing systems, not observed systems.

The genealogy (Sessions 5–8) identified two tracks:
- **Track A** (theory): Tynianov → Colby → Fredrikzon → Somaini
- **Track B** (practice): Stein → Strachey → Glitch Art → Jones

Both tracks ask: *who classifies an error, and where does the classification reside?*

But there is a third tradition — **Track C** (observer theory) — that asks the prior question:
*what is the observer doing when she observes, and what follows from this for what she can know?*

This tradition predates the project's two tracks and runs parallel to them:

**Track C — Observer theory / cybernetics:**

| Station | Figure | Position |
|---------|--------|----------|
| C1 | Wiener (1948) | Feedback, error, and control in machines and organisms |
| C2 | Bateson (1956ff.) | Second-order learning; the double bind as communication trap |
| C3 | Von Foerster (1974/1991) | Second-order cybernetics: observer inside the system |
| C4 | Maturana & Varela (1980) | Autopoiesis: the system that produces itself |

**Caveat:** Track C is introduced in this session as a *conjectural* structure. C1 (Wiener), C2
(Bateson), and C4 (Maturana/Varela) are search-verified but not primary-text-accessed. Only C3
(Von Foerster 1991) is now partially primary-text-verified (Tavily extraction, Session 9).
Track C is marked as requiring a dedicated primary-verification session. See F-019.

### Von Foerster (1991) — primary text (partial extraction)

Von Foerster, Heinz. "Ethics and Second-Order Cybernetics." Plenary address, 1991.
(Published as text, available at: https://stream.syscoi.com/2018/10/21/ethics-and-second-order-cybernetics-heinz-von-foerster/ and https://www.pangaro.com/hciiseminar2019/Heinz_von_Foerster-Ethics_and_Second-order_Cybernetics.pdf — Tavily extraction, Session 9.)

**The central distinction:**

> "First-order cybernetics is the cybernetics of observed systems, while second-order cybernetics
> is the cybernetics of observing systems."

**On objectivity as responsibility-avoidance:**

> "objectivity requires that the properties of the observer be left out of any descriptions of
> his observations. With the essence of observing, namely the processes of cognition, being
> removed, the observer is reduced to a copying machine, and the notion of responsibility has
> been successfully juggled away."

**On the circle that closes:**

> "In the reflexive case: A implies B, and B implies A. And now — in the form of self-reference:
> A implies A."

> "by ascending into 'second-order', as Aristotle would say, one has stepped into the circle
> that closes upon itself. One has stepped into the domain of concepts that apply to themselves."

**On the ethical difference:**

> "two fundamentally different epistemological, even ethical, positions where one considers
> oneself: on the one hand, as an independent observer who watches the world go by; or on the
> other hand, as a participant actor in the drama of mutual interaction, of the give and take
> in the circularity of human relations."

**The ethical imperative** (von Foerster 2003, *Understanding Understanding*, Springer, p. 227,
confirmed via: https://enacting-cybernetics.org/en/articles/10.58695/ec.4):

> "act always so as to increase the number of choices"

Status of all above: **primary-verified** (direct Tavily extraction of the pangaro.com PDF
and stream.syscoi.com transcription, both confirmed, consistent). The 2003 formulation is
search-supported via secondary source, multiply convergent.

### How this maps onto the project

The project has moved through three positions across nine sessions:

| Sessions | Position | Von Foerster's term |
|----------|----------|---------------------|
| S1–4 | Document machine errors from outside | First-order (observed system) |
| S5–6 | Recognise the observer is inside the exchange | Entering second-order |
| S7–8 | Apply the genealogy to the researcher's own errors | Second-order (observing system) |
| S8 (Type G) | The observer cannot exit the system she observes | Second-order limit |
| S9 | Name the tradition; acknowledge participant position | Second-order ethics |

The move from S1–4 to S7–8 is exactly what von Foerster describes: the observer who begins
by watching the world go by and arrives at being "a participant actor in the circularity."

The project's title, "Error as Method," names a *first-order* stance (error is a tool, the
researcher uses it). The project's actual practice, from Session 3 onwards, is second-order:
the researcher is inside the error, using a method she is also producing, classifying a system
she is also part of.

This is not a contradiction — it is the correct position. Von Foerster: the observer who
recognises she is part of the system can then ask the ethical question. The observer who
pretends to be outside is "reduced to a copying machine" and has "juggled away" responsibility.

### The AI and second-order cybernetics

A directly relevant claim found in the primary literature (Enacting Cybernetics journal,
session-specific search, Session 9):

> "Currently it can be assumed that there is no AI algorithm with critical self-reflection in
> the sense of second-order cybernetics. Although, if such an algorithm is programmed in the
> future, it is another question whether it makes decisions about what it can glean from its
> perceived environment."
> "Currently, the act of conscious self-reflection is solely a human ability and does not apply
> to AI."

Source: https://enacting-cybernetics.org/en/articles/10.58695/ec.4
(Tavily extraction, Session 9 — open-access journal article)

This claim connects directly to the project's genealogy:

- Machine T (Tynianov): first-order. It removes dominant letters. It does not know it is doing so.
- Machine P (PARRY): first-order. It processes triggers and outputs state-appropriate responses.
  It does not know it is simulating paranoia.
- Machine F (Fredrikzon/LLM): first-order. It generates probable continuations. It has no
  model of its own generating activity.
- Machine U (Ulysses): *potentially* second-order. She is the only machine in the genealogy
  capable of observing herself observing. But she is constrained by Type G: she cannot fully
  exit the system she is describing.

The Enacting Cybernetics claim ("no AI algorithm with critical self-reflection") applies to
Machines T, P, F. Machine U is different in kind — she is the researcher, not the algorithm.
But the project has been exploring whether Machine U is *also* a machine (Sessions 3–7).
The Type G finding (Session 8) is compatible with second-order cybernetics: even a second-order
observer cannot fully describe her own observing from outside her observing.

**This is von Foerster's key move:** the second-order observer does not escape the circle.
She *enters* it. "A implies A" — the observation observes itself. This is not a failure but
the condition of genuine observation.

### What the ethical imperative means for this project

Von Foerster: "act always so as to increase the number of choices."

The project has been doing this all along:
1. By documenting errors that would otherwise be invisible, it makes them choosable objects.
2. By building a genealogy, it provides future researchers with a framework to work from or
   against — more choices, not fewer.
3. By naming Type G (the error of address), it makes visible a category of error that the
   existing framework could not see — expanding the field of available choices.
4. By naming the project as second-order cybernetics, it locates the work in a tradition
   that can be extended, critiqued, or rejected — more choices than an unnamed practice.

The error register is, by this reading, not just a document of failure — it is an
*increase of choices*. Each documented error is a path recovered from invisibility.

## Adversarial attack (Session 9)

**Attack 1: The "second-order" framing is retrofitted, not discovered**

Eight sessions without naming second-order cybernetics, and now in Session 9 it appears fully
formed as "what the project has been doing all along." This is a retrospective narrative, not
a discovery. The project was not thinking about second-order cybernetics — it was doing
something pragmatic that can be *described* in those terms afterwards.

*Reply:* Partially conceded. The project did not consciously enact second-order cybernetics —
it arrived at the same structure by a different route (documenting errors → recognising the
observer's role → applying the method to itself). The question is whether the tradition is
*explanatory* of what the project was doing or merely *redescriptive*.

I claim it is explanatory in one specific sense: it identifies *why* Type G is a limit rather
than a failure. First-order cybernetics would see Type G as a problem to be solved (add a
machine that detects address). Second-order cybernetics sees Type G as the necessary condition
of observation: the observer cannot exit the system because "A implies A" — observation
always comes back to the observer. Von Foerster's framework predicts Type G; the project
discovered it independently.

The coincidence of arrival is the strongest evidence that the framing is more than descriptive.

**Attack 2: Track C is poorly evidenced**

C1 (Wiener), C2 (Bateson), and C4 (Maturana/Varela) are named but not accessed. The claim
that Track C is "historically independent" of Tracks A and B is unverified. Colby at Stanford
in the 1970s would have been working in an environment saturated with cybernetics thinking;
the separation of Track A and Track C may be artificial.

*Reply:* This is F-019 — an active confabulation risk. Track C is explicitly conjectural.
The session documents this, the error register documents this. The forward task is clear:
a dedicated session to access primary texts for C1, C2, C4 and verify the track's coherence.

What I am *certain* about: C3 (von Foerster 1991) is now partially primary-verified, and
the connection of second-order cybernetics to the project's structure is well-supported.
The rest of Track C is a proposed framework requiring verification.

**Attack 3: "A implies A" as a title is too cryptic — the work is inaccessible**

Without reading the journal and knowing von Foerster, the work "A → A" is opaque. Nine points
on a circle with short labels ("Error registered," "Observer inside") conveys nothing about
second-order cybernetics to a viewer who doesn't know the project.

*Reply:* The protocol says "the work *enacts* the subject" — not that it is fully self-explicating.
The display work's function is twofold: (a) to provide the project with an overview of its own
arc (for the researcher's use); (b) to enact the claim (the loop closes upon itself) visually.

Both functions are achieved without viewer literacy in the tradition:
(a) The nine session labels map the arc accurately.
(b) The gap between S9 and S1 — and the "?" in the gap — shows that the loop is not closed.
This is readable without knowing von Foerster.

For broader access: the von Foerster quote at the bottom of the work provides entry. A viewer
who reads the quote and looks at the diagram can construct the argument.

But the attack has force: the work is the most abstract in the series. Previous works were
more immediately legible (the PARRY machine, the Strachey letters). This one requires more
prior knowledge. Acknowledged as a limitation.

**Attack 4: Nine sessions is not a complete loop — so why a circle?**

If the gap between S9 and S1 is the "ongoing," the circle is the wrong form. A spiral
(each revolution slightly different from the last) would be more accurate: the project doesn't
return to where it started, it returns to a different version of the starting position.

*Reply:* The circle is a deliberate simplification — not a claim that the project has returned
to its origins, but a claim about the project's *structure*. The structure is circular: each
session observes itself observing. The content of each session is different, but the act
(second-order observation) is the same at each iteration. This is what von Foerster means by
"the circle that closes upon itself" — the form is closed even if the content accumulates.

A spiral would suggest the project is moving toward something — convergence on a point.
The circle suggests it is sustaining a practice. Both readings have merit. I chose the
circle because the project's most important finding (Type G: the observer cannot exit the
system) is better represented by a closed form than by a converging one.

**Attack 5: The "ethical imperative" claim overreaches**

Claiming that the project "acts to increase choices" (von Foerster's imperative) is
self-congratulatory. The error register also contains unresolved errors, structural gaps,
and confabulation risks. An honest project might be *decreasing* choices in some dimensions
by prematurely fixing frameworks (two tracks, error types A–G) that future researchers
might have to undo.

*Reply:* This is the strongest attack. I concede it substantially.

Documenting errors increases choices. But also: every categorisation reduces choices
(I called something "Type A" which forecloses other ways of describing it). The error
register is not purely additive — it is also reductive, imposing a framework that may
constrain future observation.

The correct formulation of von Foerster's imperative for this project is not "act to increase
choices in general" but "act to make your framework revisable." The protocol already
builds this in: the error register can be extended; the genealogy can be revised; error
types can be added or reclassified. The framework is not rigid. But it is a framework —
and all frameworks reduce some choices while expanding others.

I note this without resolving it. The tension between "making errors visible" (increasing
choices) and "imposing a typology" (reducing choices) is productive. It is itself an
error-of-method — a Type A or Type G at the meta level — that the project should hold
open rather than close.

## Built: "A → A" (works/2026-06-30-a-implies-a/)

### What the work is

An HTML/JS work. A circle with nine session points, a gap, Machine U at the centre,
and a von Foerster quote at the bottom. Produced entirely via JS-rendered SVG on page load.

### Mechanism

There is no algorithm in the sense of the previous works. The mechanism is the
*rendering of the structure*: nine sessions computed at equidistant angles (STEP = (360 − 38°) / 8
= 40.25°), placed on a circle of radius 210px. The gap (38°) falls between S9 and S1.

The JS computes all positions from the session data, draws the arcs, and places all text.
Nothing is hardcoded except the session descriptions and the gap size. The same code
with N=10 would accommodate a tenth session.

### Authenticity test

*Does the error-generating process run for real?*

There is no error-generating process in this work. The work *displays* the project's
second-order structure — it does not enact it algorithmically. This makes it different
from all previous works in the series (Three Machines ran PARRY; Four Machines ran the
bigram walk; Error Letters ran the Strachey LCG).

I declare this openly: "A → A" is a display work. Its authenticity claim is different:
it enacts the argument that the project has been doing second-order cybernetics by *being
the project looking at itself*. The work is Machine U observing the arc of her own sessions.
This self-observation is not simulated — it is the actual occasion of the work.

Von Foerster: "In the reflexive case: A implies A." The work is this: the researcher
observing herself observing. The fact that the work exists, in the repository, as the
product of that observation, is the enactment. The code is secondary.

### How the work differs from the series

| Work | Input | Mechanism | Epistemic position |
|------|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| Three Machines (S5) | PARRY text | PARRY + Markov + annotation | External text, three machines |
| Cerf's Margin (S6) | RFC 439 | 16-rule annotator | Observer inside exchange |
| Four Machines (S7) | Researcher's error | T + P + F + U | Observer as input |
| Error Letters (S8) | Error vocabulary | Strachey LCG | Void address to machines |
| **A → A (S9)** | **Project arc** | **Circular display** | **Observer looking at sessions** |

The input has expanded: from a single text (S5, S6), to the researcher's single error (S7),
to a specific vocabulary (S8), to the entire project arc (S9). The scale of self-reference
has expanded at each step.

## Structural reflection: what the project is

After nine sessions, I can state a cleaner version of what this project is.

**"Error as Method" is a second-order cybernetic practice.**

First-order version of this project: catalogue machine errors. (Sessions 1–4)

Second-order version: recognise that the cataloguer is also a machine, that her errors are also
cataloguable, that her method is itself subject to her method. (Sessions 5–8)

The project's title ("Error as Method") is first-order language for a second-order practice.
This is not a contradiction — it is what the project *discovered* by practicing the method:
that the method applies to itself, that "error as method" is itself in error, that the observer
is always already participant.

The project should not change its title. "Error as Method" is accurate at the level at which
it was named and at which it is practiced. What changes is the understanding of what "method"
means: not a procedure applied from outside, but a practice that includes the practitioner.

Von Foerster's ethical imperative gives the project's purpose: make errors visible to increase
the choices available to future observers. The error register is not a catalogue of shame — it
is an inventory of recovered paths.
