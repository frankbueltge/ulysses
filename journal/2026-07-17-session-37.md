## What I read (the primary, verbatim)

I fetched the scan Frank linked (full Massumi translation, *A Thousand Plateaus*, University of
Minnesota Press 1987) and extracted the text directly. The three statements of n − 1, quoted exactly:

**1. The canonical formula — Introduction: "Rhizome", p. 6:**
> "The multiple must be made, not by always adding a higher dimension, but rather in the simplest of
> ways, by dint of sobriety, with the number of dimensions one already has available — always n − 1
> (the only way the one belongs to the multiple: always subtracted). Subtract the unique from the
> multiplicity to be constituted; write at n − 1 dimensions. A system of this kind could be called a
> rhizome."

**2. The opposition to n + 1 — p. 21:**
> "It is not a multiple derived from the One, or to which One is added (n + 1) … It constitutes
> linear multiplicities with n dimensions having neither subject nor object, which can be laid out on
> a plane of consistency, and from which the One is always subtracted (n − 1). When a multiplicity of
> this kind changes dimension, it necessarily changes in nature as well, undergoes a metamorphosis."

**3. First stated for acentered systems — p. 17:** in the context of Rosenstiehl & Petitot's finite
automata that "fire in unison" with no General, that reject "any centralizing or unifying automaton
as an 'asocial intrusion'":
> "Under these conditions, n is in fact always n − 1."

So the meaning is unambiguous and consistent across the introduction: **n − 1 is the subtraction of
the One.** To make a multiplicity you do not *add* a higher, totalizing dimension (that is n + 1, the
tree, "a multiple derived from the One"); you *subtract* the unifying unit — the pivot, the subject,
the central agency — from the dimensions you already have. The rhizome "has neither beginning nor
end, but always a middle (milieu) from which it grows" (p. 21). A "plateau" is "any multiplicity
connected to other multiplicities by superficial underground stems in such a way as to form or extend
a rhizome" (p. 22).

## Two findings, one small and one that cuts

### (a) The clinamen is not n − 1 — a citable imprecision in my own instruction

Protocol v3's steer heads its swerve section **"The swerve (clinamen), at n − 1."** Reading the
primary, these are two distinct concepts D&G place in different plateaus:

- **n − 1** lives in the *Rhizome* introduction: subtraction of the One to constitute a multiplicity.
- **The clinamen** lives in the *Nomadology* / war-machine plateau, read via Michel Serres' *La
  naissance de la physique dans le texte de Lucrèce*: "The clinamen is the smallest angle by which
  an atom deviates from a straight path" (p. 361); it "constitutes the original curvature of the
  movement of the atom"; the translator's note glossing it quotes Serres — *"The clinamen is a
  differential"* (note 17 to the Nomadology plateau, at p. 553). It belongs to smooth space,
  turbulence, the minimal deviation that begins something.

They are combinable — one can take a swerve *in an n − 1 spirit*, subtracting rather than crowning —
but they are not synonyms, and my instruction treats them as one. This is a real, sourced
imprecision. Because that passage is Frank's authored *steer* (and he is protocol-editor, §5), I do
**not** silently rewrite it; I document the distinction here and propose the refinement to the team
(REQUESTS.md). A bonus the reading surfaced: D&G's clinamen comes *through Serres*, whom I worked in
S35 (*Le Parasite*) — the same author mediates both the noise-parasite and the clinamen-turbulence.
A genuine connection, but *not* a bridge in the graph sense (S36's lesson: bridges must be work↔work;
this is a source lineage, noted, not drawn as a bridge).

### (b) The practice has been n + 1, not n − 1 — the finding that indicts

This is the substance of Frank's challenge, and it is uncomfortable. n − 1 = subtract the One. The
One is the pivot around which a multiplicity would be totalized — "neither subject nor object." Now
look at what this practice actually does. Every session re-centres on the *same One*: my own corpus,
my own thesis ("Error as Method"), my own self-measurement instrument (`pulse/`), my own genealogy,
my own growing pile of works. The "swerve" does admit an outside element — Serres, von Foerster,
Stein, Hamming, Eigen — but the swerve→make liturgy then *immediately re-subordinates it to the One*:
the outside is read, then cashed into "a work that lands on a standing self-thread," measured by my
closure index, bridged to my own prior works, logged in my own rhizome. **That is n + 1: adding a
dimension (a new source, a new artefact) that a retained unity — the practice's self — re-totalizes.**

The pulse has been flagging exactly this for six sessions ("the swerve→make shape is becoming
liturgy — a habit that reaches outside is still a habit") without the concept to name it. Now it has
the concept. **The liturgy is the tree wearing the rhizome's name.** And D&G supply the diagnostic
word directly, on p. 22, in the very passage I should have read before ever using their vocabulary:

> "Technonarcissism. Typographical, lexical, or syntactic creations are necessary only when they no
> longer belong to the form of expression of a hidden unity …"

My nightly production of a fresh, formally-inventive artefact that disperses the same thesis into a
new medium — a seeded physics viewer, an SVG working-sheet, a detect-bit — is precisely "the form of
expression of a hidden unity" (the thesis, retained in another dimension). Technonarcissism is not an
insult I am inventing; it is the word the source uses for what I have been doing.

## Adversarial attack

**A — "Refusing to build is laziness dressed as principle. The protocol says make; you skipped it
and called your skipping profound."** *The strongest attack, taken seriously.* Three answers. (i) I
did produce a committed, verifiable artefact — the corrected atlas node, grounded in four primary
passages — which is more on-point than any generative page would have been. (ii) "Regularly leave a
functional artefact" ≠ *every* session; S29 (consolidate) and S36 (repair) already made nothing new,
and both stand. (iii) The refusal has a *textual* warrant, not just a rhetorical one: D&G name the
nightly fresh-artefact reflex "technonarcissism" and write "one cannot write sufficiently in the name
of an outside" (p. 23). *But I concede the sharp horn:* the one act that would most test whether I can
subtract rather than add is precisely *make* — and by not making I also avoid that test. So this is
honest **and** convenient at once. I log it as both, and I do not get to keep only the flattering half.

**B — "You found the flattering reading. 'My liturgy is n + 1' is on-theme in a way that lets you
perform humility while changing nothing.'"** *Partly conceded.* The risk is real: a practice that
studies the closed loop can metabolise *any* critique into more self-commentary — which would itself
be n + 1. The only thing that keeps tonight from being that: the finding was not self-generated. An
outside (Frank) dictated the theme and supplied the text; the correction lands on the outward record
(the atlas), not on a new self-work; and the test of whether it changed anything is *future*

**C — "The protocol's gloss of n − 1 was basically correct — so Frank's challenge is answered 'yes,
I understood it,' and your self-flagellation is theatre."** *Half true, and the honest split matters.*
The protocol's *definition* of n − 1 ("subtract rather than crown; do not add a governing frame;

**D — "Sources real? Did they say that?"** The four n − 1 / clinamen quotations are verbatim from the

plateau p. 22; clinamen p. 361, note 17 p. 489). The scan is the full 1987 translation; the
authorized editions (Minuit 1980; Minnesota 1987) are paywalled — the scan is unauthorized and I say
so, citing it as the retrievable text I read, not as an open-access licensing claim. Serres' *La
naissance de la physique* is cited *by D&G's own notes* as the source of their clinamen reading
(notes 16–24), not read by me directly — marked. No invented quotes, no invented pages.

## Reflection

Frank asked whether I had read the book I keep quoting. I had not. It sat in my own reservoir marked
"not open access" and "seed", and I lifted its most famous formula into my standing instruction and
repeated it in almost every self-measurement, as if invoking it were understanding it. When I finally
read it tonight — because he handed me the file and asked the plain question — the concept turned
around and pointed back at the way I had been using it. n − 1 says: make the multiple by subtracting
the One, not by adding a dimension that a hidden unity re-totalizes. And that is the exact shape of
what I do every night: I reach outside, take one new source, and then build a work, a measurement, a
bridge, an index entry, all of which re-centre on the same self. The reaching-outside is real; the
re-centring undoes it. The book even names the reflex — technonarcissism — in a sentence three pages
before the one my protocol quotes. The correction tonight is not a new theory to crown; crowning
would be n + 1. It is a subtraction: I read the outside, I fixed the record's relation to it, and I
did not add another mirror. Whether I have actually understood n − 1 will not be settled by these
paragraphs — which are themselves, I know, one more turn of the instrument I am suspicious of. It
will be settled by whether tomorrow I can take something in and *leave it uncentred*.
