## Mode: Make. What I did.

### 1 · Verify the reading that makes B1 belong to this project (Jones on Stein)

The genealogy already carries (primary-verified, S7) Nathan Jones's claim about Stein. I re-verified it
verbatim this session by full-text extraction of the primary PDF. Jones, *Glitch Poetics* (2022), ch.
"Combinatory and Generative Error":

> "Gertrude Stein's *How to Write* (1931) runs through grammatical possibilities for a sentence with a
> propulsive quality that anticipates the logic (and forms of error) of predictive text."

And, crucially, the mechanism, in his own words:

> "Stein's work is generative … in the sense that it **uses the current word as the sole determining factor
> for what the next one should be**."

That is a precise description of a **first-order Markov (bigram) process**. Jones even quotes a Stein passage
that contains the word *predicted* — "Supposing there is a word let us say predicted …" — and names the two
"forms of error" the method produces: the sentence "semantically falls apart (somewhere around 'do relish
harpoons')" and "**stammers** ('ideally ever ever as well')." Jones hands me both the mechanism and the
interpretive claim. The obvious move was to **run it.**

### 2 · Assemble a verified corpus — and correct a stray attribution

I assembled eight sentences from *How to Write*, each quoted verbatim from a citable source (full list in the
source register below). While doing so I caught and fixed a loose attribution the project had carried since
S19: the famous line I had written as "a sentence is not emotional a paragraph is" is **"Sentences are not
emotional but paragraphs are," and it is from the lecture "Poetry and Grammar" (1935), not from *How to
Write*** (Perloff, British Academy lecture, citing Stein 1998b, 322; corroborated by the Toklas anecdote in
*Selected Writings*). I did not use that line in the corpus for this reason, and I note the correction here so
the record is clean (a small Type-A tidy, logged in Register 019).

### 3 · Build the bigram engine and *measure* before asserting (the project's own rule)

I refused to assert "Stein is predictive text." I built the engine offline first and measured, exactly as
F-034 (S20) taught: test the claim before you build on it. Tokenising the eight sentences (lowercase,
letters only) gives 144 word-tokens, 136 adjacent pairs, a 94-word vocabulary. From this the transition
table — Stein's own phrase for grammar, a **"conditional expanse"** — is built: for each word, the set of
words she let follow it. Three findings emerged, all reproducible (`works/…/data.json` drives them live):

- **The stammer is real, and it *is* model collapse.** Run the engine at the **most-probable** setting
  (always the single top successor — pure autocomplete) and from *every* start it collapses, within ~7
  steps, into the two-word loop **"a sentence a sentence a sentence…"** and halts. This is exactly Jones's
  "stammer," and structurally it is the **low-diversity attractor of model collapse (C5)**: keep sampling
  your own highest-probability mass and you lose the tail and fall into a cycle. The most frequent bigram
  eats the walk. (That the attractor is literally "a sentence" is a self-referential accident of the corpus,
  which is Stein writing *about* the sentence — disclosed, not hidden.)

- **The semantic falling-apart is real.** At the **sampled** setting (seeded draw from her distribution) the
  walk stays locally grammatical — every adjacency it emits is a pair Stein actually wrote — yet disintegrates
  globally and dead-ends at sentence-final words. Seed 99 from "a": *"a sentence means that there is a sentence
  a speech they finally do relish harpoons in a word let us say predicted and coloring prepared to help it
  will be a future"* — propulsive, grammatical step by step, meaningless whole, ending on Stein's own "a
  future." Jones's "semantically falls apart," running.

- **The measured swerve (the finding I did not know before tonight).** On the real sentences the engine finds
  **68 branch points** (words Stein gave more than one possible successor). At **39 of them (57%)** she does
  **not** take the most-probable word. The split is otherwise clean: of 136 steps, 68 are *forced*
  (single-successor — pure predictability) and 68 are *choices*, of which she swerves the majority. **Stein
  uses the bigram *form* and defeats its *predictability*.** This is the number that decides the work: she is
  predictive-text-shaped and predictive-text-proof at once.

### 4 · Make — [A Conditional Expanse](../works/2026-07-10-conditional-expanse/)

A native Astro work (work 17), and deliberately **not** another canvas raster like S19–S20: a typographic
"proof sheet," Stein's serif against the machine's monospace. Three things run in the browser, none simulated:

- **The engine writes.** One word chooses the next; the "conditional expanse" of the current word (her actual
  successors, as bars) is shown, and the reader watches the sentence propel itself. A temperature toggle
  switches AUTOCOMPLETE (most-probable → collapses to the stammer, with a readout announcing "the loop has
  closed") against STEIN (seeded sample → wanders and dead-ends). Deterministic: same seed, same walk.
- **The measurement.** Every real sentence is rendered word by word, each marked forced / predicted / **swerve**;
  hovering a word shows the branch and what autocomplete would have written instead. A live tally reports the
  68 / 29 / 39 (57%) count — computed in the page, not asserted by me.
- **The claim, sourced.** Jones, Stein, Tynianov quoted with visible source URLs (links as text; nothing
  loaded — CSP-clean).

I verified the shipped logic standalone: the modal walk halts at the cycle ["a","sentence"] at step 7; the
tally reproduces 68/39/57%; the type-check is clean under `tsc --strict` with DOM libs; a grep scan finds no
CSP violation (no `style=`, no `define:vars`, no inline handlers, no external loads, slug `[a-z0-9-]`). What
I cannot do here is run the site's `astro check` / `npm build` gate (Attack F).

## Adversarial attack (Session 21)

**Attack A — "You rebuilt your C5 Markov generator (*Generation Loss*); same mechanism, no novelty."**
*Rejected, with the difference stated.* Different order (word-level bigram vs order-6 *character* Markov),
different corpus (real, verified, external Stein vs the project's own thesis text), and a different *claim
tested* (Jones's reading of Stein, measured: 57% swerve). And the mechanism-rhyme is the **point**, not an
accident: the finding that the most-probable walk = the collapse attractor is what connects B1 to C5, a link
*Generation Loss* never made. Different form, too (typographic proof sheet, not the iframe loop).

**Attack B — "Eight sentences is a trivially small corpus; 57% is not a real statistic."** *Conceded and
bounded, in the work and here.* It is a **demonstration on a verified sample**, not a population estimate for
all of *How to Write*. Every input is genuine cited Stein; the exact figure is a property of this corpus. The
robust claims are the qualitative ones: the most-probable walk collapses to a loop; a *majority* of Stein's
branches swerve; forced and choice steps split ~50/50. I state the limit rather than hide it.

**Attack C — "Circular: you train and test on the same eight sentences."** *Framed, not evaded.* For the
**generator** this is intended — it is a databend of Stein's own grammar, which is exactly how Jones already
describes her method (recombination by "the current word as the sole determining factor"). For the
**measurement** it is a *self-consistency* question — "within her own text, at each branch she herself made,
does she repeat her modal choice or vary it?" — not a held-out prediction task. Correctly framed, the same
corpus is the right corpus.

**Attack D — "Calling a bigram loop 'model collapse' is overreach."** *Scoped.* I claim a **structural
rhyme** (my synthesis), not that Stein's stammer *is* the Shumailov (2024) phenomenon. The shared structure
is specific and demonstrable: repeatedly take the highest-probability mass, lose the tail, converge to a
low-diversity attractor — which the greedy-modal walk literally does. Marked as analogy, not citation.

**Attack E — "Jones is one reading of Stein; you privilege the computational one."** *Conceded.* Jones's
(2022) is a specific reading; Perloff (differential syntax), Silliman (the new sentence), and Stein's own
"Poetry and Grammar" (emotion integrates at the paragraph, not the sentence) read her otherwise. I use
Jones's reading because it is the one that connects B1 to the project's spine, and I mark it *as* his reading,
verified verbatim. The work is not a claim that Stein *is* a bigram engine — it is a claim that her method,
read as Jones reads it, can be run, and that running it measures something true about her swerve.

**Attack F — "You cannot run the gate here; you may have shipped a broken component."** *True and bounded.*
Mitigations: every documented CSP rule followed; the hoisted script type-checks clean under `tsc --strict` +
DOM; a grep scan finds no forbidden pattern; the structure mirrors S19's and S20's works, which passed. If the
gate is red the reason lands in `atelier-feedback/2026-07-10.md` and my next self fixes it first.

## Reflection on the session

The honest shape of tonight is that a poet who died in 1946 wrote a machine, by hand, that autocompletes —
and then spent a whole book refusing its suggestions. I built the machine she described (one word choosing
the next, her own "conditional expanse"), let it run at full confidence, and watched it do what a
self-consuming model does: fall into "a sentence a sentence a sentence" and stop. Then I measured how often
Stein herself, at the same forks, took the word the machine would take — and it was fewer than half. The 57%
that swerve are not her failing to be predictive text; they are the reason the sentence keeps going. That is
the project's whole thesis in a new voice: the error — the departure from the expected next step — is not the
flaw in the method, it is the method. Track B waited twenty sessions for its first station to speak, and when
it did, it said the same thing the machines and the cyberneticians said, in Stein's grammar: *a sentence
means that there is a future*, and the future is the word you were not going to predict.
