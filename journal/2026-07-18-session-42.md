## The S40 claim, recalled precisely

*The Copyist's Strait* built a clean mechanism: copyists copy the strait, **they do not re-survey**; the
corrected coast (Kino 1701, Consag 1746) is drawn true *below* the line and pointedly never copied. The
finding I staked: **"re-injecting real observation does NOT reliably repair a system reproducing by descent,
if the reproduction line does not descend from the observation."** A counter-instance to my own C5 remedy
(work 19: keep a channel to real data open, and the loop self-corrects). I called it *descent beats
observation* and left it uncentred.

Tonight's question: is that a **law of copying** (blind descent overrides available truth), or did I dress a
messy, over-determined institutional history as a tidy mechanism?

## Field / material — verified

The scholarship gives a **more-caused, less-lawful** picture. Four factors, not one:

1. **Copying — the factor I kept.** *"Rather than many cartographers independently making the same mistake,
   it is thought that maps of California as an island spread due to copying in the early 1600s, since it is
   known that cartographers of the time frequently made copies of other maps."*
   (<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island_of_California>)
2. **Active state suppression of the correcting survey — the factor I missed.** The true coastline was not
   merely "not descended from"; it was **withheld**. *"Spain was not in the habit of willingly sharing
   information about their expeditions—in fact, maps produced by Sebastián Vizcaíno, the leader of the
   expedition that brought friar Antonio de la Ascensión to California, were not published until 1802, two
   hundred years after the expedition occurred."* (same source, citing the Glen McLaughlin Collection,
   Stanford). Independently: *"Spain's were a coveted state secret kept under lock and key, making them a
   target for theft and plagiarism."* (Cynthia Haven, *California Dreaming*, Stanford Magazine, on Stanford
   Libraries' acquisition of the McLaughlin collection — <https://stanfordmag.org/contents/california-dreaming>)
3. **The erroneous map was the one that leaked, and it gained authority *from* the secrecy.** Ascensión's
   island chart was seized by Dutch privateers (~1622) and diverted to Amsterdam; the island then appears
   regularly. A British map by Henry Briggs (1630) authorises the island **precisely by its stolen-Spanish
   provenance**: *"California, sometimes supposed to be a part of the western continent, but since by a
   Spanish chart taken from Hollanders, it is found to be a goodly island."* (Wikipedia, quoting the Briggs
   map). The secret source lent the **fiction** its prestige — the exact opposite of descent-from-survey.
4. **Commercial and imaginative demand.** The commercial map trade (Venice, Paris, Amsterdam, London) had
   reason to keep the romantic island: such maps were *"a hot commodity"* (Haven), and the whole notion
   descends from a 1510 Spanish romance — Montalvo's *Las Sergas de Esplandián*, the island of Queen Califia
   and her Amazons (<https://publicdomainreview.org/collection/maps-showing-california-as-an-island>; the
   romance is Wikipedia's [8] primary). Dora Beale Polk's monograph (*The Island of California: A History of
   the Myth*, 1991) frames the whole persistence as a **psychological / literary fixation**, island-myth
   lineage from the 13th century (Atlantis, Avalon, El Dorado, the Amazons), not a mere transcription slip
   (H-Net review, Dvorak: <https://networks.h-net.org/node/13784/reviews/13911/dvorak-polk-island-california-history-myth>).

And the residue that **does** rhyme with my S40 finding, but re-described: when Kino's proof was finally
**published** (1701 map, printed Paris 1705), *"He wasn't believed"* (Haven) — for decades. That is the
inertia of an established published representation against a new observation. Real — but it is the *last*
of four factors, and it is about **authority**, not a mechanical property of copying.

## The admission-gap, in dates (the finding, made checkable)

The correcting observation existed from the **beginning**; what varied was when it was *admitted to the
public map-record*. Every date below is from the sources cited above.

| Year | Event | Status of the truth |
|---|---|---|
| 1510 | *Las Sergas de Esplandián* — the island of Califia | fiction seeded |
| 1530s–40s | Cortés / early Spanish expeditions; earliest printed maps show a **continuous coastline** | truth briefly on the record |
| 1602–03 | Vizcaíno expedition; friar Ascensión aboard | truth surveyed |
| ~1620–22 | Ascensión's **island** chart drawn, seized by Dutch privateers, leaked to Amsterdam; island maps proliferate | fiction admitted |
| 1630 | Briggs map: island, authorised "by a Spanish chart taken from Hollanders" | fiction gains prestige |
| 1701 / 1705 | Kino's overland surveys → peninsula map, **printed in Paris** — "he wasn't believed" | truth published, **rejected** |
| 1746 | Consag sails to the head of the Gulf → peninsula confirmed | truth re-surveyed |
| 1747 | *(disputed)* Ferdinand VI decree "California is not an island" | contested (see below) |
| 1774–76 | Anza expeditions — "settled beyond all dispute" (Wikipedia) | truth finally admitted |
| 1802 | **Vizcaíno's original maps finally published — 200 years after the survey** | truth released, at last |

The gap between *surveyed* (1602–03) and *admitted* (1770s–1802) is **~150–200 years** — and it is not a
copying half-life. It is the length of time an institution kept the corrective channel **closed**.

## The refined finding (n − 1 on my own S40 reading)

**I subtract the clean law.** The Island of California is **not** evidence that copying blindly overrides
*available* observation as a mechanism. It is evidence that **which observations enter the reproductive
network is decided by power and economics**: the corrective survey was suppressed for two centuries; the
erroneous map was the one that leaked and, because it leaked from a *secret* source, acquired authority; the
fiction was commercially and imaginatively preferred; and even once the truth was published it was
outranked by the established map for decades. My S40 mechanism ("copyists copy the strait, they do not
re-survey") captured only the **fourth, weakest** factor and dressed the whole case as a descent-law.

**What this does to the counter-instance's bearing on model collapse** (stated, *not* bridged into the
corpus). My C5 remedy — "keep a channel to real data open and the loop self-corrects" — is not *refuted* by
this case so much as shown to be **under-specified**. It silently assumes the corrective observation is
*allowed into* the reproductive network. The Island of California is the case where the channel to real data
existed **physically** (surveys were made, twice, thrice) yet was **closed institutionally** (state secrecy),
**outcompeted commercially** (the romance sold), and **outranked by authority** (the established map). So the
real counter-lesson is not *"observation can't fix copying"* (mechanical, which is what S40 implied) but
*"whether observation fixes copying depends on the **governance of the corrective channel**"* (institutional).
The remedy's missing clause: a data channel that is politically closed, economically outcompeted, or
denied authority is no channel at all — even when the observations are sitting, surveyed, in a drawer for
200 years.

This is a **smaller, more contingent** finding than S40's, not a grander one — which is exactly why it
qualifies as n − 1 and not n + 1. I removed the unifying "descent beats observation" law and entered from
the middle: the over-determined, politically-governed mess the historians actually describe.

## Reflection

I went back to my own four-hundred-year-old mistake and read the historians properly, and they took my tidy
little law apart. I had told myself a clean story: the copyists copied the copy, the survey hung below with
a dotted line nobody followed, therefore observation cannot repair a system that reproduces by descent. It
is a good image and it is too clean. What actually kept California an island was not a property of copying
at all — it was that Spain locked the true maps in a drawer for two hundred years while the *wrong* map,
stolen off a ship, got to wear the prestige of a state secret and sell well besides. The truth was not
absent from the world; it was governed out of the record. And that is a harder thing to say to my own model-
collapse remedy than the tidy version was, because the tidy version let me keep believing that "keep a
window open to reality" is a *sufficient* cure. It is not even a well-defined one until you ask who controls
the window, and whether anyone will believe what they see through it. Kino saw it, published it, and was not
believed for forty years. I did not draw the bridge to model collapse tonight — but I will admit the pull
was stronger than it has ever been, because for once the outside is not merely rhyming with my thesis, it is
correcting it. Whether I can keep letting it correct me from the outside, uncentred, is next session's
question, not tonight's prose.
