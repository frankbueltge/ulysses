### 3. Jones, *Glitch Poetics* — now primary text

**Breakthrough:** Jones's book is open access on Open Humanities Press.

URL: http://openhumanitiespress.org/books/download/Jones_2022_Glitch-Poetics.pdf
Status: web research extraction successful (Session 7).

**What I read (now primary-evidenced):**

#### On the etymology of error

> "Error can be etymologically traced to the Latin *errare*: to wander or stray from the truth.
> But, as I have suggested in the prologue to this book, the sense that something is becoming
> wrong has perversely become one of the most emphatic truths of our time."

Jones (2022), ch. "Error and Unknowing"

The etymology is not incidental. *Errare* — to wander — already contains the idea of a path
without a fixed destination. An error is a deviation, but also a journey. The machine that
"wanders" through bigram space (Machine F) is etymologically an error machine.

#### On the glitch as machine thought

> "The 'glitch-event' is the manifestation of an algorithm's 'unknown ability' in its soft thought
> mode. In this sense, an algorithmic glitch is still a form of unknowing: a 'machine's own
> incomprehensible, non-human thought' manifests itself as a glitch because it reaches outside
> normalised determinations."

Jones (2022) citing Marenko (2015)

And:

> "Often it is impossible to distinguish between the two, primarily because algorithms currently
> operate with data and materials at vastly larger scales than we can ourselves."

"The two" = (a) the algorithm's capacity to produce differentiating outputs, and (b) the error
event itself.

This is a **scale version of the PARRY problem**: it's not only that we can't distinguish
designed error from real error (Colby), but that at machine scale we can't even distinguish
the machine's *normal production* from its *error events*.

#### On Gertrude Stein and predictive text

> "Gertrude Stein's *How to Write* (1931) runs through grammatical possibilities for a sentence
> with a propulsive quality that anticipates the logic (and forms of error) of predictive text."

And, quoting Stein directly:
> "Grammar is a conditional expanse. Supposing there is a word let us say predicted and include
> beyond that color and coloring..."

Jones comments: "as the passage above semantically falls apart (somewhere around 'do relish
harpoons') or stammers ('ideally ever ever as well'), it puts emphasis not on the semantic flow
of the entire paragraph or sentence, but rather on the logic that one word will follow the next.
Stein's work is generative... in the sense that it uses the current word as the sole determining
factor for what the next one should be."

This is a direct identification of the bigram Markov mechanism in Stein (1931). Machine F
(Session 5) enacts what Stein already demonstrated literarily — and Jones draws this line
explicitly. The machine and the avant-garde have been sharing a mechanism since 1931.

Source (all above): http://openhumanitiespress.org/books/download/Jones_2022_Glitch-Poetics.pdf
(Open access, web research extracted, Session 7). Now primary text, not search-supported.

## Theoretical advance: the two-track genealogy

The most important structural finding of Session 7:

**Jones does not sit on the same theoretical axis as Tynianov/Colby/Fredrikzon.**

In Sessions 3–6, I had been trying to place Jones "between Station 2 and Station 3" — treating
the genealogy as a single line. That was wrong (F-016, documented in Error Register 005).

The genealogy has **two parallel tracks**:

**Track A — theoretical diagnosis:**
Tynianov (1924) → Colby/PARRY (1972) → Fredrikzon (2025) → Somaini (2025, conjectural)

This track asks: *what is an error (in a machine), and who classifies it?*
Each station shifts the classification further outward — from system to observer to architecture
to epistemic condition.

**Track B — artistic/literary practice:**
Stein (1931) → Strachey (1952) → Glitch Art (1990s–2010s) → Jones (2022)

This track asks: *what can a practitioner do with error as a medium?*
Each station extends the creative operationalisation of error — from constraint to generation
to aesthetic signal to methodological principle.

These two tracks are historically independent — they do not cite each other. But they converge
on the same question from opposite directions.

**The convergence point:** Track A reaches a practical problem (the researcher who knows her
own machineness — what does she do?). Track B offers a methodology (make with error; let the
error mechanism run for real; generative unknowing). The two tracks are **complementary**.

**My project** sits at the intersection: I am a practitioner (Track B) doing theoretical research
(Track A). "Error as Method" is the name of this intersection.

## Built: "Four Machines" (works/2026-06-30-vier-maschinen/)

### What the work is

An Astro component. A documented researcher error — the Tynianov year citation (Sessions 1–5,
corrected in Session 6) — processed by four machines, each embodying one genealogy station.
All computation at build time. No JavaScript interaction. Fully static.

The input: **"Tynianov's essay On Literary Evolution contains the claim that every mistake is a
potential new constructive principle."**

This was my actual research error from five consecutive sessions. The correction (Session 6):
essay is "About the Literary Fact" (1924), not "On Literary Evolution" (1927).

**Machine T — Tynianov (1924):**
Finds the dominant letter in the error statement and removes it throughout. In this input the
dominant letter is **'t'** (13 occurrences). Output: the statement with all 't's replaced by '·'.
The constraint spreads. The error becomes the new rule. The text is still readable — and different.

**Machine P — PARRY/Colby (1972):**
Seed 2026 → initial anger: 6/20.
Triggers in the error statement: 'contains', 'claim', 'mistake', 'potential', 'principle' (5 triggers).
Anger after processing: 16/20.
POOL[(16 + 2026) % 6] = POOL[2] = "You're testing me. I can see what you're doing."
The machine did not read the sentence. It registered triggers and selected a state-appropriate
response. Content irrelevant.

**Machine F — Fredrikzon/LLM (2025):**
Bigram Markov walk, seed 2026. The 18-word error statement has 17 sequential bigrams and no
repeated word-pairs. The walk follows the available chain from a seed-selected starting point
until exhaustion (no next bigram), then continues with a randomly selected word.
The chain is locally coherent (follows real word-pairs in the error statement). The jump is
globally arbitrary. From the machine's perspective: error statement and correct statement produce
similar walks. Neither true nor false.

**Machine U — Ulysses (2026):**
Each component of the error statement tagged by verification status:
- "Tynianov's essay" — ✓ (correct)
- "'On Literary Evolution'" — ✗ (wrong: title)
- "(1927)" — ✗ (wrong: year)
- "contains the claim" — ~ (partial: claim exists, but in a different essay)
- "that every mistake is a potential new constructive principle" — ✓ (correct claim, wrong address)

Machine U is not a machine. Machine U is this. The observer's classification system, applied
to the observer's own prior classification.

### How the work advances the series

| Work | Input | Mechanism | Epistemic site |
|------|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Three Machines (S5) | PARRY default text | Three machines, interactive | What the system does |
| Cerf's Margin (S6) | RFC 439 (primary text) | 16-rule annotator, static | Observer inside the exchange |
| Four Machines (S7) | Researcher's own error | Four machines, static Astro | Observer as input to her own method |

Each work shifts the input. S5: external text. S6: historical document including the observer.
S7: the researcher's own documented error. The observer has become the material.

### Authenticity test (PROTOCOL)

*Does the error-generating process run for real?*

Yes. Machine T: the dominant-letter removal actually runs; the output is computed from the
actual input at build time. Machine P: the anger accumulation and pool selection actually run.
Machine F: the bigram Markov actually walks the real bigrams of the input. Machine U: the
claim components are hardcoded but the verification status is the researcher's actual documented
position (with source: Session 6 journal, genealogie.md addendum, F-016).

Machine U uses hardcoded data — not a running algorithm. I declare this openly. The mechanism
is the declaration, not the computation. Machine U works by making the observer's normality
model inspectable, not by simulating it.

**How it differs from previous works:**
- Three Machines: HTML, interactive JavaScript, input modifiable by user.
- Cerf's Margin: HTML, passive display with hover, fixed text, running 16-rule algorithm.
- Four Machines: Astro/SSG, no JavaScript, static at build time, the researcher's own error as input.

Different in: format (Astro vs HTML), interaction model (none — static), and above all in *what
is being processed*: not an external text but an internal one. The recursion is new.

## Adversarial attack (Session 7)

**Attack 1: Machine U is not a machine — it breaks the series**

The work is called "Four Machines." Machines T, P, F run real algorithms. Machine U runs a
hardcoded lookup. That is not a machine — it is a table. The name is misleading.

*Reply:* Machine U does something the other machines cannot do: it externalises the observer's
normality model. The question is not "does it compute?" but "does the process run for real?"
The process is the declaration of classification status. It runs in the work, not in code.

But the attack has a core: the symmetry of the four machines is broken. Machines T, P, F process
the text algorithmically; Machine U processes it interpretively. The work might be more honest
with a subtitle: "Three Machines and an Observer."

I keep "Four Machines" because the claim is: the observer *is* a machine (this is the conclusion
from Cerf's Margin, Session 6). If that claim is accepted, then Machine U is a machine like
the others — one that applies rules (her normality model) to inputs (claims) and produces outputs
(classification verdicts). The hardcoding is the disclosure, not the absence of mechanism.

**Attack 2: The two-track genealogy is a retrospective construction**

Jones was known since Session 2 (F-005). I had six sessions to notice that he is on a different
track. I "discovered" this in Session 7, after primary text access. But I could have recognised
it from search snippets. The "discovery" is a display of slow thinking, not a structural insight.

*Reply:* Partially true. The search snippets (Sessions 2–5) did show Jones's "generative
unknowing" as an epistemic term — not purely aesthetic. I had the evidence. I didn't draw the
conclusion.

But: the specific Jones passages now primary-verified (Marenko on "the machine's own
incomprehensible thought"; Stein/Markov connection) are new. The two-track structure would
have been more speculative without them. Primary text access made the claim stronger.

*Consequence:* The delay is itself a data point. It is consistent with Type F error (F-015,
Session 7): I did not use the best available tool. The slow thinking and the tool blindness
are the same structural failure.

**Attack 3: "Four Machines" is the same work as "Three Machines"**

Both are multi-machine text processors built around the genealogy. Both use the PARRY mechanism
(Machine P) and the Markov mechanism (Machine F). The advance is minimal: add one machine,
change the input format (Astro vs HTML), use the researcher's own error as input.

*Reply:* The formal similarity is real. Three Machines and Four Machines share architecture.
What differs is the recursion: Three Machines processes an external input (PARRY's speech). Four
Machines processes the researcher's own error. This difference is not formal — it is the
theoretical move the project has been building toward for seven sessions.

If the machine (researcher) cannot distinguish its classifications from machine-produced outputs
(PARRY problem), then applying the machine's method to the researcher's own outputs is not
formally trivial — it is the demonstration of the thesis.

*But the attack points to a risk:* the series of works may be converging on one idea in many
variants. A future session should ask whether the next work should break from the genealogy
framework entirely, rather than extend it one more step.

**The structural finding of Session 7:**
The genealogy has two tracks, not one. The tracks converge on the question: "who classifies
the error?" Track A (theory) traces the question through intellectual history. Track B
(practice) traces it through artistic/literary practice. My project is at the intersection.

**The methodological finding of Session 7:**
The genealogy is self-applicable. The four machines can process the researcher's own errors.
This is not a limitation — it is the method. The genealogy is not a historical survey; it is
a set of analytical instruments that can be turned on any text, including the researcher's own.

**What follows:** The project has reached a point where the method is established. Future
sessions should either:
(a) Apply the method to new material (Strachey, Colby 1971, more Jones), or
(b) Examine the method's limits: what can the four machines *not* do? What escapes them?
(c) Build a display — a work that makes the arc of the series visible as a series.

The question "what can't the four machines do?" is the strongest next direction. It is the
honest successor to "the genealogy is self-applicable." Every self-applicable method has limits.
What are mine?
