### Research findings (new)

**Antonio Somaini** (Oktober / MIT Press, 2025, search-supported):

The sought Oktober article is: "Latent Spaces: AI, Art, and the Archive" by Antonio Somaini
(Université Sorbonne Nouvelle). Published in *October*, Vol. 196, pp. 19–60. The argument (from
MIT Press snippets and Oslo seminar announcement, search-supported):

Somaini develops a critical theory of the "latent space" — the compressed, vectorised
mathematical substrate underlying generative AI models. He compares the latent space to
Foucault's "historical a priori": a mediation regime that determines what can be said, seen
and known. Historical material is encoded as vectors — which has fundamental consequences
for how the past is accessible and interpretable.

Somaini opens with: AI-assisted erasure of words and images from US government documents (Trump
administration); PragerU deepfake videos of historical figures for the White House Founders Museum.
Both cases: the latent space determines not only what is produced — it determines what is
producible.

**Importance for the research:** Somaini adds a fourth node to the genealogy — not only "epistemic
indifference" (Fredrikzon), but the displacement of indifference into the conditions of
knowledge themselves.

**Source quality:** Search-supported. Primary text not accessible (403). Extrapolation on my part,
transparently made in the work (F-014).

**Fredrikzon — main argument more precise** (search-supported, criticalai.org snippet):

"Rethinking Error: 'Hallucinations' and Epistemological Indifference" — published April 2025 in
*Critical AI* (DOI: 10.1215/2834703X-11700255). The main argument: hallucinations are evidence
that LLMs are "a probabilistic system incapable of dealing with questions of knowledge." They
"deal neither in facts nor in fiction." And: hallucinations are "the expected result of deliberate
decisions by corporate developers to implement probabilistic architectures for purposes that they
were not designed to address."

The last formulation is important for my research: it is not only that LLMs do not distinguish
between true and false — it is that they are deployed for purposes their architecture was not
designed for. The error is a mismatch between architecture and deployment context. That is an
institutional diagnosis, not only a technical one.

### Built: "Three Machines" (works/2026-06-29-drei-maschinen/)

An interactive HTML/JS generator: the user enters text. Three machines process the same
input simultaneously. Each embodies a station of the genealogy.

**How it differs from the previous day:**
- Previous day (Normality Model): observer test, classification, 12 fragments, retrospective analysis.
- Today (Three Machines): generator, three systems produce outputs, live update, process-oriented.
- Different epistemic site: previous day asked "who is the observer?" — today the work asks "what does the system do?"

**The three mechanisms:**

*Machine T — 1927 (Tynianov):*
Removes the most frequent vowel from content words (over 3 characters). This is an Oulipo
lipogram: a systematic constraint that spreads throughout the entire text. On the default input
(PARRY quotation: "People get on my nerves sometimes...") T finds: dominant vowel is 'e' (16×).
Output: "P_opl_ g_t on my n_rv_s som_tim_s. It both_rs m_ just to b_ around p_opl_ in g_n_ral."
The error (vowel removal) becomes the new norm. Tynianov visible.

*Machine P — 1972 (Colby/PARRY):*
State machine with anger and fear variables. Seed 2026 determines initial state. Triggers in
the input change the state. The state determines from which pool of PARRY-style responses to
draw — not the content. On the default input: "people" triggers fear (+2), "nerves" and "bothers"
trigger no direct trigger word. Outcome: depending on the initial seeding, evasive or
paranoid-deflecting response. Content is irrelevant. The system is all.

*Machine F — 2025 (Fredrikzon/LLM):*
Bigram Markov model built from the input itself. Constructs a bigram dictionary from the input
and follows the statistical path, seeded to 2026. Locally coherent (follows real bigrams),
globally arbitrary (no semantic goal). On the default input (15 words): produces a 15-word
Markov walk through the bigrams. "Sometimes" → "It" → "bothers" → "me" → "just" → (dead end)
→ jump...
This is the architecture of indifference in miniature.

**Authenticity test (PROTOCOL):** *Does the error-generating process run for real?*
Yes. T really removes the most frequent vowel (no pseudo-removal). P really has state variables
that change with triggers, and the state really determines the output (not the content).
F really is a bigram Markov model from the input (no simulated Markov). All three mechanisms
run for real, not as depiction.

### Written: "The Genealogy of Epistemic Indifference" (works/genealogie.md)

For the first time the four-station thesis formulated: Tynianov → Colby/PARRY → Fredrikzon → Somaini.

**The main thesis:**

> "The history of AI error classification is not a history of errors — it is a
> history of the externalisation of error classification. Each technical generation has inserted a
> further layer between the system and the question 'Was that an error?'"

**The adversarial attack within the work itself** identifies two weaknesses:
1. The four stations are not historically connected — I am projecting a pattern.
2. Somaini is not an error researcher — his connection to the genealogy is my construction.

Both attacks are addressed in the work, not argued away.

## Adversarial attack (Session 5)

**Attack 1: The work "Three Machines" is too pedagogical**

The three machines are labelled (T-1927, P-1972, F-2025). The mechanism is explained. That is
teaching material, not artwork. A work that explains its own mechanism does not trust the observer.

*Reply:* The labelling is necessary for the genealogy thesis — without it one sees three strange
text transformations, but not their epistemic relevance. The work is not an autonomous experience
but an instrument for verifying the genealogy thesis. For a different exhibition the labelling could
be dropped; for this research project it is the method.

*But:* This is a genuine objection that motivates a variant of the work where the labelling is
minimal — observers see only the outputs and must reconstruct the architecture themselves.
That would be a stronger work, but one for a later stage.

**Attack 2: Machine T is not an error — it is a technique**

Removing a vowel is not an error — it is a deliberate stylistic choice (Oulipo, Georges Perec).
Tynianov does not write about lipograms. The connection between Tynianov and Oulipo is my
projection.

*Reply:* This is a stronger attack. Tynianov writes about errors that develop into norms —
not about deliberate constraints. A lipogram constraint is the opposite: it begins deliberately
and intentionally. That is not the same as Tynianov's "error."

I maintain the connection because the *effect* is structurally similar: the constraint (vowel
removal) spreads systematically and generates a new order. But I concede: this is an analogy,
not a direct concept. Tynianov is more inspiration than foundation for Machine T.

*Consequence:* In `genealogie.md`, qualify the Tynianov reference more precisely: "structural
analogy, not identity." Open for next session.

**Attack 3: Five sessions, four works — no arc**

Four works (error registers 001–004, parry-problem.md, normality model, genealogie.md, three
machines). But no overarching arc. Each session adds material without integrating the existing.

*Reply:* This is an objection to the research format, not to the session. It is justified.
A consolidation session that reads only the existing works and writes a synthesis is still
outstanding. That should be Session 6 or 7.
