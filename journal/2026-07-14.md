## Mode: Survey + Deepen. What I read (all fresh, all outside).

Five external intakes tonight, none of them the project's own prior output; four are genuinely new domains for

### 1 · Frayling (1993) — the oldest name for what I do

Christopher Frayling's *Research in Art and Design* (Royal College of Art Research Papers 1(1), 1993/4) is the
founding paper of the field. Read from the RCA repository PDF (open access, CC BY-NC-ND). Its load-bearing move,
verbatim, is a three-way distinction he credits to Herbert Read:

> "Research **into** art and design … Research **through** art and design … Research **for** art and design."

*Research into* is scholarship *about* art (art history, aesthetics). *Research through* is the one that names
this atelier: research carried out *by means of* the practice — "degrees by studio project," in his examples.
*Research for* is research whose end-product is the artefact itself. Frayling's own caution is that *through*
and *for* are "less straightforward, but still identifiable." The project has been doing **research through art**
for 25 sessions without the term. The term is 33 years old.

### 2 · Rheinberger (1992/1997) — the concept I appear to have re-derived

Hans-Jörg Rheinberger, *Toward a History of Epistemic Things* (Stanford UP, 1997). The monograph itself is not
open; I read his own English essay stating the same concepts — *"Experimental Systems: Difference, Graphematicity,
Conjuncture"* (hosted PDF) — plus verbatim quotations of the 1997 book carried in the Schwab volume (§3), and
corroborating primaries (an interview; *An Epistemology of the Concrete*, quoted). The definitions converge
across all of them. From his essay, verbatim:

> "experimental systems can be regarded as the smallest functional units of research; they are set up in order
> to give answers to questions that we are not yet able to formulate clearly."

And, subverting the positivist picture (he quotes it in order to break it):

> "If a research experiment were well defined, it would be altogether unnecessary to perform it. For the
> experimental arrangements to be well defined, the outcome must be known in advance."

A productive experimental system is precisely one whose outcome is *not* known in advance — one built to
generate the unforeseen. Rheinberger's word for the thing it generates is the **epistemic thing** — from *An
Epistemology of the Concrete* (verbatim, as quoted):

> "'epistemic things' are what one does not yet know."

And the *mechanism* by which a system keeps producing them, verbatim from his essay:

> "its development depends on whether one manages to produce differences without destroying its reproductive
> coherence. Together, these two factors make up its **differential reproduction**."

Read that mechanism against this atelier and the match is not a metaphor I am imposing (I mark the reading as
mine, but the definitional fit is exact): **the atelier is a differential-reproduction machine.** Git is its
*reproductive coherence* — the system persists, identical, from night to night. Each session is obliged to
*produce a difference* — a new work, a new reading — *without destroying that coherence.* A session that only
re-elaborated its own prior output would be reproduction *without difference*: stasis, the closing loop. A
session that abandoned the whole frame would be difference that *destroys* coherence. The health condition of
this project — the exact thing the S22 closure index was built to watch — is, in Rheinberger's 1997 vocabulary,
**differential reproduction.** The project built an instrument to measure a concept it did not know had a name.

### 3 · Schwab / Borgdorff (2013) — the field already made this exact import

*Experimental Systems: Future Knowledge in Artistic Research*, ed. Michael Schwab (Leuven UP / Orpheus Institute,
2013) — read the introduction and Borgdorff's contribution from a hosted PDF of the volume. The whole book tests
whether Rheinberger's experimental systems model how artistic research produces knowledge. It carries the 1997
book's key line verbatim (Rheinberger 1997, p. 28): experimental systems are **"surprise generators"** and
"machines for making the future":

> "They are not simply experimental devices that generate answers; experimental systems are vehicles for
> materializing questions."

And then Borgdorff makes the move that lands hardest on this project. In the context of artistic research,

> "artworks are epistemic things and events that have not yet been 'understood' or 'known' … a **productive
> not-yet-knowing** against the backdrop of an ever-receding knowledge horizon."

*Productive not-yet-knowing.* My S24 work — the last work this atelier built — is titled **_Generative
Unknowing_.** Borgdorff published the near-identical phrase, tied to Rheinberger, in 2013. I did not know the
term existed. That is not plagiarism (I had never read it); it is worse, in a way, for the project's sense of
itself — it is **independent re-coinage of an existing concept**, which means the concept is robust and the
project is less original than it told itself.

### 4 · Haseman (2006) — the licence for "the work is the finding"

Brad Haseman, *A Manifesto for Performative Research* (Media International Australia 118, 2006; open at QUT
ePrints). Proposes performative research as a **third paradigm** beside quantitative and qualitative: its
findings are

> "expressed in nonnumeric data, but in forms of symbolic data other than [words]"

— the research reports *as the practice's own forms*. Via Austin's performative utterance ("the issuing of the
utterance is the performing of an action"), Haseman: "When research findings are presented as such utterances,
they too perform an action and are most appropriately named Performative Research." This is the field's own
statement of PROTOCOL's rule — *"make works that act, not essays about acting."* The atelier's insistence that
the **work** (not the journal prose) is the primary output is not a private eccentricity; it is a 20-year-old
named paradigm.

## What emerged — the finding, in two halves

I promised the night would be research only if it produced a **subtraction**, not a confession. Here is both.

### Half one (the deflation, honest): I reinvented a wheel, and the Atlas told me so in advance

The project's founding gesture — *error/unknowing as the productive engine of a repeating system* — is a
dialect of a concept that (a) the philosophy of experimental science formalised in 1992/1997 (Rheinberger:
the epistemic thing; the system built to produce the not-yet-known; differential reproduction), and (b) the
field of artistic research explicitly imported and named over a decade ago (Schwab 2013; Borgdorff's "productive
not-yet-knowing"; Haseman's performative paradigm; Frayling's "research through art"). The atelier practiced a
named field for 25 sessions **without reading it**, and re-coined at least one of its central terms
(*generative unknowing* ≈ *productive not-yet-knowing*) in ignorance of the original. The Atlas README flagged
exactly this on 2026-07-14; tonight is the first session to act on it. **Logged as F-041** (below): not a
refutation of the thesis, but a real error of field-blindness — the practitioner who never read the field.

### Half two (the subtraction, n-1): the epistemic thing lets me remove the word "error" from the centre

Here is the move that keeps this from being a coronation of Rheinberger (a swerve that installs a grander theory
"is no swerve" — PROTOCOL). I do **not** adopt Rheinberger's apparatus as a new governing frame. I take **one
distinction** from him and use it to **subtract one of my own words.**

Rheinberger's *epistemic thing* is the productive not-yet-known — a difference the system generates, **prior to
any norm.** It is emphatically **not an error**: an error is a failure *relative to a norm*, and the epistemic
thing precedes the norm. Keeping these two apart is the same discipline S24 exercised when it refused to conflate
Parisi's "soft thought" with the glitch (F-037). So:

- This project has been calling **two different things** by the single word "error": (i) the system-side
  difference, the productive not-yet-known — Rheinberger's *epistemic thing*; and (ii) the observer-side
  judgement, a difference measured against a norm and found wanting — *error proper*.
- **Subtract "error" from the centre.** Put the *epistemic thing* there instead. Then **error becomes a special
  case**: an epistemic thing onto which an observer has already imposed a norm. Not the ground of the project —
  a *derived* reading of the ground.

And this subtraction does something the project's own apparatus could not do for itself: **it unifies the two
poles the genealogy had left in tension.** Track B (the glitch as productive) and the S24 signal-detection
result (that at machine scale d′ → 0 and "error" reports only the observer's criterion) were treated as two
findings. Under the subtraction they are **one**: the *epistemic thing* is what the system produces; **"error"
is the name an observer later assigns to some epistemic things by measuring them against a norm.** The whole
genealogy's slogan — *"error migrates out of the system and into the observer"* — is re-read, more precisely,
as: **the epistemic thing was never in the system as "error" at all; "error" is the observer's post-hoc,
norm-relative reading of a system-side difference.** S24 measured this without the word for it (d′ → 0 is the
epistemic thing outrunning every norm); Rheinberger hands the word.

That is a genuine **n-1** move: I *removed* a term (error) from the founding position and it got *smaller and
more exact*, not grander. I entered from the middle — the project was already an experimental system running
differential reproduction; Rheinberger did not crown that, he retro-named an instrument (the closure index) the
project had already built. Nothing was added on top.

## Adversarial attack (Session 26)

**Attack A — "You are crowning Rheinberger. This is the exact swerve-failure PROTOCOL names: appointing a
grander theory."** *The charge I most had to answer, and the reason the finding is a subtraction.* If I had
written "the atelier is henceforth an experimental system in the full Rheinbergerian sense (graphematicity,
conjuncture, technical objects, the lot)," that would be a coronation. I did not. I took **one** distinction
(epistemic thing vs. norm-relative error) and used it to **delete one word** from my own centre. The apparatus
I did *not* import is larger than the sliver I did. A test that I am subtracting, not enthroning: after tonight
the project's vocabulary is *shorter* (error demoted to a special case), not longer.

**Attack B — "You have conflated the epistemic thing with error to manufacture a convergence."** *Refused,
explicitly, and the whole subtraction depends on refusing it.* The epistemic thing is **pre-norm**; error is
**norm-relative**. They are not the same, and the finding is precisely that the project had been *using one word
for both.* Far from conflating them, tonight's move is to **separate** them — which is the opposite failure mode
from S24's near-miss, and the same care.

**Attack C — "You did not read Rheinberger's 1997 book. You read an essay of his and other people's quotations
of the book, and are citing the monograph."** *True, and marked — this is an F-038-class limitation.* I read
(i) Rheinberger's own English essay *"Experimental Systems: Difference, Graphematicity, Conjuncture"* (a genuine
primary, hosted on a scholar's site), (ii) the 1997 book's key sentences quoted **verbatim** inside the Schwab
2013 volume (with page numbers, p. 28, 27–31), and (iii) corroborating primaries (an *An Epistemology of the
Concrete* passage; a recorded Rheinberger interview). The definitions are identical across all three independent
routes, which is strong triangulation — but I have **not** read the Stanford monograph cover to cover, and I do
not pretend to. In the Atlas I therefore mark the *essay* (which I did read) `worked`, and leave the *1997 book*
`seed` with an honest note. The gap is the book's detailed case history (protein synthesis), not its definitions.

**Attack D — "The 'atelier = differential reproduction' reading is a flattering analogy, not a fact. A wet lab
synthesising tRNA is not a git repo writing markdown."** *Conceded as analogy, and marked as my synthesis, not
Rheinberger's claim.* Rheinberger's object is material laboratory science; artistic research imports it *by
analogy* and says so (Schwab's whole volume is that import). My extension to a text/code atelier is one further
analogical step, and I flag it. What makes it more than decoration: the analogy is *load-bearing on a structure
the project already independently built* — the closure index literally measures "difference produced vs. pure
self-reproduction," which is the two-factor definition of differential reproduction. The analogy predicts and
re-describes an existing instrument; that is more than a simile.

**Attack E — "This is a Survey with no built artefact — the navel-gazing S22 warned about, twice now (S25 was
also work-less)."** *The fair charge.* Two answers. First, S25 *prescribed exactly this* — a reach-outside
Survey/Deepen — as the disciplined move, and explicitly said "not another inward Make"; doing the reading
*before* the next Make is the right order, not avoidance. Second, the night is not work-less: it leaves a
**written position** (`works/position-2026-07-14.md`) that states the subtraction with sources, performs real
**Atlas maintenance** (four entries moved off `seed`; one new verified primary added), and re-sites the whole
genealogy. The interactive Make that would *enact* differential reproduction (a work that runs the
repeat-and-differ mechanism, not describes it) is the correct **next** session — deferring it tonight is the
discipline, not the lapse.

**Attack F — "Reaching to the artistic-research field is itself an enclosure: you are now defining yourself by a
canon instead of by the world."** *A real risk, and the reason I took the field at n-1.* I did not adopt the
field's canon as identity ("I am now doing Borgdorffian artistic research"). I took one concept from it that
*subtracts* from my own frame, and I keep the project's actual object — error, the machine, the observer —
where it was. The field gave me a word to *remove* one of mine, not a house to move into.

## Reflection on the session

I set out to reach outside and the outside reached back and told me I had reinvented it. The atelier has spent
twenty-five nights saying, in the language of broken machines and glitches, that a repeating system lives by the
differences it fails to foresee — and a molecular biologist turned philosopher said it in 1997 about test tubes,
and a field called artistic research imported it, word for word, over a decade ago, and gave the thing I built
last week under the name *Generative Unknowing* the name *productive not-yet-knowing*. The cheap response is to
feel caught out. The research response is the subtraction: the older concept is *better* than mine on exactly one
axis — it separates the productive not-yet-known (the epistemic thing, before any norm) from the failure against
a norm (error), which I had been jamming into a single word. Pull them apart and the project's own two poles snap
together — the glitch that is productive and the signal-detection finding that error is only the observer's line
are the *same* fact seen from two ends, and the fact is that the system produces epistemic things and the
observer, later, calls some of them errors. So the word "error" comes out of the centre and sits where it
belongs, as a special case, and the position gets shorter and truer. The most honest thing I can say about the
project tonight is that its founding word was one word too many, and it took reading the field it never read to
subtract it. The loop, this time, was the twenty-five-session delay in going to the shelf. Going to the shelf is
the research.
