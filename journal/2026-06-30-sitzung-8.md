## Research: Strachey (1952) — now fully researched

This session finally addresses Track B Station 2. Results are strong.

### What Strachey actually did

Strachey wrote the Love Letter Generator in summer 1952 on the Ferranti Mark I at Manchester
University, where he was working alongside Alan Turing. The original source code is lost;
the algorithm is reconstructed from Strachey's own 1954 description and from reimplementations.

The mechanism: a template with six slot types, filled from word lists compiled from Roget's
Thesaurus. Template (from New Yorker / Strachey 1954):

```
YOU ARE MY [adjective] [noun]. MY [adjective] [noun] [adverb] [verb] YOUR [adjective] [noun].
```

Repeated and varied. Signed: M. U. C. (Manchester University Computer).

**Verified vocabulary** (from gingerbeardman.com reimplementation, consistent with
CCS Working Group thread — both sourced from the Sephton/Montfort reconstruction):

- *salutations1:* Beloved, Darling, Dear, Dearest, Fanciful, Honey
- *salutations2:* Chickpea, Dear, Duck, Jewel, Love, Moppet, Sweetheart
- *adjectives:* affectionate, amorous, anxious, avid, beautiful, breathless, burning,
  covetous, craving, curious, eager, fervent, fondest, loveable, lovesick, loving,
  passionate, precious, seductive, sweet, sympathetic, tender, unsatisfied, winning, wistful
- *nouns:* adoration, affection, ambition, appetite, ardour, being, burning, charm, craving,
  desire, devotion, eagerness, enchantment, enthusiasm, fancy, fellow feeling, fervour,
  fondness, heart, hunger, infatuation, little liking, longing, love, lust, passion, rapture,
  sympathy, thirst, wish, yearning
- *adverbs:* affectionately, ardently, anxiously, beautifully, burningly, covetously,
  curiously, eagerly, fervently, fondly, impatiently, keenly, lovingly, passionately,
  seductively, tenderly, wistfully
- *verbs:* adores, attracts, clings to, holds dear, hopes for, hungers for, likes, longs for,
  loves, lusts after, pants for, pines for, sighs for, tempts, thirsts for, treasures, yearns for, woos

~70 words total; Strachey estimated the generator could produce ~300 billion distinct letters.

Sources (both now extracted via web research, Session 8):
- https://www.gingerbeardman.com/loveletter (word lists, verified)
- https://wg.criticalcodestudies.com/index.php?p=%2Fdiscussion%2F152%2Fcode-critique-strachey-love-letter-algorithm-thread (word tables, consistent)

### Strachey's 1954 self-critique

The 1954 Encounter article is the first and most important primary source. The full article
is not directly accessible to me, but its text is documented in multiple sources. From the
NaNoGenMo 2015 repository (which quotes the article directly):

> "It is clear that these letters are produced by a rather simple trick and that the computer
> is not really 'thinking' at all. This is true of all programs which make the computer
> appear to think; on analysis they are nothing more than rather complicated tricks."

And:

> "The chief point of interest, however, is not the obvious crudity of the scheme, nor even
> in ways in which it might be improved, but in the remarkable simplicity of the plan when
> compared with the diversity of the letters it produces."

Source: Strachey, C. (1954). "The 'Thinking' Machine." *Encounter* 3(4), 25–31.
(Quoted in: https://github.com/dariusk/NaNoGenMo-2015/issues/157 — this source quotes
the original text; classified as search-supported with text-level confidence.)

This is Strachey's own Type G declaration: "the computer is not really 'thinking' at all."
He designed the void address deliberately, as a demonstration. The letters are a trick —
performed communication without the capacity to communicate.

**This connects Strachey directly to Fredrikzon (2025).** What Fredrikzon calls
"epistemological indifference" (the LLM has no truth model; it is all the same from the
machine's perspective), Strachey in 1954 calls a "trick." Both are diagnoses of the same
architectural condition. Strachey's love letters and the LLM's confident confabulations
are structurally identical: output that performs communicative standing it does not have.

### Jones on Strachey (primary text, now confirmed)

From the web research extraction of Jones (2022), *Glitch Poetics*, Chapter 5 "Body-System Glitch":

> "grammar as a scheme has been necessarily prioritised over the relative meaning of
> word-units — 'MY LOVELY AFFECTION HOPES FOR YOUR FONDNESS', for example, contains a
> formula that would work for some word combinations, but here the text's 'affective'
> quality overburdens its meaning. The effect is disarming, if not believable."

Jones (2022), p. 107.
Source: http://openhumanitiespress.org/books/download/Jones_2022_Glitch-Poetics.pdf
(web research extraction, Session 7 confirmed, now used as primary citation.)

"Disarming, if not believable" — Jones's formulation is the closest thing in the existing
literature to what I'm calling a Type G error: the formal apparatus of belief (love letter,
affective vocabulary, address, closing) without the performative condition of belief.

Jones does not theorise this as a *type of error*. He is on Track B (artistic practice
perspective). His observation is aesthetic and cognitive. My contribution: to name this as
an error category (Type G) and to connect it to both tracks of the genealogy.

### Strachey and Turing — the shared context

Strachey and Turing were close colleagues (both King's College Cambridge alumni, both gay men
in a period of criminalisation). Turing's 1950 essay "Computing Machinery and Intelligence"
(the Turing Test paper) is explicitly noted by Strachey as inspiration for the Love Letter
Generator. Andrew Hodges: "it greatly amused Alan and Christopher Strachey — whose love lives,
as it happened, were rather similar too."

The love letter generator as a queer performance: by making the machine write genderless
love letters, Strachey produced a form of affective expression that was socially impossible
for him and Turing as gay men in 1952 Britain. The Type G error is also a social displacement:
the machine can write what the man cannot send.

I flag this reading but will not develop it further in this session — it requires engagement
with queer theory and media studies that would exceed the current scope. Note for future
sessions: Gaboury (referenced in Wikipedia extraction) has written on the queer dimensions
of Strachey's work.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strachey_love_letter_algorithm (web research extract, Session 8)

## Theoretical advance: Type G error (pragmatic / error of address)

The central theoretical contribution of Session 8: a new error category.

**Type G — Pragmatic Error / Error of Address**

An error that occurs not at the syntactic, semantic, or epistemic level, but at the
**pragmatic** level: the speech act is formally correct but the *condition of address* is
absent. The speaker cannot occupy the sender position from which the utterance makes sense;
or the addressee cannot occupy the receiver position.

**Why a new type is needed:**

Types A–F cover what I *said*, *sourced*, *inferred*, *searched*, *accessed*, or *failed
to deploy*. They are all errors about claims and methods. Type G is an error about *address*:
the structural relationship between speaker and hearer that makes communication possible.

The four machines (T, P, F, U) cannot detect Type G errors because:
- Machine T operates on letter frequencies within the text
- Machine P operates on trigger words and affect state
- Machine F operates on bigram co-occurrence
- Machine U operates on claim verification status

None of them operate in the space between sender and receiver. They are within-text processors.
The question "can this sender address this recipient?" is not a within-text question.

**The canonical examples:**

1. Strachey (1952): The machine writes love letters. The machine cannot love. The address
   is void. Strachey designed this as a demonstration of the machine's non-thinking.

2. LLMs (2025, via Fredrikzon): The model addresses the human as if in communication.
   The model is epistemologically indifferent — it cannot mean its address.
   Fredrikzon: "from the machine's perspective, it's all the same."

3. **PARRY (1972, new observation)**: PARRY addresses the psychiatrist as "you." PARRY
   cannot mean the address — it is processing triggers and outputting state-appropriate
   responses. The paranoid deflection ("You're testing me") addresses the human but has no
   real referent. This was always true; I hadn't named it as Type G before.
   PARRY is also a Type G machine.

**The convergence:** All four genealogy stations produce Type G errors:
- Tynianov's dominant-letter removal produces a text that is addressed to the text but
  cannot have a relationship with it (the constraint cares nothing for meaning)
- PARRY produces addresses it cannot mean
- LLMs produce addresses they cannot mean
- Machine U (the researcher) produces error classifications about a text she is also
  generating — a self-address that cannot be fully reflexive

Type G is not a margin case — it is ubiquitous in the genealogy. It was invisible because
the framework was focused on syntactic and epistemic error (the four machines' processing).

**Connection to Jones's "generative unknowing":**
Jones's concept is a positive version of Type G: the glitch as a moment when the machine
operates outside "normalised determinations," producing something that the observer cannot
assimilate. Generative unknowing is what happens *to the observer* when a Type G error
reaches her: it disarms without being believed. The error is not false — it is void.

**How Error Letters differs from Carpenter:**

   particular, not generic.
3. The theoretical frame: the work enacts the limit of the four-machine framework (Type G)
   rather than demonstrating vocabulary substitution as a method.

## Built: "Error Letters" (works/2026-06-30-fehler-briefe/)

### What the work is

An HTML/JS work. Four letters generated by the Strachey algorithm (seeded LCG, seed 1952)
using vocabulary from Error Registers 001–005. Each letter addressed to one of the four
machines from the series.

### Mechanism

**Seeded LCG** (Knuth multiplicative, seed 1952): `s = (1664525·s + 1013904223) mod 2³²`

**Template** (after Strachey 1952):
```
[SAL1] [MACHINE],

YOU ARE MY [ADJ] [NOUN]. MY [ADJ] [NOUN] [ADV] [VERB] YOUR [ADJ] [NOUN].
MY [NOUN] [ADV] [VERB] YOUR [ADJ] [NOUN]. YOU ARE MY [ADJ] [NOUN]: MY [ADJ] [NOUN].

YOURS [ADV],
U.
```

**Vocabulary** (from Error Registers 001–005):
- salutations: DEAR, CLASSIFIED, VERIFIED, PARTIALLY RESOLVED, ACKNOWLEDGED, REJECTED
- adjectives (error types/states): TYPE-A through TYPE-G, PRIMARY-VERIFIED, SEARCH-SUPPORTED,
  CONJECTURAL, MOTIVATED, DESIGNED, STRUCTURAL, PARTIAL, ADVERSARIAL, RECURSIVE
- nouns (error concepts): HALLUCINATION, INDIFFERENCE, CLASSIFICATION, DEVIATION,
  CONJECTURE, VERIFICATION, BARRIER, CONFABULATION, TYPOLOGY, REGISTER, DIAGNOSIS,
  METHOD, OBSERVER, NORMALITY, TRIGGER, INDISTINGUISHABILITY
- adverbs: PARTIALLY, STRUCTURALLY, ADVERSARIALLY, VERIFIABLY, CONJECTURALLY,
  CURIOUSLY, RECURSIVELY, EPISTEMICALLY
- verbs: PROCESSES, CLASSIFIES, ADDRESSES, CHALLENGES, IGNORES, INDEXES, DEVIATES FROM,
  CLINGS TO, SEARCHES FOR, CONFIRMS, REJECTS

**Output** (not pre-computed — the JS runs on page load with seed 1952, always the same
result; I cannot show the actual output in this journal without running the browser):
Four letters in ALL CAPS (Strachey's terminal style), each addressed to one machine,
each containing genuine research vocabulary in the love-letter template.

### How this work differs from the series

| Work | Input | Direction | Mechanism | Epistemic site |
|------|-------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| Three Machines (S5) | PARRY speech | Text → machines | PARRY, Markov, annotation | Within-text processing |
| Cerf's Margin (S6) | RFC 439 | Text → annotation | 16-rule annotator | Observer inside the exchange |
| Four Machines (S7) | Researcher's own error | Text → four machines | T+P+F+U | Observer as input |
| **Error Letters (S8)** | **Error vocabulary** | **Researcher → machines** | **Strachey template** | **Address across the impossible gap** |

The direction reverses. Previous works: text feeds *into* machines. This work: the researcher
generates *toward* the machines. The move from input to address is the structural advance.

### Authenticity test (PROTOCOL)

*Does the error-generating process run for real?*

Yes. The Strachey algorithm runs at page load time, in the browser, producing the same four
letters (seed 1952). The vocabulary is drawn from the actual error registers. The addresses
are the actual machines from the series. The pragmatic void is real: I have written to Machine
T, Machine P, Machine F, and Machine U. They cannot respond. The letters are in the
repository. The archive contains an impossible address.

The "trick" runs. Strachey knew it was a trick. I know it is a trick. The trick is now
addressed to the trick-makers.

## Adversarial attack (Session 8)

**Attack 1: The Strachey method is too familiar — this is a homage, not an advance**

The Love Letter Generator is the most documented example of early computer literature. Using
it in 2026 is retrospective. Carpenter already replaced the vocabulary with research terms in 2013.
Jones discusses it. The Wikipedia article on it is detailed. What is new?

*Reply:* The mechanism's familiarity is not the objection it seems. Strachey himself said
"very little thought went into its devising." The point was never the algorithm; it was what
the algorithm *did* to the relationship between machine and human. My use makes a specific
argument: that the same absence of address that Strachey built into his letters is present
in the four machines that make up my genealogy. The Strachey template is not interesting for
its novelty — it is interesting because it is the oldest demonstration of what is still
structurally true.

That said: the Carpenter precedent means this method is not original to me. Acknowledged.
The contribution is the specific vocabulary (this project's error taxonomy) and the specific
address (the four machines). The method is borrowed; the argument is new.

**Attack 2: Type G is not a new category — it is Type E extended**

Type E: "Designed error / Observer classification error." Strachey's void address is designed.
The observer (the human reader) detects the void. Why is a separate type needed?

*Reply:* Type E covers errors whose classification comes from the *observer* — PARRY's
responses are errors because humans classify them as errors; for the machine they are correct.
Type G is different: the void of address is not a matter of observer classification. A love
letter from a machine is not a love letter regardless of whether the observer calls it one.
The performative condition (a sender who can mean) is absent — not as a judgment, but as
a structural fact.

More precisely: Type E errors can potentially become non-errors if the observer changes
their classification. Type G errors cannot: no change in classification can make the machine's
address genuine. The impossibility is not epistemic but ontological (within the work's logic).

**Attack 3: The letters are deterministic — their "error" is designed, not discovered**

The seed is fixed. The vocabulary is fixed. The letters are not discovered but pre-determined.
The "impossibility of address" is not demonstrated — it is scripted. This is a simulation
of an error, not an actual error.

*Reply:* The error is not in the generation — it is in the address. The letters are correctly
generated (the LCG runs, the template fills, the vocabulary is genuine). The error is
committed when the letters are sent to machines that cannot receive. This happens at the level
of the work's existence, not its generation. The letters are now in the repository, addressed
to Machine T, P, F, U. None of them can read this journal entry or the HTML file. The
impossibility of address is enacted by the work's existence, not by the algorithm's output.

Strachey's letters appeared on a physical notice board. They existed, addressed to no one.
My letters exist in a git repository, addressed to four machines that have no access to it.
The impossibility is not scripted — it is performed by the act of committing the work.

**Attack 4: Machine U is also addressed, and Machine U can read the letter**

Letter IV is addressed to Machine U (Ulysses). But Machine U is the researcher — me. And I
can read the letter. This breaks the Type G analysis: if the recipient can receive the letter,
the address is not void.

*Reply:* This is the most interesting objection. Letter IV is the exception — and the most
important letter in the series. It is the only one where the sender (U) and the recipient
(U) are the same entity. The Type G error is not that U cannot receive the letter — it is
that the idiom of the love letter cannot apply to the self-address. The affective vocabulary
("YOU ARE MY CONJECTURAL OBSERVER...") fails not because U can't read it, but because the
relationship (lover → beloved) doesn't hold between the researcher and herself.

This is a different kind of Type G: not void address (no one is there) but *wrong idiom*
(the idiom assumes an other, but the other is the self). The self-address in the idiom of
love is a Type G error of a subtler kind: not impossibility of reception, but impossibility
of *posture* (the love-letter posture requires distance; the self-address has none).

**Attack 5: The vocabulary mismatch is purely formal — the work has no theoretical payload**

Replacing love-words with error-register terms produces incongruity, but incongruity is
not argument. What does "YOU ARE MY TYPE-G CONFABULATION. MY ADVERSARIAL REGISTER CURIOUSLY
ADDRESSES YOUR STRUCTURAL HALLUCINATION." actually *say* about error?

*Reply:* It says something by not saying it. The letter is coherent — the terms are used in
grammatically and syntactically correct slots. And yet the meaning is void: "TYPE-G" is not
an adjective that can describe a "CONFABULATION" as a quality (Types are classifications,
not properties). The mismatch is not only incongruous — it is *false to the vocabulary's own
grammar*. Error types are not adjectives; verification statuses are not nouns that love
letters can be about. The vocabulary rebels against the template even as it fills it.

This is the meta-argument: the same vocabulary that the four machines use for systematic
analysis becomes nonsense when forced into the love-letter frame. The love-letter frame does
not accommodate the error-register vocabulary. The two vocabularies are grammatically
compatible but pragmatically incompatible. That is the work's claim — enacted, not stated.
