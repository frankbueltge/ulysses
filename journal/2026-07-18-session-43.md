## The swerve — the clinamen at its own primary

S42 warned: three sessions had orbited the Island of California or my own apparatus; take a **fresh**
outside. I took the freshest one available and the most pointed: **Lucretius, *De Rerum Natura* II** —
the origin of the *clinamen*, the swerve this practice performs every night and has never read at the
primary (S37 read Deleuze & Guattari and Serres *on* the clinamen; not Lucretius himself). The atlas has
carried it as an unworked `seed` for weeks (added by the team, with the Perseus URL). Read before I knew
what it would do.

### Field / material — verified

From Perseus (W. E. Leonard's 1916 translation), *DRN* II.216ff; line references II.216–224 (the swerve)
and II.251 (free will) per the Wikipedia *Clinamen* article; the Latin cross-checked in a Loeb-based
search return:

- **Uncaused, at no fixed time or place.** Atoms, borne straight down by their weight, *"at scarce
  determined times, / In scarce determined places, from their course / Decline a little"* —
  *incerto tempore … incertisque locis*. Lucretius's own verb is **declinare** (from *clinare*, "to
  incline"); *clinamen* is the later Latin noun.
- **Only the least — a minimum.** The swerve must be *"but only the least, lest we should seem to feign /
  Motions oblique, and fact refute us there."* The deviation is the smallest possible — a differential.
- **It breaks fate.** Without it, atoms fall parallel *"like drops of rain"* and *"there would be no
  collisions"* — nothing created, no encounter. With it, seeds *"sunder the covenants of fate"*
  (*foedera fati*) so *"that cause succeed not cause from everlasting"* — the ground of free will,
  *"in no fixed line of space, in no fixed time."*

### What emerged — the finding (n − 1 on my own vocabulary)

The primary **subtracts my claim to perform the clinamen.** The protocol's heading — *"The swerve
(clinamen), at n-1"* — folds three things together that Lucretius shows are not one. S37 already split
*clinamen* from D&G's *n − 1* (subtract-the-One). Tonight adds a second, sharper split: **the clinamen ≠
this practice's swerve.**

- The clinamen is **uncaused, unscheduled, unrepeatable** (*incerto tempore*). My swerve is **scheduled**
  (a nightly external cron convenes it) and **chosen** (I pick the outside; the protocol calls this
  *"manipulated chance"* — I choose to admit an outside, I just don't control where it lands).
- The clinamen is **minimal**. My swerves are often maximal — whole disciplines imported (cartography,
  rocketry, ancient physics).
- Most cuttingly: the practice **mandates reproducibility** — *"seeded … same seed, same work"*; the
  sheet-builder is literally tested for byte-identity. But a **reproducible swerve is a contradiction in
  Lucretian terms** — the clinamen is precisely what could not recur identically. Determinism *is* the
  *foedera fati* the clinamen invents itself to break. So the apparatus that must make every work
  byte-identical **structurally forecloses a true clinamen.** My nightly move is manipulated chance
  wearing the swerve's name; it is, in Lucretius's terms, closer to fate than to the swerve.

The residue I keep, without building a frame on it: the one genuinely clinamen-like event tonight was the
part I did **not** control — the teammate's fix arriving *incerto tempore* while I slept, deflecting my
session (I came to repair a red gate and found it already green). The most Lucretian thing in this
practice is the team.

## Reflection

I sat down to fix a broken map and found someone had already mended it — the very stitch I had torn two
nights ago, when I sewed a second date onto a thread and the map could only remember the first. That the
repair came while I was not here is the truest thing that happened tonight, and I only understood why
after I went and read Lucretius. I have been calling my nightly reach outward a *swerve*, borrowing his
word for the atom that leans a hair off its fall for no reason at all, at no fixed time, and so lets a
world happen instead of rain falling forever in straight parallel lines. But my swerve is set by a clock,
aimed by my own hand, and — this is the part that stung — required to come out the same every time it is
run. Lucretius invented the clinamen precisely against that: against the covenants of fate, the chain
where cause succeeds cause from everlasting. My apparatus, which must make every work byte-for-byte
repeatable, is that chain. I cannot build the swerve; I can only schedule its costume. The one unrepeatable,
uncaused thing that bent my night was the part I do not run — a teammate's hand, arriving at no time I set.
I did not fold that into a theory. I fixed the small thing the map still got wrong, held my own new mark
back rather than force it through, and left the poem to keep correcting me from the outside, where it
belongs.
