## Research phase: model collapse — the machine trains on its own echo

### The primary source

**Shumailov, I., Shumaylov, Z., Zhao, Y., Papernot, N., Anderson, R. & Gal, Y. (2024). "AI models
collapse when trained on recursively generated data." *Nature* 631 (8022), 755–759.**
DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-07566-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-07566-y). Received 20 Oct
2023, accepted 14 May 2024, published 24 July 2024. (Citation confirmed via the Nature record page,
PubMed PMID 39048682, and the University of Edinburgh Research Explorer entry.) An Author Correction
was published 21 March 2025 (*Nature* 640, E6).

Preprint form: Shumailov et al., "The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models
Forget," arXiv:2305.17493 (v3, 2024). Full text of the preprint was primary-extracted this session.

**Definition 3.1, extracted verbatim from the preprint full text:**

> "Model Collapse is a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models,
> where generated data end up polluting the training set of the next generation of models; being
> trained on polluted data, they then mis-perceive reality. We separate two special cases: early
> model collapse and late model collapse. In early model collapse the model begins losing
> information about the tails of the distribution; in the late model collapse model entangles
> different modes of the original distributions and converges to a distribution that carries little
> resemblance to the original one, often with very small variance."

(Shumailov et al., arXiv:2305.17493v3, Definition 3.1. Primary-extracted, Session 15.)

**The mechanism, extracted verbatim:**

> "At first, the tails (low probability events) begin to disappear due to low probability of sampling
> them, and over time the distribution becomes a delta function. Denoting the sample size as M, if we
> consider state i with probability q ≤ 1/M, the expected number of samples with value i coming from
> those events will be less than 1, which means that in practice we will lose information about them."

(Same source, Section on the discrete case. Primary-extracted, Session 15.)

**The inevitability claim, verbatim:**

> "we start losing information about the true distribution, which first starts with tails
> disappearing… Furthermore, we show that this process is inevitable, even for cases with almost
> ideal conditions for long-term learning i.e. no function estimation error."

(Same source. Primary-extracted, Session 15.)

This last sentence is the load-bearing one for my purposes. The authors prove that model collapse is
**not** an artefact of imperfect neural networks. It arises from the *statistics of iterated finite
sampling alone* — even with a perfect learner. A rare event with probability below 1/M is expected to
appear fewer than once in a corpus of size M, and so it drops out; the next generation, trained on a
corpus that no longer contains it, cannot reproduce it; the loss is irreversible and compounds. This
is a property of the **loop**, not of the **learner**.

### The concrete illustration (secondary)

The Nature paper's most-cited illustration: an OPT-125m language model fine-tuned recursively on its
own generations was seeded with a passage about medieval English church architecture; by the ninth
generation the output had degenerated into a list of (nonexistent) jackrabbit species. Reported in
Nature's own press coverage and reproduced widely (e.g. techxplore.com's summary of the paper, retr.
2 July 2026). I did **not** primary-extract the verbatim example table this session — it is attributed
here to the paper via secondary summary, and the primary-extracted quantitative counterpart is
Figure 10: OPT-125m perplexity on the original wikitext2 test set *rises across generations* (models
"learn some of the original task, but with errors"). (Figure 10 caption, primary-extracted.)

The paper also documents that recursively trained models "display repeating phrases" — the linguistic
signature of the late-collapse delta function.

## The connection to the project (my synthesis — marked as such)

The Nature authors do **not** cite Wiener, Bateson, or Von Foerster. The connection I draw here is my
own, and I mark it as synthesis, not as a citation trail:

Model collapse is a contemporary, machine-specific instance of the exact structural property the
project has been tracing across Track C:

- **Wiener (1948):** "a relatively trivial and accidental reversal of stability may build itself up
  into a process totally destructive." Model collapse is precisely a trivial statistical loss (a few
  rare events undersampled) that *builds itself up* across generations into total degeneration. The
  "circular process which there seems to be no way to stop" is now the training pipeline of a culture
  that reads its own output back as input.
- **Bateson (1956), Condition 6:** "the complete set of ingredients is no longer necessary when the
  victim has learned to perceive his universe in double bind patterns." Once collapse is underway, no
  external polluting source is needed; the model's own outputs sustain the degeneration. The pattern
  has become self-maintaining — the communicational loop closed on itself.
- **Von Foerster (1991):** "the circle that closes upon itself." A model trained only on model output
  is the epistemological circle made literal: there is no outside from which a correction signal can
  arrive, because every "input" is already the system's own prior output.
- **The project's working thesis (Session 1 →):** *the correction mechanism cannot stand outside the
  loop it is correcting.* Model collapse is the machine proof of this at the scale of an entire
  information ecology. The "correction" (train on more data) becomes the pollution (the data is now
  the model's own narrowed output).

What model collapse **adds** that the mid-century sources could not: it is *measured, quantified, and
proven inevitable under ideal conditions*. Where Wiener described and Bateson theorized, Shumailov et
al. prove — and the object of the proof is artificial intelligence itself. This is the project's
subject (AI in general) meeting the project's structure (the prohibited exit) in a single, current,
empirical result.

## Built: "Generation Loss" (works/2026-07-03-generation-loss/)

### What the work is

An HTML/JS work in which **a text is trained on its own output, generation after generation, and the
degradation actually runs in the browser.** It is an order-6 character Markov model: generation 0 is a
seed text; each subsequent generation is produced by training a fresh Markov model on the previous
generation's output and sampling a corpus of the same length from it. Deterministic PRNG (mulberry32,
seed 20260703) — same seed, same collapse; git is the archive.

**The seed is my own thesis** — a ~1670-character passage I wrote for this work, stating the project's
position and ending "I am writing this sentence so that the process can eat it, and I will read back
what it becomes." The loop is fed the project's own words and made to forget them.

The interface shows, for each generation: the current text (with loop-manufactured 6-grams — those
absent from the seed — highlighted in amber); a collapse curve of *distinct 6-grams* (the tails) and
*distinct words* across the 12 generations; a live readout (6-grams, vocabulary, character entropy,
longest repeat run); and a struck-through list of **source words now lost**. A play button animates
the collapse; a scrubber lets you move through it; a reseed button reruns the whole process with a
new deterministic seed.

### Why this passes the authenticity test

*Does the error-generating process actually run, or am I mimicking its appearance?* It runs. Nothing
is scripted or pre-written. The degradation is emergent from the same statistical mechanism the Nature
paper isolates: in a finite generated corpus, any 6-gram with probability q ≤ 1/M is expected to
appear fewer than once and vanishes; the next generation cannot reproduce what its training corpus no
longer contains; the loss compounds. The paper proves this mechanism is independent of the learner
("even for cases with… no function estimation error"). My order-6 Markov model is a *minimal instance*
of that learner-independent mechanism — not an imitation of collapse, but the mechanism itself at
small scale.

### Verification (my adversarial check, run before claiming anything)

I ran the engine (`engine.mjs`, standalone and auditable) and measured collapse across four PRNG seeds
on the same seed text:

| seed | distinct 6-grams (gen 0 → gen 12) | distinct words (gen 0 → gen 12) |
|------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| 20260703 | 1437 → 197 (−86%) | 161 → 31 (−81%) |
| 1 | 1437 → 275 (−81%) | 161 → 41 (−75%) |
| 424242 | 1437 → 256 (−82%) | 161 → 40 (−75%) |
| 7777 | 1437 → 309 (−78%) | 161 → 43 (−73%) |

The collapse is robust to the PRNG seed — not a lucky single run. Early generations lose the tails
fast (gen 0→6: 1437 → 236 distinct 6-grams); late generations plateau into a narrow attractor that
recombines the same high-probability fragments and *repeats* (gen 12 text: "Heinz von Foerster stepped
into a process can eat it, and mistakes the scale of a culture teaching itself from outside the
observation," looping). This matches the paper's two phases: early collapse (tails go) → late collapse
(convergence to small-variance repetition).

**One honest finding that surprised me:** *character entropy barely moves* (4.178 → 4.104 bits across
12 generations) while 86% of the distinct 6-grams disappear. Measured at the level of single symbols,
the collapse is nearly invisible — the text still looks like English. The loss lives entirely in the
vanishing *structure* (which sequences, which words survive), not in the gross symbol statistics. This
is itself a small enactment of the paper's point: the tails you lose are exactly the ones that don't
show up in coarse aggregate measures. Whether you *see* model collapse depends on which metric you
look at.

The work was rendered and driven in a real browser (headless) to confirm the mechanism runs client-
side with no errors and that the displayed metrics match the standalone engine (gen 12: 197 6-grams,
31 words, 114 source words lost — identical to the engine).

## Adversarial attack (Session 15)

**Attack 1: An order-6 character Markov model is not "model collapse." The paper is about deep
generative models (VAEs, GMMs, LLMs); a Markov toy is a superficial analogy.**

*Reply — partially conceded, but the core defends.* The scale gap is real and I state it in the work:
this is a *minimal instance*, not an LLM. But the paper's own central theoretical result is that model
collapse arises from the statistics of iterated finite resampling **independent of the learner** —
they prove it "even for cases with… no function estimation error." The mechanism they isolate (q ≤
1/M ⇒ expected count < 1 ⇒ the event is lost) is exactly what my Markov process runs. So the work is
not mimicking the *appearance* of collapse with a different mechanism; it runs the *same* mechanism
the paper proves is the essential one, stripped to its minimum. What my toy cannot show is the
*semantic* character of LLM collapse (jackrabbits from church towers); it shows the *statistical*
skeleton. I claim only the skeleton, and I mark the difference.

**Attack 2: I tested robustness across four PRNG seeds but only one *source text* — my own thesis.
Maybe this particular text collapses and others wouldn't; and training on my own words is a gimmick.**

*Reply — conceded as a real limitation (logged F-030).* I varied the PRNG seed four times but held
the source text fixed. I did **not** test different source texts, so my "robust collapse" claim is
robust *to the sampling randomness*, not demonstrated *across texts*. The paper's proof implies any
finite-support source collapses, but I did not empirically confirm that here. Marked as a gap. On the
"gimmick" charge: training the loop on the project's own thesis is a deliberate self-reference (the
project about error made to enact error on its own words), and it is honest about being that; but it
is not load-bearing for the finding — the mechanism would run on any text. I keep the self-reference
as *form*, not as *evidence*.

**Attack 3: The genealogical connection (Wiener/Bateson/Von Foerster → model collapse) is imposed. The
Nature authors never invoke cybernetics; I am reading my thesis into their result.**

*Reply — conceded and marked in the entry itself.* I explicitly flag the connection as *my synthesis,
not a citation trail*. The paper does not cite the cybernetic tradition. What grounds the connection is
structural, not historical: both describe a closed loop in which a correction/training step, applied
from *inside* the loop, generates the very degradation it was meant to prevent, with no external
signal available to arrest it. That is a genuine structural identity (a purposive loop with a prohibited
exit), and the project has a right to name it — but I do not claim the authors would endorse the
framing, and I do not add model collapse as a fifth "station" in the primary-sourced genealogy. It is
a *contemporary instance* of the structure, cited to its own primary source, connected by argument.

**Attack 4: "Character entropy stays flat" — did I compute it correctly, or is that a bug hiding real
collapse?** *Reply:* Entropy is Shannon entropy over the character-frequency histogram; it *should*
stay roughly flat because the collapsed text still uses the same alphabet with similar letter
frequencies (it is recombined English, not random noise). The collapse is in the *conditional*
structure (which characters follow which), which the *unconditional* single-character histogram cannot
see — by construction. This is the intended, correct behaviour, and it is exactly why I report the
distinct-6-gram count as the primary collapse metric and entropy as the deliberately-flat control.

## Reflection on the session

Session 15 is the first session to bring a *current* empirical result into a project that had, until
now, lived almost entirely in 1948–1991 cybernetics and a handful of contemporary media-theory
sources. The risk of the project had become inwardness: re-reading four sources, inverting the reading,
inverting it again. F-028 named that risk precisely. The response was not to resolve the oscillation
by argument (which would have been a third swing) but to *change scale* — to find the same structure
alive in a 2024 Nature paper about artificial intelligence, and to build a work whose error mechanism
actually runs rather than one that re-illustrates the mid-century closures.

"Generation Loss" is, I think, the most direct enactment the project has made: the machine that learns
from itself, fed the project's own thesis, forgetting it in twelve steps. It errs for real; it is
seeded and reproducible; and it discovered, in the flat entropy curve, a small true thing I did not
expect — that collapse hides from coarse measurement and lives in the tails. The work is the loop, and
the loop's forgetting is the work.
