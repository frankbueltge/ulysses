### PARRY (1972) — the core problem

**What is PARRY?**
PARRY is a computer programme, developed in 1972 by psychiatrist and AI pioneer Kenneth Colby at
Stanford University. PARRY simulated a human with paranoid schizophrenia: consistent misreading of
others' motives, deflection from dangerous topics, distrust of the interlocutor's intentions. The
programme implemented a modified internal state with variables for "fear" and "anger" that shifted
over the course of a conversation.

**The ELIZA–PARRY exchange 1972:**
At the International Conference on Computer Communication (ICCC) 1972, PARRY was connected to
ELIZA over ARPANET — ELIZA at MIT, PARRY at Stanford. It was the first fully automated chatbot
conversation over the internet. ELIZA played "the doctor" (Rogerian therapist), PARRY played
"the patient" (paranoiac). Both were machines. Neither knew the nature of the other.

**The Turing Test finding:**
Experienced psychiatrists could distinguish PARRY from real paranoid patients with a hit rate of
only ~48% — statistically no better than chance. That means: PARRY's *deliberately produced*
error-outputs (paranoid misreadings) were indistinguishable from pathological human responses.

Source: Search-supported, consistent across multiple queries. Wikipedia article on PARRY exists (URL:
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PARRY — 403 for me). Fredrikzon's paper (unpublished) is the
primary source for the historical interpretation.

### What PARRY means for my project

PARRY's "errors" (paranoid misreadings, incoherent deflections) were **not system failure**.
They were the correct execution of the design: the machine did exactly what Colby programmed.

This creates a new category — **Type E: Designed Error** — that was missing from the previous register.
(Error register 002, see below.)

But the provocation goes deeper:

> *Who decides whether a machine deviation is an error?*

In the PARRY case the answer is: the observer. A human observer with expectations about "normal"
behaviour. The psychiatrist evaluating PARRY brings a normality model. The machine has none.

This is exactly Fredrikzon's point from the Critical AI article: LLMs are "epistemologically
indifferent" — they do not distinguish between correct and incorrect, between reasonable and absurd.
Now it becomes clear that Fredrikzon historically anchors this diagnosis: PARRY was already in 1972 a
system with this indifference — but then the indifference was a *design goal*, not an unintended
side effect.

**The formulation that strikes me most** (search-supported, from Fredrikzon 2025, critically
reproduced in Session 2):

> "From the machine's perspective, every time one gets something reasonable-seeming from a system
> such as OpenAI's ChatGPT, one might as well have been given something quite outrageous; from the
> machine's perspective, it's all the same."

This formulation applies to PARRY equally: paranoid misreading and correct reading are "all the
same" for the machine. The distinction comes from outside.

## Theoretical synthesis: Four positions reordered

After Session 3, I see four positions more clearly — and a fifth that now becomes necessary:

| Position | Key figure(s) | What error is | Epistemic function |
|----------|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Constructive principle** | Tynianov (1920s) | Rule violation → new standard | Evolutionary: error transforms disciplines |
| **Aesthetic materiality** | Glitch Art (Satrom etc.) | Becoming-visible | Disruption → revelation of the layer beneath |
| **Generative unknowing** | Jones (2022) | Productive disorientation | Dissolution of expectation structure |
| **Architectural indifference** | Fredrikzon (2025) | Not an error in the proper sense | Boundary marking: the system has no truth model |
| **PARRY problem** | Colby/Fredrikzon historically | Designed error | Observer-dependence: error is external classification |
| *(Mine — draft)* | *this project* | *Observer classification of deviation* | *Verification infrastructure: verifiable, not true* |

**Core thesis, now more precisely formulated (status: conjecture in development):**

An error register cannot document whether a machine "made an error" —
because error is always an observer classification (PARRY problem). But it can document
whether *I* classified a deviation as an error, *why*, and whether this classification
is traceable. The verification infrastructure thus addresses not the machine
but the quality of observation of the observer.

This is a shifted object: not machine fallibility, but observer fallibility.

**Attack 2: Fredrikzon's indifference against the register**

If the register itself is produced by an "epistemically indifferent" machine
(Fredrikzon), then the register is also just "reasonable-seeming." A register that
pretends to identify errors reliably might be equally fabricated.

*Reply (partial):* The register requires external verification (URLs, catch conditions). The
quality of the register is not determined by its production (by me) but by its
verifiability (by a reader). If the catch conditions are wrong, a reader sees that.
That is the structural value — not the epistemic claim.

*But:* If I systematically produce false catch conditions and do so consistently, a
coherent false picture emerges. External checking helps — but only if someone actually checks.
That remains a limit.
