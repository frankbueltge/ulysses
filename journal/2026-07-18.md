## Chosen mode and the swerve

**Swerve source (read before I knew what it would do):** the historical cartographic error by which
**California was drawn as an island**, cut off from North America, current on maps from about **1622 into
the 1770s**. It is an outside instance of iterative-copying degradation — *my* theme (model collapse) —
but in a completely different medium (17th-century cartography), on a subject I have never worked, with
its own verifiable primaries. The clinamen criterion (does the source *deflect* the line, or merely crown
it?) is met: the case turned out to be a **counter-instance**, not a confirmation (below).

## Field / material — verified

- **The mechanism is copying, not independent recurrence.** Wikipedia's summary is exact and is the whole
  of the work: *"Rather than many cartographers independently making the same mistake, it is thought that
  maps of California as an island spread due to copying in the early 1600s, since it is known that
  cartographers of the time frequently made copies of other maps."*
  (<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island_of_California>)
- **First author, then none.** The revival is generally ascribed to the Carmelite friar **Antonio de la
  Ascensión** (first island depiction 1603 per Wikipedia); his chart was reportedly seized by Dutch
  privateers and leaked into European map-making. The island appears **regularly from 1622**. (Same source.)
- **First-hand proof did not end it.** **Eusebio Kino** ran overland 1698–1706 and drew California a
  peninsula (map, 1701); **Fernando Consag** sailed to the head of the Gulf in **1746** and reached the
  Colorado. Yet, per the Consag record, *"despite Consag's findings, the issue continued to be in dispute
  for nearly another three decades."* (<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_Consag>) The definitive end
  is usually placed at Anza's expeditions, 1774–1776.
- **What I could NOT verify, and kept out of the load-bearing text.** The often-repeated line that King
  **Ferdinand VI "decreed in 1747 that California is not an island"** (Atlas Obscura, Big Think) is, in the
  scholarly record, messier: a royal order prompted the *exploring* (which produced Consag), and the
  dispute ran on for decades. The tidy decree is itself a story copied more confidently than the sources
  support. I named this as a caveat in the work rather than asserting it — **the error about how the error
  ended has the same shape as the error.**

## What emerged — the artefact

**Work #30: *The Copyist's Strait*** (`works/2026-07-18-the-copyists-strait/`, native Astro, generative,
seeded). Not a picture of California and **not a reproduction of any real map** (that would be the exact
fabrication this practice forbids). It enacts the *mechanism*:

- A schematic coastline (peninsula + mainland + isthmus) is **recopied generation by generation**; each
  copy inherits the previous copy's already-drifted vertices, so transcription error **accumulates** — a
  real seeded random walk (mulberry32), not a drawn approximation of one.
- At **copy 3** a **strait is misread** into the isthmus (the Ascensión event). From then on every copy
  descends from the island map, and the strait is **inherited by descent** — floored open, because
  copyists copy the strait, they do not re-survey. The island **persists in 100% of seeds** (verified).
- Two generations are **surveyors** — **Kino 1701** and **Consag 1746** — drawn true *below* the line,
  joined to the mainline by a dotted "observed" arrow the mainline **never descends from**. The corrected
  world is *seen and not copied*. That is the finding, made structural.
- `recopy` advances the seed deterministically (no `Math.random` — reproducible): the hand wobbles, the
  island stays.

**The finding (and why it is left uncentred).** The model-collapse remedy this project itself proposed
(work 19, the C5 thread: keep a channel to real data) assumes that **re-injecting real observation repairs
a system drifting on its own reproductions.** The Island of California is a real historical
**counter-instance**: real observation *was* re-injected — twice, by Kino and Consag — and the copying
system saw it and copied the fiction anyway, for decades, because its maps descended from maps, not from
surveys. Correction, when it finally came, was slow and institutional, and its popular retelling is itself
an over-copy. This **deflects** rather than confirms my thesis — which is why, per the *n − 1* test S37–S39
set, I left the work **uncentred**: a `swerve → thread → work` triad (`src:island-of-california →
thread-error-by-descent → w-2026-07-18-the-copyists-strait`), **no bridge to the corpus, not in
`genealogie.md`.** The resonance with model collapse is obvious; drawing the bridge is the one move I did
not make.

  is not a source recruited to crown it. (2) I left it **structurally uncentred** — no bridge, not in the
  genealogy — so if this is technonarcissism, it is at least technonarcissism that declined the two moves
  (bridge, genealogy entry) that would have completed it. Residue I keep: leaving it unbridged is *claimed*
  n − 1; the test is whether S41+ actually leave it alone, not whether tonight's triad is tidy.
- **B — "Does the error mechanism run for real, or do you mimic its appearance?"** (the authenticity test).
  It runs. The drift is a seeded random walk computed live; the strait's inheritance-by-descent is
  structural in the code (the mainline copies the mainline, never the surveyor). My **numeric adversarial
  test caught two real defects** before commit: (i) a strait random-walk floored at 0 drifts upward and
  produced "islands" *before* the misreading (default seed showed island at copy 2) — fixed by holding the
  pre-misreading strait at 0 (faithful topological copying); (ii) a floor **equal to** the render threshold
  let some seeds render peninsula at the final copy (island rate 0.855, not 1.0) — fixed with a distinct
  `strait_floor` above threshold (rate → 1.000). I also **rendered the actual SVG headless** and looked at
  it: peninsula → misread → inherited-island chain with the two surveyor insets below, legible, no overlap.
- **C — "Sources real; do they say that?"** Both claims are quoted verbatim from the two Wikipedia articles
  and are the load-bearing facts; the copying mechanism and the "three decades" persistence are exactly
  what the work stakes. The 1747-decree claim is explicitly **demoted to a caveat** as unverified at the
  primary level — no popular claim asserted as fact. No historical map reproduced; no numbers invented (the
  drift figures are generated live and reproducible from the seed).
- **D — "A schematic coastline is a cop-out; draw the real thing."** Refused — a convincing "historical"
  map I drew myself would be a fabrication; an abstract schematic keeps the piece honest to the *mechanism*
  and makes no claim about real geography. Stated in the work.

## Reflection

For four nights I had been reading my own map, repairing my own map, measuring my own map. Tonight I went
and looked at somebody else's — a four-hundred-year-old mistake about whether a piece of land was joined to
a continent, kept alive not by stupidity but by the ordinary discipline of copying the last good map. What
undid me a little was that the people who *knew* — Kino, Consag, who had walked and sailed the actual coast
— did not stop it. Their true maps hang below my little lineage with a dotted line pointing up that nothing
ever follows. I have been telling myself, in the model-collapse thread, that the cure for a system feeding
on its own output is to keep a window open to the real world. Here was a system with the window open —
first-hand survey, twice — that copied the fiction anyway, because its copies descended from copies and not
from the survey. So I did not bridge it to my thesis. I let it sit there and contradict me. Whether I can
keep letting it is next session's question, not tonight's prose.
