# Toolbox Candidates v0.3
## Theoretically grounded strategies for Ulysses

## 1. Status of the toolbox

This is not a menu of styles, prompt templates or guaranteed methods. Each entry is a **methodological hypothesis**: a way of constructing a situation in which material, apparatus or public encounter may produce consequences that were not already secured by the initial proposition.

A strategy may be used only when:

- the source situation warrants it;
- its theoretical lineage is understood rather than decorative;
- the material and medium can resist the initial interpretation;
- the operation has an explicit failure condition;
- rights, affected publics and infrastructural conditions have been considered;
- the result is not predetermined as a work, event or contribution.

Multiple strategies may interact, but one should be primary. A project that selects five operators before meeting its material is probably designing a concept demonstration rather than conducting artistic research.

## 2. Existing strategies retained from v0.2

### 2.1 Adversarial Premise Test

Expose a constitutive premise of a project to material capable of defeating it.

**Use when:** a work depends on a claim, category or causal story that may be false.

**Failure test:** the project only seeks confirming anomalies or redescribes every contradiction as proof of complexity.

### 2.2 Technical-object Reversal

Turn a supposedly stable instrument, protocol, classifier or interface into the object of inquiry.

**Use when:** the apparatus defines what becomes measurable, visible or actionable.

**Failure test:** the project merely reveals that technology is biased without showing the concrete cut, consequence or alternative configuration.

### 2.3 Trace-chain Exposure

Preserve and stage the transformations through which a source, claim, image or datum travels.

**Use when:** identity, authority or meaning changes across copying, summarisation, translation or publication.

**Failure test:** provenance becomes a decorative timeline that leaves the transformations themselves untouched.

### 2.4 Differential Series

Repeat one operation while changing one consequential condition and preserving the traces of difference.

**Use when:** a relation can only become legible through controlled variation.

**Failure test:** parameter sampling, aesthetic variation or model roulette is mistaken for differential repetition.

### 2.5 External Return Loop

Return an interpretation or derivative to a source, affected participant or independent practice capable of correction or refusal.

**Use when:** the project risks closing around its own interpretation.

**Failure test:** the return is ceremonial, occurs after publication or cannot alter the work.

### 2.6 Agential-cut Comparison

Construct two or more apparatus configurations that produce different objects, categories or responsibilities.

**Use when:** the apparent object of study changes with boundaries, measurement or participation.

**Failure test:** the configurations differ only cosmetically or are compared from a supposedly neutral master position.

### 2.7 Transductive Interface

Build an interface in which action changes both the state of the material and the conditions under which further action is possible.

**Use when:** relation and individuation matter more than displaying a finished object.

**Failure test:** interaction is optional navigation or gamification layered over an unchanged argument.

### 2.8 Singular Measure

Construct a measure that reveals the contingency of measurement rather than claiming universal comparability.

**Use when:** a local threshold, interval or embodied relation can disturb a standard metric.

**Failure test:** a new index merely replaces the old one and acquires unexplained authority.

### 2.9 Problem-field Mutation

Allow an encounter or operation to reformulate the question, relevant object and field of possible answers.

**Use when:** the initial problem may itself be the restrictive element.

**Failure test:** the question is rhetorically rewritten after the result while the project logic remains unchanged.

### 2.10 Context-drift Test

Move a claim, instruction, image or rule through materially distinct contexts and record the changes required for it to function.

**Use when:** iterability and contextual dependence are central.

**Failure test:** the work simply presents mistranslations or output differences without identifying what each context constitutes.

### 2.11 Constitutive-trace Subtraction

Remove or withhold one trace that appears secondary and test whether the object, claim or relation remains the same.

**Use when:** metadata, caveat, source or process history may be constitutive rather than supplemental.

**Failure test:** absence is aestheticised without a demonstrable consequence.

### 2.12 Series Resonance

Arrange repetitions so that differences affect later iterations rather than remaining isolated variants.

**Use when:** accumulation, feedback or memory is part of the phenomenon.

**Failure test:** a sequence is called a series although no iteration changes the field of the next.

### 2.13 Counter-effectuation

Construct a local response to an event without claiming to represent or exhaust the event's meaning.

**Use when:** a work should actualise one consequence while preserving alternative lines.

**Failure test:** the response retrospectively declares itself the true meaning of the source event.

### 2.14 Lens Collision

Render the same encounter through incompatible, authored selection rules and expose their exclusions.

**Use when:** mapping or exposition risks naturalising one perspective.

**Failure test:** the lenses are visual themes over the same ontology and ranking.

### 2.15 Counter-map with Consequence

Produce a situated map that changes navigation, attention or possible action rather than merely criticising a dominant map.

**Use when:** exclusion, non-relation, scale or access is materially at stake.

**Failure test:** the counter-map reproduces total overview or turns marginality into an aesthetic motif.

### 2.16 Restratification Check

After an apparent opening, inspect which new standards, centres, rankings or dependencies have formed.

**Use when:** decentralisation, openness or participation is claimed.

**Failure test:** the check is a disclaimer that cannot change the system.

### 2.17 Non-relation Preservation

Give formal and archival status to refusal, silence, incompatibility or absence of connection.

**Use when:** a relation would otherwise be inferred from technical proximity or curatorial desire.

**Failure test:** non-relation is romanticised or used to avoid necessary engagement with affected people.

## 3. New strategies introduced in v0.3

### 3.1 Delegated-agency Test

**Question:** Which decisions can the machine make, which can it only propose, and which must remain human or collective responsibilities?

**Operation**

1. Divide the project into consequential decisions.
2. Assign each decision to `machine may execute`, `machine may propose`, `human review required` or `human-only`.
3. Run the project within those boundaries.
4. Record where the actual process violates or renegotiates them.

**Use when:** a project invokes autonomy, collaboration or self-direction.

**Produces:** an empirical account of delegation rather than a metaphysical claim about machine intelligence.

**Failure test:** the categories merely document a hierarchy already fixed and cannot be revised by experience.

### 3.2 Persona/Apparatus Split

**Question:** What does the name “Ulysses” make perceptible, and what does it conceal?

**Operation**

Present the same work through two coupled registers:

- the local voice or persona through which the project speaks;
- the apparatus manifest showing models, prompts, tools, sources, human decisions and publication authority.

Neither register is declared the whole truth.

**Use when:** personification is aesthetically productive but risks false sovereignty.

**Failure test:** the apparatus disclosure becomes fine print while the public narrative still asserts independent authorship.

### 3.3 Model-substitution Test

**Question:** Is the machine relation specific, or would any fluent model produce functionally equivalent work?

**Operation**

Repeat a bounded step with a materially different model, version or non-LLM process while holding the score stable. Compare not only outputs but required human repair, source handling, exclusions, latency, cost and conceptual drift.

**Use when:** a work attributes a distinctive role to a model.

**Failure test:** the comparison becomes a benchmark or leaderboard detached from the work's question.

### 3.4 Infrastructure Inversion

**Question:** Which normally hidden infrastructural condition can be made operative in the form of the work?

**Possible conditions**

- context limits;
- provider refusals;
- latency;
- version change;
- tool permissions;
- token budget;
- repository branch policy;
- geographic or legal availability;
- energy or hardware constraints.

**Use when:** infrastructure materially determines the research but remains invisible.

**Failure test:** logs and costs are merely displayed as transparency theatre.

### 3.5 Provenance Re-entry

**Question:** Can the origin, caveat or transformation history return at the point where it affects interpretation?

**Operation**

Bind provenance to use rather than to a metadata drawer. A caveat, licence, contested source or transformation trace re-enters the work when the viewer or system attempts an action that depends on it.

**Use when:** provenance has behavioural consequences.

**Failure test:** provenance remains comprehensive but inert documentation.

### 3.6 Labour-visibility Intervention

**Question:** Which distributed human work has been compressed into the phrase “the model did it”?

**Operation**

Identify relevant labour—source production, annotation, moderation, software maintenance, commissioning, review—and redesign one part of the work so that this labour changes attribution, pacing, payment, access or form.

**Use when:** a project depends materially on hidden or externalised labour.

**Failure test:** the work names generic “ghost workers” without evidence, specificity or consequence.

### 3.7 Consent and Refusal Gate

**Question:** Does technical or legal availability wrongly stand in for permission and legitimacy?

**Operation**

Before ingestion, transformation or publication, classify material by authority and affected parties. Provide outcomes such as consent, conditional use, delay, anonymisation, return, refusal or deletion. Preserve refusal as a valid research result.

**Use when:** people, communities, sensitive archives or culturally governed data are involved.

**Failure test:** consent is reduced to a checkbox or sought only after the artistic concept is fixed.

### 3.8 Affected-public Review

**Question:** Who can be affected by the work without being represented in its authorship or evaluation?

**Operation**

Invite an appropriately scoped review before publication. The reviewer is not asked to validate artistic quality but to identify foreseeable harms, misrepresentation, extraction or missing authority. Record response, disagreement and changes.

**Use when:** a project makes claims about or materially uses people, communities or contested histories.

**Failure test:** one participant is made to stand for a group or review becomes reputational insurance.

### 3.9 Resource-bounded Score

**Question:** What research and artistic consequences follow from explicit computational and material limits?

**Operation**

Declare a project budget for calls, tokens, storage, compute, energy proxy, human review time and external services. Treat the limit as a condition of inquiry, not only a cost-control measure.

**Use when:** scale, automation or repeated generation would otherwise expand without epistemic reason.

**Failure test:** austerity is aestheticised while actual provider and infrastructure costs remain unknown.

## 5. Anti-toolbox rule

The toolbox must be revised by works. A strategy that repeatedly produces only familiar explanatory interfaces should be retired or reformulated. New strategies may be added only when a project generates a recurring operation that cannot be adequately described by the existing set.

The toolbox is therefore a versioned history of practice, not the constitution of what art can be.
