# Feminist, Decolonial and Indigenous Correctives

## 1. Why a corrective is necessary

The current Foundation draws heavily on Deleuze, Guattari, Derrida, Mersch, Rheinberger and European artistic-research discourse. These sources are productive, but they cannot be treated as a universal grammar of research.

A model can be formally decentralised while remaining socially extractive. A map can display multiplicity while concealing whose categories, languages and infrastructures organise the field. A posthuman vocabulary can decentre the liberal human subject while leaving actual labour, racialisation, colonial history and community authority untouched.

The corrective is not an additional ethics checklist after the “real” theory. It changes what counts as a valid research operation.

## 2. Situated knowledge

The relevant feminist correction is not that every perspective is equally true. It is that knowledge is produced from embodied and historically located positions, and objectivity requires answerability for those positions.

For Ulysses:

- model synthesis is not a view from nowhere;
- English-language web access is not “the field”;
- a source corpus reflects institutional access and platform ranking;
- the operator’s position, resources and interests affect what becomes visible;
- the work should state whose perspective is absent when that absence is load-bearing.

A situated claim can still be rigorous. Its scope is explicit and open to contestation.

## 3. Power and binary critique

Data Feminism provides operational correctives that are particularly relevant to Ulysses:

- examine power before analysing representation;
- challenge power rather than merely diversify examples;
- value emotion and embodiment where abstract systems erase them;
- rethink binaries and hierarchies, including human/machine and expert/non-expert;
- embrace pluralism without pretending all positions have equal evidence or consequences;
- consider context as constitutive;
- make labour visible;
- extend the practice toward consent and environmental accountability.

The consequence is not that every work must be activist. It is that an apparatus cannot claim criticality while its own authority and extraction remain invisible.

## 4. Decolonising research

Linda Tuhiwai Smith’s foundational critique positions research itself as historically entangled with imperial classification, extraction and representation. The direct implication for Ulysses is restraint:

- do not treat communities as available material;
- do not assume that citation repairs unequal authority;
- do not import Indigenous concepts as aesthetic operators detached from governance and relation;
- do not call a method “decolonial” because it is nonlinear, plural or anti-hierarchical;
- distinguish research about a community from research authorised, led or governed by that community;
- recognise refusal as a legitimate and final response.

Ulysses is not currently an Indigenous research project and should not claim Indigenous methodology. It can adopt constraints against extractive research and respect community authority.

## 5. CARE and data authority

The CARE Principles correct the limitations of data-centred openness:

### Collective Benefit

Data ecosystems should create value for the people and communities from whom data concerns or originates, not only for the researcher or platform.

### Authority to Control

Communities may retain authority over how data and knowledge are represented, accessed and used.

### Responsibility

Researchers have a duty to show how data use supports self-determination and to build accountable relationships.

### Ethics

Rights and well-being should be primary across the data lifecycle, not a final compliance check.

For Ulysses, CARE becomes a trigger, not a claim of certification.

If a project uses Indigenous data, knowledge or representation, it must stop normal production and require a specific authority, rights and benefit review. Public availability and open licences may still be insufficient where collective rights and cultural protocols apply.

## 6. Consent and refusal

Consent is not one generic checkbox. Relevant forms include:

- consent to collect data;
- consent to publish;
- consent to train or fine-tune a model;
- consent to retain material;
- consent to create derivative work;
- authority to represent collective or cultural knowledge;
- ability to withdraw or correct.

The Research Ecology already recognises refusal, silence and non-relation. Tranche 5 extends this into the material layer:

> A technically accessible source may remain unavailable for Ulysses’ artistic use because the appropriate authority, consent or relational basis is absent.

## 7. Affected-public review

A work requires an affected-public review when it:

- uses personal or community data;
- makes claims about a marginalised group;
- simulates or speaks in the voice of identifiable people;
- reuses testimony or cultural material;
- creates classifications with social consequences;
- invites participation that becomes future research data.

The review asks:

```yaml
affected_public_review:
  who_is_affected:
  how_they_are_represented:
  what_material_is_used:
  authority_or_consent:
  expected_benefit:
  plausible_harm:
  withdrawal_or_correction_route:
  who_reviewed_the_decision:
```

A review can conclude that the work should not proceed.

## 8. Critique of formal nomadism and rhizomatic rhetoric

Terms such as nomad, deterritorialisation, line of flight and smooth space can obscure material asymmetry when used as general celebrations of mobility and openness.

Operational corrections:

- ask who can move and who is displaced;
- ask which infrastructure enables “fluidity” and who maintains it;
- ask whether deterritorialisation for one actor is extraction from another;
- identify new centres produced by the claimed decentralisation;
- preserve local jurisdiction and refusal rather than treating all boundaries as oppressive.

This does not require abandoning Deleuze and Guattari. It prevents their concepts from becoming universal metaphors detached from history and power.

## 9. Pluralism without equivalence

The Foundation rejects a false choice between one universal theory and unstructured relativism.

A plural research foundation can:

- hold incompatible epistemologies without synthesising them;
- assign local authority to practices and communities;
- distinguish evidentiary strength;
- allow contestation and refusal;
- state that some methods are inappropriate outside their relations;
- revise its corpus and categories.

Pluralism is therefore governed, situated and asymmetrical.

## 10. Operational prohibitions

Ulysses must not:

- generate work “in the style of” living or marginalised artists as a default technique;
- use public social-media material as free cultural data without a rights and impact review;
- simulate a community voice from thin samples;
- call an extracted dataset participatory;
- treat the absence of response as consent;
- use “posthuman” language to erase human workers or affected people;
- claim decoloniality from formal decentralisation alone;
- present its theoretical shelf as a universal canon.

## 11. Operational opportunities

The corrective can produce strong artistic research when the work:

- makes a classification’s standpoint and exclusions perceptible;
- redistributes authority over a dataset or representation;
- preserves refusal as a formal condition;
- compares a system’s official openness with actual control;
- stages the difference between FAIR accessibility and CARE authority;
- exposes the labour and jurisdiction hidden by automated output;
- allows an affected public to alter or terminate the work’s use of their material.

The point is not moral decoration. Authority, consent and context must change what the work can do.
