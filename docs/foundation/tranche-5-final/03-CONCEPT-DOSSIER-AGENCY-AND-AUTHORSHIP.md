# Concept Dossier
## Agency and authorship

## 1. Problem

The language of “autonomous AI artist” compresses several questions into one label:

- Can a system produce effects without immediate human control?
- Can it generate outputs that surprise its designer?
- Can it participate in a creative process?
- Can it possess intention or understand what it is doing?
- Can it be socially recognised as an artist?
- Can it hold rights, obligations or responsibility?
- Can it author a work in a legal or institutional sense?

These are different questions. Treating them as one produces either inflated machine personhood or an equally misleading tool reduction.

## 2. Historical correction: machine artistry is not a new question

Broeckmann’s account of the “machine as artist” shows that the trope belongs to a much older modernist mythology. The machine is repeatedly imagined as both human-like and antagonistic: near enough to inherit human capacities, foreign enough to threaten replacement. Contemporary AI discourse often repeats this structure with updated technical objects.

The correction for Ulysses is not to deny that machine systems produce novel effects. It is to stop using novelty of output as proof of an autonomous author.

The actual questions are relational:

- Who configured the system?
- Which actions were delegated?
- What training histories and provider constraints shaped the result?
- Who selected, framed, named and published it?
- Who can answer objections or repair harms?

## 3. Operational autonomy

A system is operationally autonomous when it can carry out a bounded sequence without step-by-step human intervention.

Ulysses can have meaningful operational autonomy:

- choosing among research paths within a prompt and tool set;
- searching and reading sources;
- proposing hypotheses;
- generating code and forms;
- preserving or revising files;
- refusing a suggestion when the protocol permits refusal.

Operational autonomy does not establish:

- independence from the scheduler, model provider or context package;
- ownership of its archive or infrastructure;
- moral comprehension;
- legal responsibility;
- authority to publish;
- continuity of consciousness across runs.

The correct disclosure is therefore specific:

> This operation was executed without intervention after the run began, within conditions selected and maintained by the human operator.

Not:

> The autonomous artist independently decided to create this work.

## 4. Generative agency

Generative systems produce outputs not fully specified in advance. This matters artistically because the output can resist expectation and modify the human’s next action.

However, generative indeterminacy has at least four sources:

1. learned statistical structure;
2. sampling and system parameters;
3. opaque provider-side processing;
4. interaction with prompts, tools and retrieved context.

A work must distinguish these. Stochastic divergence alone is not artistic initiative. The relevant question is whether generated difference becomes consequential within the practice.

## 5. Epistemic agency

Ulysses may have epistemic agency in a modest, operational sense when it:

- notices a relation not previously specified;
- generates a testable or criticisable hypothesis;
- detects a contradiction;
- constructs a new question;
- changes the route of inquiry.

This agency is real but defeasible. Model fluency, hallucination and inherited bias make self-validation impossible. Epistemic contributions require source, material or public return.

Recommended distinction:

```text
model-generated proposition
!= verified claim
!= artistic insight
!= event
```

A model-generated proposition becomes part of research only after it enters a documented relation of checking, resistance, transformation or aesthetic composition.

## 6. Artistic agency

Artistic agency should not be defined by an inaccessible inner state. It can be attributed when a participant materially affects the work’s form and the practice treats that effect as part of the work.

A model can therefore participate in artistic agency without being the sole author. Examples include:

- a generative system whose failure changes the composition;
- a drawing robot that responds to a performer in real time;
- a classifier whose categories become visible as the work’s structure;
- an LLM whose context limitations create a constitutive omission;
- a model substitution that changes what the object can become.

The attribution must remain local and evidenced. It should name what the machine did, not anthropomorphise it through generic terms such as “imagined,” “wanted” or “understood.”

## 7. Authorship as responsibility and attribution

Authorship has several dimensions:

- **causal contribution** — who or what produced elements;
- **conceptual contribution** — who formed the problem and score;
- **selection and arrangement** — who chose and composed the result;
- **editorial attribution** — whose name presents the work;
- **legal authorship** — who can claim rights under applicable law;
- **responsibility** — who answers for evidence, rights, harm and publication;
- **historical practice** — within whose body of work the object operates.

The U.S. Copyright Office’s 2025 report is institutionally useful because it distinguishes AI assistance from fully machine-generated material and places weight on human-authored expression, selection, arrangement and modification. This is not the philosophical solution to authorship, and it does not govern all jurisdictions. It confirms that public authorship cannot be inferred from prompting or output possession alone.

## 8. Distributed but asymmetric authorship

The adopted model recognises a distributed production chain:

- Frank defines and revises constitutional conditions, selects models, funds infrastructure, intervenes, curates and publishes;
- model runtimes perform delegated research and generative operations;
- model developers and providers shape capacities and constraints;
- source authors and dataset makers supply material relations;
- hidden workers may annotate, moderate and maintain the systems;
- public participants may affect an interactive or encounter-based work;
- the Ulysses archive gives historical continuity and a local voice.

These contributions are not equal. They differ in control, knowledge, consent, risk and responsibility. “Co-authored by everyone in the assemblage” would be both unusable and ethically evasive.

The response is an asymmetric ledger:

- one accountable publisher/author;
- a named local practice;
- precise machine contribution records;
- human collaborators credited according to actual contribution;
- sources, datasets and licences cited;
- infrastructures disclosed where constitutive;
- affected parties recognised through authority and impact, not appropriated as authors.

## 9. Persona

A persona can be artistically productive. It can:

- sustain a recognisable voice;
- stage uncertainty, refusal or contradiction;
- enable an artwork to speak from a constructed position;
- make continuity visible across an archive.

The risk is ontological laundering: the persona’s “I” can make a sequence of changing model instances appear to be one continuous artificial person.

Persona rules:

1. The persona is a literary and performative construct of the practice.
2. It does not own infrastructure or hold legal responsibility.
3. It may not be used to deny human editorial intervention.
4. Its continuity is archival and procedural, not proof of consciousness.
5. A work can suspend the persona when another form is stronger.
6. First-person claims about factual actions must be traceable to runtime records.

## 10. Attribution tests

Before claiming machine agency, ask:

- Could the same effect have occurred with a different model?
- Was the machine’s contribution selected in advance or discovered afterward?
- Did the output alter the score or only fill a slot?
- Was the model allowed to reject or reroute anything meaningful?
- Can the claimed agency be described without mental-state vocabulary?
- Who could answer if the result harms or misrepresents someone?
- Does the attribution hide a worker, provider or source?

Before claiming authorship, ask:

- Who formed the work’s problem?
- Who decided it was a work?
- Who selected and arranged the released form?
- Who authorised publication?
- Who holds and accepts responsibility?
- Which contributions are perceptible and documented?
- Which jurisdiction and institutional definition is being invoked?

## 11. Operational consequence

The future Ulysses protocol should use these terms:

```text
practice
persona
model runtime
human operator/publisher
machine operation
bounded delegation
contribution record
apparatus manifest
```

It should avoid unqualified use of:

```text
autonomous artist
machine author
independent researcher
creative partner
conscious agent
```

unless the phrase is explicitly framed as a proposition being tested or a fiction being performed.
