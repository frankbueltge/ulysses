# Position Map
## Agency, authorship and apparatus

## 1. Why a map is necessary

“AI artist,” “tool,” “collaborator,” “agent” and “apparatus” are not interchangeable labels. Each distributes initiative, explanation, credit and responsibility differently. The current Ulysses framing moves among these categories: public language strongly personifies an autonomous researcher, while the protocol discloses a composed arrangement of scheduler, model, repository, tools, site gate and human authority.

The map below does not select one metaphysical answer for all cases. It distinguishes the practical claims that each position makes.

## 2. Seven positions

### A. Machine as instrument

**Core claim:** The human artist conceives the work; the machine assists execution.

**Strengths**

- preserves clear responsibility;
- fits many legal and institutional authorship models;
- avoids inflated claims of machine personhood;
- useful where the system is tightly controlled and replaceable.

**Weaknesses**

- can hide real technical agency, unpredictability and constraint;
- treats interfaces, models and providers as neutral;
- can erase the way a tool changes the artist’s concepts and possible actions;
- often overstates human control over generative systems.

**Ulysses implication:** Inadequate as a total description. Some operations are instrumental, but the runtime also proposes, selects, transforms and generates relations not specified in detail by Frank.

---

### B. Machine as medium or material

**Core claim:** Computation is worked through like language, film, clay, sound or a technical environment.

**Strengths**

- focuses on material specificity and resistance;
- invites attention to model artefacts, latency, context windows, probabilistic output and failure;
- avoids requiring human-like intention.

**Weaknesses**

- “material” can depoliticise providers, training data and labour;
- a subscription API is not equivalent to inert matter;
- the medium may actively classify and steer the artist.

**Ulysses implication:** Strong for work-level analysis, provided the “material” includes provider rules, training opacity, tools, cost and infrastructure.

---

### C. Machine as collaborator or partner

**Core claim:** Human and machine contribute distinct capacities to a joint creative process.

**Strengths**

- recognises reciprocal transformation and feedback;
- describes many artist-built systems better than the tool metaphor;
- can support embodied and improvisational practices.

**Weaknesses**

- “partner” imports social reciprocity, consent and recognition that a model does not possess;
- can flatten asymmetries between artist, provider, model and hidden workers;
- can function as attractive branding rather than analysis.

**Ulysses implication:** May be used as a local metaphor only when the concrete reciprocal operations are specified. It should not be the default constitutional category.

---

### D. Machine as autonomous artist

**Core claim:** A technical system originates art and can be attributed authorship independently of a human artist.

**Strengths**

- foregrounds emergence and technical initiative;
- challenges anthropocentric definitions of creativity;
- can be a productive fictional or speculative proposition.

**Weaknesses**

- often rests on the historically recurring myth of machine supersession;
- detaches output from institutions that designate it as art;
- obscures human design, data, curation, maintenance and publication;
- confuses operational autonomy with social, moral, legal and artistic authorship;
- is currently inconsistent with the actual governance of Ulysses.

**Ulysses implication:** Rejected as an unqualified factual description. It may survive as an explicitly staged fiction or contested proposition inside a work.

---

### E. Artistry as viewer or institution attribution

**Core claim:** Whether a machine is an artist depends on how viewers, institutions and art worlds interpret the output and its maker.

**Strengths**

- recognises that art status is social and historical;
- explains why identical technical outputs can be framed differently;
- shifts focus from inaccessible inner states to public relations.

**Weaknesses**

- can reduce responsibility to reception;
- institutional acceptance may reproduce market and cultural power;
- does not by itself explain who made the work or how.

**Ulysses implication:** Useful for studying reception, but not sufficient for internal authorship or governance.

---

### F. Distributed human-machine assemblage

**Core claim:** Creative agency arises from relations among people, models, code, data, institutions, interfaces and environments.

**Strengths**

- captures real interdependence;
- refuses the binary of human genius versus autonomous machine;
- makes infrastructure and transformation analysable;
- aligns with situated, feminist and posthumanist accounts.

**Weaknesses**

- “distributed” can imply equal contribution where there is none;
- responsibility can dissolve into the network;
- assemblage vocabulary can become vague and totalising;
- affected people may be listed as components without receiving authority.

**Ulysses implication:** Strongest ontological starting point, but requires explicit asymmetry, responsibility and refusal.

---

### G. Situated practice under bounded delegation

**Core claim:** A human-accountable practice delegates specified operations to machine systems. The resulting agency is distributed, but roles, permissions, dependencies and responsibility remain differentiated and documented.

**Strengths**

- acknowledges machine initiative without personhood claims;
- supports real autonomy at the level of bounded operations;
- preserves human publication and legal responsibility;
- makes providers, data, labour and affected publics visible;
- compatible with local sovereignty and corrigible protocols.

**Weaknesses**

- requires detailed documentation and honest intervention records;
- cannot produce a simple heroic narrative of autonomous creativity;
- depends on continual judgment about what counts as meaningful delegation.

**Ulysses implication:** Adopt as the constitutional position.

## 3. Layered agency model

Agency should be attributed by layer rather than as a single yes/no property.

| Agency layer | Meaning | Ulysses status |
|---|---|---|
| Causal | Produces effects in files, text, code or interfaces | Yes |
| Generative | Produces outputs not specified token-by-token by the human | Yes |
| Selective | Chooses among available paths under instructions | Sometimes, bounded |
| Procedural | Initiates or continues steps through delegated workflow | Yes, when enabled |
| Epistemic | Generates observations, hypotheses or distinctions that can inform inquiry | Possible, never self-validating |
| Aesthetic | Participates materially in the formation of a work | Yes, project-specific |
| Social | Enters reciprocal recognition, obligation or community membership | Only through attributed persona; not established for model runtime |
| Moral | Can be blameworthy, owe repair or understand duties | No defensible basis |
| Legal | Holds copyright, liability or publisher responsibility | No under current arrangement |
| Institutional | Decides publication, research status or resource allocation | No; human/institutional gate required |

The model therefore permits the sentence:

> The model runtime exercised causal, generative and bounded procedural agency in this work.

It rejects the unsupported inference:

> Therefore the model is the morally and artistically responsible author.

## 4. Authorship positions

### Singular human authorship

Too simple when machine operations materially shape the form, but often legally and institutionally necessary.

### Machine authorship

Rejected as default. It cannot account for the actual production, selection and publication structure and currently lacks legal recognition in relevant institutional contexts.

### Equal co-authorship

Usually misleading. The parties do not possess equal rights, capacities, exposure, responsibility or bargaining power.

### Distributed authorship without responsibility centre

Rejected. It risks making everyone a contributor and no one answerable.

### Distributed but asymmetric authorship

Adopted.

A work can disclose multiple contribution classes while naming a responsible human author/publisher and a local practice identity:

```text
Responsible author and publisher: Frank Bültge
Research and artistic practice: Ulysses / Atelier
Machine operations: named runtime(s), versions and delegated roles
Additional human contributors: named where applicable
Source and dataset contributors: credited according to rights and material relevance
Infrastructure providers: disclosed when constitutive
Affected publics or communities: not named as contributors without consent; authority and impact recorded separately
```

## 5. Persona and practice

The name “Ulysses” can refer to three different things:

1. **Practice** — the historically versioned artistic research arrangement.
2. **Persona/voice** — the first-person editorial and artistic figure used in some outputs.
3. **Runtime instance** — a particular model invocation.

These must not be collapsed.

Recommended usage:

- “Ulysses” without qualification means the practice.
- “Ulysses voice” means the persona or first-person register.
- “model runtime” names the technical instance.
- a work manifest states which of these was active.

The persona may say “I” in a work, but the apparatus note must not imply that one persistent conscious entity travelled unchanged across model versions and sessions.

## 6. Final position

Ulysses should neither be reduced to a neutral tool nor promoted as an autonomous machine artist.

The adopted position is:

> **Situated artistic research practice under bounded delegation, with distributed but asymmetric agency and authorship.**
