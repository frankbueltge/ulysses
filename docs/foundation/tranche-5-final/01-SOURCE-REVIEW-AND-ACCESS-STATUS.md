## Tranche 5

## 1. Review principle

This tranche distinguishes:

- **full-text reviewed** — complete text available and substantially read;
- **chapter/article reviewed** — relevant complete contribution read inside a collection;
- **official summary or report reviewed** — authoritative source, but not the complete underlying literature;
- **case documentation reviewed** — artist, museum, repository or project material;
- **secondary orientation only** — useful for locating debates, not treated as decisive evidence;
- **identified gap** — important source not yet available in sufficient form.

No source is treated as a universal authority. The aim is a conflict-aware constellation that can support operational decisions while preserving disagreement.

## 3. Machine art and artistic practice

### Full collection reviewed selectively

**The Machine as Art / The Machine as Artist** (2020)

Relevant complete contributions reviewed or closely examined:

- Aaron Hertzmann — “Can Computers Create Art?”
- Andy Lomas — “On Hybrid Creativity”
- Andreas Broeckmann — “The Machine as Artist as Myth”
- Sofian Audry and Jon Ippolito — “Can Artificial Intelligence Make Art without Artists? Ask the Viewer”
- Arthur Still and Mark d’Inverno — Deweyan account of machine artistry
- Frieder Nake interview
- editorial and historical framing

Principal contribution: the “machine as artist” question is historically recurrent and often mythological. The collection also exposes genuine disagreement: some accounts reserve artmaking for socially situated human agents; others treat artistry as attribution, emergent system property or hybrid relation.

### Full collection reviewed selectively

**Artificial Intelligence — Intelligent Art? Human-Machine Interaction and Creative Practice** (2024)

Relevant chapters:

- Jens Schröter — automation, creativity and cognitive labour
- Hannes Bajohr — artificial semantics and meaning
- Guljajeva, Canet Sola and Clarke — artist-guided neural networks
- Serbanescu et al. — embodied voice and techno-social systems
- Jennifer Becker — collaborative writing with GPT-3

- Sebastian Kunas — human-machine relationships in musical practice
- Jan Torge Claussen — roughness and failure aesthetics

Principal contribution: “AI as tool” versus “AI as autonomous creator” is an inadequate binary. Practice is better described through changing roles, custom datasets, interfaces, embodied relations, provider constraints and artist-guided technical development.

## 4. Feminist and situated apparatus theory

### Canonical sources used with mixed access

**Donna Haraway — “Situated Knowledges” (1988)**  
The canonical argument was reviewed through the published essay’s established formulation, scholarly references and later operational literature. The decisive point used here is not “all views are subjective,” but that objectivity requires partial location, embodied perspective and answerability for how one learns to see. Full licensed text was not stored in the project corpus.

**Karen Barad — “Posthumanist Performativity” (2003); Meeting the Universe Halfway (2007)**  
The concept of apparatus and agential cut was reviewed through the canonical article, prior Foundation work and reliable scholarly summaries. Barad’s apparatus is not a static tool between pre-existing subject and object; it is a material-discursive practice through which provisional boundaries and exclusions are enacted. The full book was not available in the project corpus.

**Lucy Suchman — Plans and Situated Actions / Human-Machine Reconfigurations**  
Reviewed through accessible descriptions, excerpts and established STS reception. The load-bearing insight is that action is situated and contingent rather than execution of a complete prior plan, and that human-machine boundaries and claims of autonomy are configured in practice. Full monograph access remains a gap.

**D’Ignazio and Klein — Data Feminism (2020) and “Data Feminism for AI” (2024)**  
The open-access framework and the 2024 AI adaptation were reviewed. Relevant principles include examining and challenging power, elevating embodiment, rethinking binaries, embracing pluralism, considering context, making labour visible, and extending the framework toward consent and environmental impact.

## 5. Decolonial and Indigenous correctives

### Authoritative source reviewed

**Global Indigenous Data Alliance — CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance**

The official source defines the need to complement data-centred openness with people- and purpose-oriented principles. The relevant categories are:

- Collective Benefit
- Authority to Control
- Responsibility
- Ethics

The source explicitly argues that FAIR-style openness can ignore power differentials and historical contexts. This is directly relevant to Ulysses because public availability, technical accessibility and repository openness do not establish ethical permission for artistic reuse.

### Foundational source, limited direct access

**Linda Tuhiwai Smith — Decolonizing Methodologies**

The book’s central critique — research as historically entangled with imperial extraction and representation — is used cautiously through authoritative summaries and extensive scholarly reception. Full-text close reading remains necessary before making more specific claims or deriving Indigenous methods for use outside their contexts.

### Additional critical sources

- Indigenous Data Sovereignty and CARE literature
- Abeba Birhane and decolonial AI scholarship
- Roberts and Montoya on data mining as colonial practice
- Mignolo and Vázquez on decolonial aesthesis, used as orientation rather than a complete operational doctrine

Important restriction: Ulysses must not treat decolonial or Indigenous methodologies as detachable tools. Their primary operational consequence here is to limit extraction, require authority and benefit analysis, preserve refusal, and prevent universalisation of a European philosophical framework.

## 6. Labour, documentation and infrastructure

### Primary or original framework sources

- Gebru et al. — “Datasheets for Datasets”
- Mitchell et al. — “Model Cards for Model Reporting”
- D’Ignazio and Klein — “Make Labour Visible” and the AI extension
- Casilli et al. — global inequalities in data work
- participatory annotation and feminist data work literature
- Luccioni and colleagues — reporting environmental impacts of ML

These sources are not adopted as bureaucratic templates without modification. They establish that datasets, models and outputs require documented provenance, intended use, exclusions, labour and limitations.

### Legal and policy source reviewed

**U.S. Copyright Office — Copyright and Artificial Intelligence, Part 2: Copyrightability (January 2025)**

Relevant findings:

- purely AI-generated material is not copyrightable under current US doctrine;
- AI assistance does not prevent protection of human-authored expression;
- prompts alone do not normally provide sufficient expressive control with current general-purpose systems;
- human selection, arrangement and modification may be protectable;
- disclosure of AI-generated material and human contribution is required in registration contexts.

This is not treated as a global definition of artistic authorship. It is nevertheless a useful institutional stress test against public claims that a model is the responsible author.

The Copyright Office’s AI page also records that Part 3 on model training was released in pre-publication form in May 2025. Legal questions around training data remain unsettled and jurisdiction-specific.

## 8. Remaining gaps

The Foundation is sufficient for protocol derivation, but the following should remain a living research queue:

- full close reading of Suchman’s *Human-Machine Reconfigurations*;
- full close reading of Linda Tuhiwai Smith before invoking specific Indigenous research practices;
- deeper engagement with Black feminist technology studies and disability justice;
- European copyright and moral-rights implications for the actual publication jurisdiction;
- direct accounting information from the exact model providers used by Ulysses;
- empirical energy and water data for the specific runtime;
- a direct interview or structured self-audit by Frank on actual human interventions, prompt design and selection history;
- perspectives of viewers and affected source communities once concrete works are proposed.

These gaps do not require delaying the protocol. They require the protocol to remain corrigible and to prohibit claims that exceed the available evidence.
