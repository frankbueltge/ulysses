# Review protocol and corpus audit

## 1. Review type

This is a **critical scoping review**, not a systematic review in the medical-science sense.

The field is conceptually heterogeneous, partly institution-driven and distributed across philosophy, artistic practice, research policy, artist writings, exhibition formats and case studies. The review therefore needs:

- transparent inclusion and exclusion decisions;
- primary texts wherever available;
- explicit source-status labels;
- comparison of incompatible positions;
- separation of textual claims from our operational inferences;
- later validation through concrete works and artistic-research cases.

## 2. Governing research questions

1. What makes a practice artistic research rather than art, art-based research, research for art or research about art?
2. What kind of knowledge, insight, awareness or transformation is claimed?
3. Is artistic research methodical, anti-methodical, project-specific or method-generating?
4. What role do artworks, processes, documentation and exposition play?
5. How are rigor, criticism and public accountability possible without reducing art to propositional science?
6. Which concepts can become operational without being trivialised?
7. What changes when machine systems participate in the practice?

## 3. Source-status vocabulary

- `FULL_PRIMARY_READ` — primary source read in full for the relevant argument.
- `SUBSTANTIAL_PRIMARY_READ` — relevant sections read closely; full source larger than current tranche.
- `SECONDARY_OR_METADATA_ONLY` — only abstract, catalogue entry or secondary summary available; no substantive claims should rest on it alone.
- `QUEUED` — identified as relevant but not yet reviewed.

## 4. Tranche 1 source status

### Christopher Frayling — *Research in Art and Design* (1993/94)

**Status:** `FULL_PRIMARY_READ`

Relevant contributions:

- dismantles stereotypes of artist, designer and scientist;
- distinguishes research **into**, **for** and **through** art and design;
- insists that some art counts as research and some does not;
- makes communicability and an object of inquiry beyond the researcher important;
- warns against treating every act of making as research.

### Henk Borgdorff — *The Debate on Research in the Arts* (2006)

**Status:** `FULL_PRIMARY_READ`

Relevant contributions:

- distinguishes research **on**, **for** and **in** the arts;
- frames ontological, epistemological and methodological questions;
- defines artistic research as original investigation in and through artistic objects and processes;
- treats situated and tacit knowledge as embodied in practice;
- requires contextual questions, appropriate methods, documentation and dissemination;
- acknowledges that individual projects may generate their own criteria.

### Julian Klein — *What is Artistic Research?* (2010/2012)

**Status:** `FULL_PRIMARY_READ`

Relevant contributions:

- replaces “What is artistic research?” with “When is research artistic?”;
- defines the artistic as a mode of experience rather than a professional identity;
- treats artistic experience as reflexive, embodied and inseparable from how it is undergone;
- proposes “felt knowledge” as artistic research’s epistemic mode;
- rejects a categorical art/science separation.

### Dieter Mersch — *Art, Knowledge, and Reflexivity* (2017)

**Status:** `FULL_PRIMARY_READ`

Relevant contributions:

- distinguishes goal-directed research from open artistic searching;
- rejects the uncritical transfer of proposition, truth-validation and method to art;
- locates artistic cognition in media-specific, non-propositional practices;
- treats contradictions, paradoxes and perceptual limits as artistic modes of cognition;
- replaces knowledge production with disclosure, awareness and opening;
- defines artistic searching as zetetic and self-transforming.

### Henke, Mersch, van der Meulen, Strässle, Wiesel — *Manifest der Künstlerischen Forschung* (2020)

**Status:** `SUBSTANTIAL_PRIMARY_READ`

Relevant contributions:

- diagnoses artistic research as historically entangled with Bologna-era academisation;
- criticises imported scientific models, evaluation formats and output logics;
- warns against coarse analogies to laboratories, experimental systems, surveys and ethnography;
- demands emancipation from a false competition with university research;
- frames artistic research as needing a distinctive aesthetic practice rather than a weaker academic science.

### Vytautas Michelkevičius — *Mapping Artistic Research: Towards Diagrammatic Knowing* (2018)

**Status:** `SUBSTANTIAL_PRIMARY_READ`

Relevant contributions:

- maps the field as a contested network of definitions, institutions, methods and roles;
- treats diagrams and mapping as both research method and form of knowing;
- documents methodological promiscuity and identity transformations between artist, researcher and curator;
- links field theory to concrete projects and doctoral configurations;
- keeps the institutional and political production of “artistic research” visible.

### Michael Schwab (ed.) — *Transpositions: Aesthetico-Epistemic Operators in Artistic Research* (2018)

**Status:** `SUBSTANTIAL_PRIMARY_READ` — introduction and relevant chapters/sections.

Relevant contributions:

- treats transposition as a situated transformation rather than representation of an unchanged identity;
- uses concepts operationally, as underdetermined epistemic things rather than fixed theoretical foundations;
- defines transpositions as methodological hypotheses that may transform the practice itself;
- makes the aesthetic and epistemic mutually dependent in concrete operations;
- shows that artistic research may keep epistemic objects open rather than stabilising them into propositions.

### Brad Haseman — *A Manifesto for Performative Research* (2006)

**Status:** `SECONDARY_OR_METADATA_ONLY`

Current reliable note:

- proposes performative research as a third paradigm alongside quantitative and qualitative research;
- argues that findings may be expressed in the symbolic forms of the practice itself.

The full text must be obtained and read before this position is used as a foundation.

### Sarat Maharaj — *Know-how and No-How*

**Status:** `QUEUED`

The text is central for tacit knowledge, indeterminacy and non-methodical artistic knowing, but has not yet been incorporated into the findings.

## 5. Important corpus bias

The current shelf is strong in theory and field definition but weaker in close analysis of actual works. That creates a danger of building a philosophically sophisticated protocol that still produces weak art.

The foundation must therefore develop two parallel corpora:

1. **theory corpus** — concepts, epistemologies, critiques and institutional histories;
2. **case corpus** — concrete works, artist research projects, exhibitions, failures and critical receptions.

No operational toolbox should be frozen before both corpora interact.

## 6. Anti-circularity rule

Existing Ulysses tracks, works and annotations are historical evidence, not the interpretive frame for the review.

For each source we record:

- what the source argues;
- what problem it addresses;
- what it would criticise in Ulysses;
- what Ulysses might wrongly appropriate from it;
- only then, what provisional operation it may suggest.
