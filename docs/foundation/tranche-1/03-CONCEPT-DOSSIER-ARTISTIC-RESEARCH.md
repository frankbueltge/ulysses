# Concept dossier 01 — Artistic research

## Status

`PROVISIONAL`

This dossier records incompatible uses rather than enforcing one canonical definition.

## 1. Problem addressed

The concept “artistic research” is used to describe several different relations:

- research about art;
- research supporting art;
- research conducted through artistic practice;
- artistic practice presented as research;
- research that becomes artistic in its mode of experience;
- institutionally recognised research in art academies;
- a critique or transformation of what counts as art, research or knowledge.

Without distinctions, Ulysses can call almost anything research.

## 2. Major positions

### Differentiation position

Frayling and Borgdorff distinguish research about/on, for and through/in art. The key function is to prevent “all art is research” from becoming empty.

### Modal position

Klein argues that the artistic is a mode in which research may occur. Artistic experience is reflexive, embodied and felt.

### Aesthetic-difference position

Mersch argues that artistic cognition differs structurally from scientific and philosophical cognition. It operates through media, perception, contradiction, disclosure and singularity rather than propositions and validation.

### Institutional-critical position

The *Manifest* argues that the category is politically and institutionally produced, and that its imitation of university research can suppress art’s own epistemic capacities.

### Transformational position

Schwab’s transpositional framework understands artistic research as concrete operations that may change objects, identities and practices, including the concept of artistic research itself.

### Cartographic position

Michelkevičius treats the field as a plural and contested network that needs diagrammatic, historical and institutional mapping rather than a single essence.

## 3. Shared minimum

Despite conflict, the reviewed positions support a minimal distinction:

Artistic research is not merely:

- gathering references before making art;
- using scientific subject matter;
- visualising data;
- explaining a philosophical concept interactively;
- documenting an artistic process;
- producing art inside an academic institution;
- calling an artwork “research”.

At minimum, practice must participate constitutively in the inquiry.

## 4. Epistemic claims

Possible claims include:

- new propositional knowledge;
- articulation of tacit or embodied knowledge;
- felt knowledge through artistic experience;
- disclosure of overlooked or excluded phenomena;
- awareness rather than knowledge production;
- transformation of concepts, materials or practices;
- creation of singular epistemic objects that resist full conceptual stabilisation.

These claims are not interchangeable. Every Ulysses project must state which claim it is making, and which it is **not** making.

## 5. Methodological consequences

The literature does not justify either a universal method or total methodological freedom.

A defensible position is:

- no universal artistic-research workflow;
- project-specific methods or strategies;
- methods may emerge during practice;
- choices and changes should remain traceable;
- the method must be answerable to the material and problem;
- the work may challenge its starting method;
- ethical, factual and source obligations are not suspended by aesthetic autonomy.

## 6. Criteria of rigor

Rigor may include:

### Research rigor

- relation to an existing question, context or field;
- source and material integrity;
- awareness of prior work;
- clear distinction between claim, interpretation and speculation;
- public traceability of relevant decisions;
- honest scope and uncertainty.

### Artistic rigor

- exactness of form, medium and situation;
- non-substitutability by explanatory prose;
- consequences of material and medium;
- capacity to produce perception, experience or transformation rather than illustrate it;
- resistance to default style, generic interaction and theoretical decoration;
- ability of the work to exceed the initial concept.

### Reflexive rigor

- visibility of framing and exclusions;
- capacity to question the inquiry’s own apparatus;
- willingness to revise or abandon the initial problem;
- acknowledgement of the researcher’s and machine system’s position.

## 7. Misuse risks for Ulysses

1. **The theory shield** — weak work protected by sophisticated references.
2. **The research costume** — ordinary art production described with questions, methods and findings.
3. **The interface fallacy** — interactivity mistaken for artistic experience.
4. **The data fallacy** — using data mistaken for generating knowledge.
5. **The non-propositional alibi** — obscurity or incoherence protected from criticism.
6. **The experiment metaphor** — serial outputs called experiments without a materially productive experimental system.
7. **The transposition label** — adaptation or visualisation renamed transformation.
8. **The documentation takeover** — apparatus notes become more significant than the work.
9. **The autonomy myth** — model output presented as sovereign machine research while human and technical conditions disappear.
10. **The novelty claim** — statistical difference or surprise presented as epistemic or artistic novelty.

## 8. Operational implication — not yet a toolbox

Before production, a project should be able to answer:

1. What relation between art and research is being claimed here: on, for, in/through, or research becoming artistic?
2. What form of knowledge, awareness, disclosure or transformation is sought?
3. Why is artistic practice constitutive rather than illustrative?
4. Which existing works and debates make the project non-trivial?
5. What is the project-specific method or strategic wager?
6. What could the material or operation change that the starting theory does not already know?
7. What exposition is needed for public scrutiny without replacing the work?
8. Which claims remain contestable or unresolved?

These questions are diagnostic. They are not a mandatory linear workflow.
