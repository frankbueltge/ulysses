# Tranche 1 position map

## 1. The field is structured by conflicts, not by one definition

The first tranche does not yield a unified doctrine of artistic research. It yields at least four positions that overlap but cannot be collapsed without loss.

## 2. Position A — research as differentiated and publicly accountable inquiry

### Frayling

Frayling’s founding distinction separates research:

- **into** art and design — historical, theoretical or critical inquiry;
- **for** art and design — instrumental research supporting practice;
- **through** art and design — inquiry in which practice participates in producing knowledge.

His deeper contribution is negative: not every artwork or creative process becomes research by self-description. He rejects both the romantic artist stereotype and the rationalist scientist stereotype, but retains care, inquiry, external reference and communicability as important conditions.

### Borgdorff

Borgdorff systematises the field. Artistic practice qualifies as research when it is an intended, original investigation that broadens knowledge and understanding through artistic objects and creative processes. It requires pertinent questions, appropriate methods, documentation and dissemination.

At the same time, he does not simply import a scientific template. He argues that:

- subject and object are not fundamentally separated;
- theory and practice are interwoven;
- knowledge is situated, tacit and embodied;
- experimental and hermeneutic methods reveal and articulate that knowledge;
- criteria may emerge within each project.

### Strength for Ulysses

This position supplies accountability:

- a relation to an existing field;
- an intentional research claim;
- traceable context and methods;
- public exposition;
- differentiation between art practice and art practice-as-research.

### Danger for Ulysses

If implemented mechanically, it can turn into a research-management template: question, method, output, documentation, dissemination. Ulysses already tends to produce exactly this kind of procedural legitimacy while the work itself remains weak.

---

## 3. Position B — research becomes artistic in the mode of experience

### Klein

Klein relocates the issue from professional identity to modality. The relevant question is not whether an artist is doing research, but when research becomes artistic.

Artistic experience:

- is an active, constructive, aisthetic process;
- includes the subjective perspective irreducibly;
- cannot be delegated;
- fuses mode and substance;
- is itself a form of reflection;
- produces embodied or “felt” knowledge.

Artistic research may therefore occur in methods, questions, implementation, publication, evaluation or discourse. It need not be a separate institutional species from science.

### Strength for Ulysses

This position prevents Ulysses from equating research with written explanation. It makes reception and experience epistemically relevant, and suggests that a work can think through how it is encountered.

### Danger for Ulysses

“Felt knowledge” can become vague and immunise work from criticism. Without exact analysis of how an experience is constructed, any interactive or affective interface could be declared artistic research.

---

## 4. Position C — aesthetic difference and non-propositional disclosure

### Mersch

Mersch makes the strongest break with the scientific model.

Artistic research is not primarily:

- proposition production;
- thesis validation;
- method application;
- problem solution;
- progressive accumulation of results.

Artistic practice works through actions, performances, images, objects, composition, montage and dispositifs. It can reveal what was concealed, overlooked or excluded; push perception to its limits; and make contradictions or paradoxes appear.

Its reflexivity is not only commentary by the artist. The medium and practice must turn on their own conditions. Artistic searching is zetetic: it searches and simultaneously questions the search, its medium, its desire, its framing and the researcher.

### Strength for Ulysses

This position directly diagnoses Ulysses’ weak mode: discursive thought first, artwork second. It requires the work’s medial organisation to perform the research rather than illustrate a conclusion.

### Danger for Ulysses

Mersch’s position can be misused in three ways:

1. treating obscurity as non-propositional depth;
2. treating any contradiction as artistic cognition;
3. refusing documentation or criticism because art allegedly needs no public justification.

Ulysses cannot simply adopt the anti-method claim literally. As a machine-mediated public practice, it still needs source discipline, apparatus disclosure and ethical accountability.

## 5. Position D — artistic research must resist its own academic institutionalisation

### The Manifest

The *Manifest der Künstlerischen Forschung* argues that artistic research was substantially shaped by academisation and remains trapped in a false competition with university research.

It criticises:

- imported theoretical and methodological models;
- academic qualification and evaluation formats;
- state-of-the-art, methodology, progress and output metrics as default standards;
- simplistic borrowing of laboratory, experimental-system, survey and ethnographic models;
- the assumption that adding research language strengthens art.

### Strength for Ulysses

This is a direct warning against our own tendency to solve weak art by writing a more elaborate protocol, ontology or workflow.

### Danger for Ulysses

Pure refusal is insufficient. Rejecting academic form does not itself produce an artistic epistemology, a work or a credible public practice. The critique must be paired with concrete aesthetic operations and serious engagement with sources.

## 6. Position E — artistic research as situated transformation

### Schwab and the transposition volume

Transposition is not the transport of identical content into another form. It is a movement in which the identity of what moves may change.

Key consequences:

- positions are produced through movement rather than existing beforehand;
- concepts can function as underdetermined epistemic things;
- methods are hypotheses enacted in practice, not guaranteed tools;
- aesthetic and epistemic dimensions depend on each other;
- operations can transform objects, media, practitioners and criteria;
- successful work may resist integration into a final proposition.

### Strength for Ulysses

This offers a bridge between theory and work without reducing theory to illustration. A concept can initiate a concrete operation, but the operation must exceed and alter the concept.

### Danger for Ulysses

“Transposition” is broad enough to become a fashionable name for ordinary adaptation, visualisation or remix. Each case must specify what changes, why the change matters and why the operation cannot be replaced by a simpler translation.

---

## 7. Position F — the field itself is diagrammatic, institutional and plural

### Michelkevičius

Michelkevičius maps artistic research as a moving field of:

- competing definitions;
- institutional regimes;
- relations between theory and practice;
- methodological promiscuity;
- changing artist/researcher/curator identities;
- different doctoral and public formats;
- concrete local practices.

Diagrammatic knowing is not merely presentation. Mapping can become a method for navigating contested relations and producing a form of understanding.

### Strength for Ulysses

This supports a plural foundation rather than a single philosophical canon. It also makes the institutional production of the category “artistic research” part of the object of inquiry.

### Danger for Ulysses

A map can substitute taxonomy for thinking. Ulysses already has a tendency to turn conceptual problems into diagrams, graphs and interfaces. Mapping is only epistemically valuable if the selection, relation and exclusion operations are themselves consequential and contestable.

## 8. Conflict matrix

| Axis | Pole 1 | Pole 2 | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Research status | explicit criteria and public accountability | project-specific criteria and aesthetic autonomy | Ulysses needs accountability without a universal workflow |
| Knowledge | contribution to knowledge and understanding | disclosure, awareness, felt or non-propositional knowing | no single “knowledge output” field is adequate |
| Method | appropriate, documented methods | no fixed method; method emerges or is questioned | methods must be provisional and revisable |
| Artwork | outcome and carrier of embodied knowledge | singular operation that cannot be translated without remainder | work cannot be a decorative research result |
| Documentation | required for research status | risks subordinating art to discourse | documentation must expose apparatus without replacing the work |
| Rigor | intention, originality, context, methods, dissemination | exactness of aesthetic construction and self-reflexive medial operation | rigor is plural, not absent |
| Publicness | research community and wider public | singular experience cannot be delegated | exposition must enable encounter, not claim universal transfer |
| Theory | contextual and interpretive resource | danger of preforming the work | theory must remain transformable by practice |

## 9. Provisional synthesis

A defensible working statement for the foundation is:

> Artistic research is a situated inquiry in which artistic practice is not only an object, illustration or delivery channel, but a constitutive mode of investigation. Its rigor lies both in accountable relations to materials, contexts and prior work, and in the exact medial operations through which something can be experienced, disclosed or transformed that could not be obtained unchanged in propositional form. Its methods and criteria are project-specific but not arbitrary, and its exposition must keep the work, process, apparatus and claims distinguishable without reducing one to another.

This is not yet the Ulysses model. It is a provisional boundary statement to be tested against further theory and cases.
