# Concept Dossier
## Trace, différance and iterability

## 1. Status

**Theoretical status:** Supported by direct reading of Derrida's “Différance” and authoritative reconstruction of iterability and trace.  
**Operational status:** Suitable for source, version, context and repetition practices; not a general aesthetic recipe.

## 2. The problem of the pure origin

Ulysses often organises research around a sequence such as:

```text
source -> interpretation -> work -> genealogy
```

This sequence can imply that the source begins as a stable identity and that later stages merely transform or reveal it.

A trace-based account complicates this. What appears as an origin is already marked by:

- prior distinctions and vocabularies;
- technical formats;
- institutional conditions;
- absences and exclusions;
- anticipated future uses;
- the possibility of being repeated in another context.

The origin remains addressable, but it is not pure or self-sufficient.

## 3. Différance

Différance names the interdependence of:

- **difference** — a sign or object functions through relations to what it is not;
- **deferral** — meaning is never fully present at one moment but unfolds through other signs, contexts and future readings;
- **spacing** — relations require intervals, cuts and arrangements that are not neutral containers.

For Ulysses, the concept corrects two temptations:

1. treating an object as if it carried its final meaning intact across media;
2. treating a later interpretation as if it completed the object's meaning.

## 4. Constitutive trace versus documentation

A trace is constitutive when it participates in making the object or meaning possible.

Examples:

- a missing source affects what a claim can mean;
- a model version changes the conditions under which an output exists;
- a citation carries earlier contexts into a new argument;
- compression or translation alters what can be recognised;
- an interface preserves some relations while making others inaccessible;
- a correction changes the status of downstream derivatives.

A log is merely documentary when its presence or absence has no consequence for the object, claim or experience.

The practical question is:

> Which traces must remain active because removing them would change the identity, interpretation or consequence of the work?

## 5. Iterability

A mark can function again beyond the circumstances and intention of its first production. This repeatability is not accidental. It is what allows the mark to be recognised and used at all.

But each repetition occurs in another context and can alter the mark's effect.

```text
iterability = repeatability + structural exposure to contextual alteration
```

This has direct machine relevance:

- the same prompt in another model version is not simply the same instruction;
- the same dataset under another schema is not simply the same evidence;
- the same citation in another work can support a different claim;
- a copied interface pattern carries assumptions from its earlier context;
- a generated sentence may function beyond the model's or operator's intended use.

## 6. Iterability and differential repetition

The concepts overlap but should not be identified.

### Differential repetition

Emphasises how a differential condition can be reactivated to individuate differently.

### Iterability

Emphasises that repeatability is possible only because a mark is detachable from its original context, and that this detachment prevents complete contextual saturation.

Used together, they ask:

- what condition is being repeated;
- what allows the operation or mark to travel;
- what new context changes its force;
- which traces of earlier contexts remain active;
- how the repeated instance changes the series.

## 7. The context-drift test

A candidate strategy for Ulysses:

1. Select one mark, score, claim, image, rule or interface element.
2. Identify the assumptions of its current context.
3. Re-stage it in at least two defensible contexts.
4. Preserve the transformations, not only the final outputs.
5. Analyse which effects depend on context and which remain invariant.
6. Do not treat the first context or authorial intention as the final authority.

Suitable materials:

- legal or scientific statements reused in public media;
- model prompts across versions and tool regimes;
- institutional labels applied to different works;
- a dataset transferred among Meridian, Ensemble and Ulysses;
- repeated public instructions;
- archival fragments that reappear after correction.

## 8. Remainder and loss

A remainder is not automatically profound. It becomes relevant when the operation can show why something resists transfer, compression or interpretation.

A strong trace-based work specifies:

- what was expected to travel;
- what changed;
- what disappeared;
- what returned unexpectedly;
- what cannot be reconstructed;
- who or what bears the consequence of the loss.

## 9. Archive implications

The archive should not present one clean developmental line. It should preserve:

- superseded versions;
- corrections without erasing the corrected object;
- abandoned or contested relations;
- context manifests;
- transformation histories;
- absent or inaccessible sources when they materially affect interpretation;
- the difference between a record and a later claim about its meaning.

## 10. Failure tests

A trace/iterability work fails when:

- it merely lists metadata;
- every context is treated as equally valid despite factual or ethical differences;
- semantic drift is generated artificially by vague prompts;
- absence is aestheticised without specifying whose absence and why;
- the project claims that meaning is infinitely open and therefore beyond criticism;
- the original source is ignored even though the work makes factual claims about it;
- provenance becomes an ornamental transparency layer.

## 11. Ulysses implication

The strongest role for Ulysses is not to reconstruct a pure source or final interpretation. It can build forms in which the trace chain remains active and in which context changes produce perceptible, attributable consequences.
