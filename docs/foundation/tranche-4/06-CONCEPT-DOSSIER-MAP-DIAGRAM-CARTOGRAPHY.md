# Concept Dossier
## Map, diagram and cartography

## 1. Status

**Theoretical status:** Supported by direct reading of *A Thousand Plateaus*, the Research Ecology cartographic principles, *Transpositions* and critical cartography.  
**Operational status:** Mature enough to govern Ulysses maps and interfaces.

## 2. The central distinction

A map is not defined by looking like a map. A graph is not defined as a tracing merely because it uses nodes and edges.

The distinction is operational:

### Tracing

- reproduces a structure treated as already given;
- stabilises identities and relations;
- returns to the same organising grammar;
- hides or naturalises the rules of selection;
- treats unknown relations as absent or invalid;
- often presents one view as the system itself.

### Map

- constructs a situated field of relations and possible action;
- can be revised, reversed, detached or connected elsewhere;
- declares its lens and exclusions;
- can alter the relations it maps;
- allows counter-mapping and contestation;
- does not claim to exhaust the territory.

## 3. Six forms that must be distinguished

### Graph

A formal structure of vertices and edges. It can support analysis or rendering. Its openness is not guaranteed by visual complexity.

Questions:

- Who can create an edge?
- Which relation types exist?
- What cannot be represented?
- Does the graph imply a canonical whole?

### Model

A selective representation, explanation or simulation built for a purpose and domain.

Questions:

- What assumptions produce the model?
- How is adequacy tested?
- Which phenomena are excluded?
- Is it explanatory, predictive, exploratory or demonstrative?

### Diagram

An operational arrangement that relays forces, relations and possible transformations. It need not resemble the object it acts upon.

Questions:

- What can the diagram do or make possible?
- Which transformations does it stage?
- Does it merely illustrate a theory, or does it generate a new relation?

### Map

A situated projection that orients perception and action.

Questions:

- Which lens constructs this view?
- What paths or decisions does it enable?
- What changes when the map is used?
- How can it be contested or forked?

### Counter-map

A map that contests an authoritative allocation of visibility, identity, territory or agency.

Questions:

- Which authority or distribution is challenged?
- Who gains the ability to speak, see or act?
- Does the counter-map reproduce another hidden centre?

### Interface

An enacted protocol of access, sequence, permission and possible action.

Questions:

- What can a participant do?
- What is impossible or delayed?
- What data and infrastructure mediate the relation?
- How does the interface position its user?

One artefact may perform several of these roles. Each role must nevertheless be declared rather than blended into the prestige term “cartography.”

## 4. Map as experiment

A map becomes experimental when it does not merely depict known relations but creates a situation in which relations can be tested, altered or contested.

Examples:

- a lens that changes what can count as a relevant encounter;
- a map whose categories are modified by participant contestation;
- a spatial or digital arrangement that makes an exclusion consequential;
- a counter-map that reroutes institutional attention or resource access;
- a versioned mapping where incompatible lenses produce distinct objects rather than alternative styles.

## 5. Lens manifest

Every Ulysses map should carry a readable manifest:

```yaml
lens_manifest:
  title:
  author_or_practice:
  version:
  date:
  object_or_encounter:
  source_records:
  admissible_relation_types:
  filters_and_weights:
  transformations:
  known_exclusions:
  blind_spots:
  contestations:
  infrastructure:
  status:
```

The manifest is not a legalistic appendix. It identifies the conditions under which the map can act.

## 6. Cartographic power

Maps participate in producing reality by:

- naming objects;
- authorising categories;
- assigning proximity or centrality;
- making some paths visible;
- ranking or suppressing alternatives;
- converting uncertainty into absence;
- fixing scale and temporal resolution;
- defining who appears as actor, object or background.

A plural map interface can still be centralised if all lenses share one schema, one data source, one ranking system or one publisher.

## 7. `n - 1`

`n - 1` should be interpreted as subtraction of the instance that claims to organise multiplicity as one.

For Ulysses this means:

- no universal map of all research;
- no privileged self-map presented as ecology truth;
- no single scalar for openness or closure;
- no obligatory genealogy for every work;
- no final interface from which the whole can be surveyed;
- no model authorised to resolve all contested relations.

It does **not** mean:

- remove one node;
- leave one relation blank;
- create visual asymmetry;
- celebrate disconnection;
- make navigation confusing.

## 8. Line of flight and restratification

A rupture or new connection is not automatically liberating. It can be recaptured by a new category, aesthetic, platform or authority.

Every claimed line of flight therefore needs a restratification check:

```yaml
restratification_check:
  what_existing_order_was_broken:
  what_new_order_emerged:
  who_controls_the_new_order:
  what_became_possible:
  what_became_impossible:
  who_or_what_was_excluded:
  can_the_relation_be_reversed_or_refused:
```

## 9. Aesthetic form as cartographic action

A cartographic artwork should not be judged by informational completeness. Its form can:

- sustain incompatible scales;
- make navigation itself consequential;
- deny total overview while preserving orientation;
- expose how a route produces the object it reaches;
- turn omissions into visible limits rather than decorative voids;
- stage several mappings that cannot be collapsed into one.

The form is successful when the mapping operation changes perception or possible action and when that change cannot be replaced by a textual summary.

## 10. Failure tests

A cartographic work fails when:

- it uses network aesthetics as proof of complexity;
- the map reproduces the project's existing ontology;
- every source is admitted only through Ulysses' preferred vocabulary;
- the interface promises decentralisation while one actor controls all edges;
- alternative lenses change appearance but not the object or possible action;
- missing data are silently rendered as no relation;
- a counter-map has no identifiable target of contestation;
- a global overview remains the privileged endpoint.
