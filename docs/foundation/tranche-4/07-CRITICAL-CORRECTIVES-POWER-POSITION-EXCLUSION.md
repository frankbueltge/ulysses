# Critical Correctives
## Power, position, exclusion and capture

## 1. Why this document is necessary

Difference, multiplicity, rhizome, event and openness can become politically empty style terms. A system may appear non-hierarchical while relying on highly centralised infrastructure, hidden labour, proprietary models and unilateral editorial authority.

Tranche 4 therefore adds correctives that are not external ethical decorations. They affect what counts as a valid map, encounter, trace and work.

## 2. Situated knowledge

No map, model or artistic-research claim is produced from nowhere.

Each Ulysses project should identify:

- the human publisher and decision rights;
- model providers and versions;
- datasets, source languages and missing languages;
- legal and institutional context;
- infrastructure and access constraints;
- who or what is represented as material;
- who can contest, refuse or withdraw;
- which audiences are assumed.

The aim is not total disclosure. It is adequate disclosure of conditions that materially affect interpretation and consequence.

## 3. Formal multiplicity versus distributed authority

A thousand nodes do not distribute authority if:

- one schema defines all possible identities;
- one importer decides what exists;
- one ranking determines relevance;
- one interface controls public visibility;
- one human or model authors every relation;
- affected people cannot contest or leave.

Ulysses should evaluate authority at the level of operations, not appearance.

## 4. Colonial and extractive risk

Terms such as nomadism, smooth space, deterritorialisation and mapping can erase histories of land, displacement and unequal mobility when used abstractly.

Operational safeguards:

- do not universalise mobility as freedom;
- distinguish chosen mobility from forced displacement;
- identify ownership, jurisdiction and affected communities in spatial or cultural datasets;
- do not treat Indigenous or marginalised knowledge as raw material for conceptual experimentation;
- preserve source conditions, permissions and refusals;
- include counterpositions from the relevant knowledge traditions rather than using them as examples of an existing European theory.

This area requires deeper work in Tranche 5. The current requirements are minimum constraints, not completion.

## 5. Feminist and material correctives

An apparatus does not passively observe pre-existing entities. Its cuts help produce what becomes an object, actor, signal or background.

For Ulysses this means:

- authorship boundaries must be argued, not assumed;
- “the machine” must be decomposed into models, training data, tools, operators, infrastructures and institutions;
- data are made through collection and classification practices;
- excluded bodies, maintenance work and environmental costs cannot be treated as external to the work when they are load-bearing;
- changing the apparatus can change the phenomenon rather than merely its representation.

## 6. Critical cartography

A map critique must ask both:

1. What does the map show or omit?
2. Through which conditions does it gain the authority to define reality?

Relevant conditions include:

- institutional legitimacy;
- software defaults;
- database schema;
- available geographies and languages;
- visual conventions;
- computational resources;
- editorial and moderation policy;
- rights to create, challenge or delete relations.

## 7. Capture and restratification

A project can begin as an attempt to escape a dominant order and end by producing a new standard.

Typical Ulysses capture patterns:

- every outside source becomes an Atlas entry;
- every encounter becomes a genealogy edge;
- every uncertainty becomes a named method;
- every failure becomes evidence for “Error as Method”;
- every relation is absorbed into the public Ulysses identity;
- every critique becomes a protocol improvement that strengthens the centre.

The question is not whether this can be avoided entirely. It is whether the capture is visible, contestable and reversible.

## 9. Failure tests

A project fails the corrective layer when:

- it claims decentralisation solely through form;
- it uses vulnerable groups as conceptual examples without relation or accountability;
- it treats proprietary model output as spontaneous nonhuman agency;
- it celebrates opacity while affected parties bear the risk;
- it maps extraction without changing its own extractive practice;
- it invokes situated knowledge but hides the decisive human interventions;
- it presents all interpretations as equal where evidence, rights or harm are asymmetric.
