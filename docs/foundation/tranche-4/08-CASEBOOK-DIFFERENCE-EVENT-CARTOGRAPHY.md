# Casebook
## Difference, event, trace and cartography

## 1. Status

This is a selective analytic casebook, not a canon. Cases are included because they clarify a problem in the emerging Ulysses model. They should later be expanded with direct artwork documentation, installation views, technical records and critical reception.

## 2. Robert Smithson — Non-Sites

### Problem

How can a site move without remaining identical, and how can a map act as more than a representation of territory?

### Operation

Material from a site is displaced into the gallery together with maps, containers, mirrors, photographs or text. The relation between site and non-site is not secured by resemblance. The gallery object does not simply stand in for the site; it constructs a differential circuit between positions.

### Relevance

- transposition changes the object;
- the map enables a conceptual and imaginary movement rather than total access;
- source and destination remain non-identical;
- the work's logic is immanent to its concrete arrangement.

### Warning

“Site/non-site” cannot become a generic label for every source/interface relation. Smithson's material, sculptural and institutional specificity is part of the work.

## 3. Marcel Duchamp — Three Standard Stoppages

### Problem

Can a standard remain neutral when repeated through singular material events?

### Operation

A nominally identical procedure is repeated. Each fall produces another line, and the resulting forms become alternative standards.

### Relevance

- repetition exposes singularity rather than confirming identity;
- the standard becomes material for inquiry;
- the operation is exact enough for deviation to matter;
- measure, chance and form are inseparable.

### Warning

The work is not evidence that random generation is intrinsically artistic. The historical and institutional relation to measurement and standardisation is essential.

## 4. Annette Arlander — Animal Years

### Problem

How can long-duration repetition transform performer, site, score and relation rather than merely accumulate documentation?

### Operation

Related performances are repeated over years in changing locations and conditions. The series is not one invariant action plus scenery; the work emerges through the shifting relations among body, place, duration, seasonal conditions and technical recording.

### Relevance

- repetition can be environmental and situated;
- the repeated act becomes a device for perceiving conditions that a single performance cannot expose;
- the work and its documentation co-individuate over time.

### Warning

Duration alone is not rigor. A long series can still become routine if prior results do not alter attention, score or interpretation.

## 5. David Pirrò — Staging Collisions

### Problem

How can scientific data be transposed without merely visualising it?

### Operation

Neural simulation data are approached through time, behaviour, correlation and aesthetic staging rather than through the scientists' standard reduction to aggregate quantities. The resulting object compounds data, scientific apparatus and artistic decisions.

### Relevance

- data are treated as `facta`, produced through apparatuses;
- transposition resists univocal comparison with source data;
- aesthetic and epistemic operations are coupled;
- collision changes the relations among practices.

### Warning

Resistance to integration does not exempt the work from describing what data and transformations were used.

## 6. Bureau d'Études — political cartographies

### Problem

How can cartography expose infrastructures, power and dependencies obscured by conventional representations?

### Operation

Dense maps connect corporate, military, institutional and technological structures. They counter official segmentation by making ownership and power networks visible.

### Relevance

- mapping is an intervention into political legibility;
- the map contests authoritative divisions of domains;
- visual density can communicate systemic entanglement.

### Warning

Density can become totalising or inaccessible. A counter-map can still imply mastery if its sources, categories and omissions are not contestable.

## 7. Forensic Architecture

### Problem

How can models, media traces and spatial reconstructions function as public evidence under contestation?

### Operation

Distributed media and sensor traces are synchronised, modelled and spatialised to reconstruct events. The outputs operate across legal, journalistic, activist and exhibition contexts.

### Relevance

- trace chains are consequential rather than decorative;
- models have declared evidentiary purposes and domains;
- public exposition is part of verification and contestation;
- transposition across regimes changes both form and responsibility.

### Warning

Forensic work is not a style for artistic research to imitate. Its evidentiary standards, affected parties and legal consequences differ from speculative art.

## 8. Ulysses — The Third Pile

### Problem

Can Ulysses' own classification or interpretive premise be contradicted by the material it examines?

### Operation

A bounded corpus is tested in a way that can weaken the initial thesis rather than only exemplify it.

### Relevance

- external material exerts resistance;
- the research question can change;
- the work is stronger as adversarial audit than as autonomous aesthetic event.

### Limitation

Its research strength does not by itself establish a necessary aesthetic form.

## 9. Ulysses — Inaccurate Citations

### Problem

Can a verification instrument become an object of recursive verification?

### Operation

The citation checker and its interpretation are tested against primary material. A machine reading of the result is corrected rather than preserved for narrative coherence.

### Relevance

- technical-object reversal;
- trace and correction remain active;
- the apparatus can lose authority;
- interpretation is not identical with instrument output.

### Limitation

The work risks remaining an excellent research instrument or essay unless its aesthetic register produces an encounter not replaceable by documentation.

## 10. Ulysses — Differential Reproduction

### Problem

Can simulated mutation and persistence generate epistemic novelty?

### Operation

A fully specified artificial system produces variable configurations under internal rules.

### Relevance

- useful as exploratory simulation;
- repeatable and technically legible;
- shows how a system can produce unpredicted configurations.

### Negative finding

Its objects, viability and novelty are defined by the system itself. The simulation does not meaningfully expose its ontology to an outside return loop. It demonstrates variation more strongly than event or epistemic individuation.

## 11. Ulysses Atlas and Pulse — negative/diagnostic case

### Problem

Can a source graph and research pulse function as an open cartography?

### Operation

Sources are classified through a fixed vocabulary and related to Ulysses threads and works. Closure and vitality are represented through self-authored measures and graphs.

### Diagnostic value

- creates useful bibliographic and historical structure;
- records Ulysses' own reading;
- makes internal assumptions inspectable.

### Negative finding

- outside sources enter through Ulysses' existing categories;
- source-to-thread-to-work relations create an assimilation pipeline;
- scalar closure metrics recentre Ulysses as the authority on its own openness;
- the graph can be mistaken for the ecology rather than one local assertion.

This case directly motivates the cartographic audit and the move from canonical genealogy to versioned local maps.

## 12. A failed “rhizomatic” interface — generic negative case

### Pattern

An interface uses animated nodes, free navigation and nonlinear links to claim rhizomatic form.

### Why it fails

- the data model remains fixed and hierarchical;
- all nodes pass through one editorial authority;
- the user can browse but cannot alter, contest or fork relations;
- “nonlinear” means only that links can be clicked in several orders;
- the interface hides exclusions and unavailable paths;
- visual complexity replaces epistemic or political plurality.

### Lesson

A rhizomatic appearance is neither a map nor a line of flight. The operative rights, schemas and consequences matter more than the graphic style.

## 13. Casebook synthesis

The strongest cases share no single medium. They do share a pattern:

```text
specific material or situation
+ explicit apparatus
+ differential or transpositional operation
+ consequential trace
+ form that changes the encounter
+ claims limited to the operation
```

Weak cases tend to substitute one of the following:

- generative variation for differential production;
- complexity aesthetics for cartography;
- documentation volume for constitutive trace;
- surprise for event;
- theoretical vocabulary for material consequence.
