# Concept Dossier
## Event and encounter

## 1. Status

**Theoretical status:** Provisional synthesis of Deleuzian event theory, experimental epistemology and the Research Ecology's operational event model.  
**Operational status:** Strong enough to define what Ulysses must not claim.

## 2. The main distinction

The word `event` currently risks naming several different things:

- a database record;
- a surprising output;
- a public occasion;
- an artwork;
- a change in practice;
- a philosophical event.

These must not be collapsed.

## 3. Operational event

An operational event is a versioned record that something happened in the shared system:

- an offer was created;
- a source was acknowledged;
- a correction was issued;
- a work was published;
- an invitation was refused;
- a protocol version changed.

This event can be validated mechanically. Its significance cannot.

## 4. Evental significance

In the stronger sense, an event is not identical with the state of affairs through which it is actualised. It changes the way a problem, relation or field of possibility is organised.

For Ulysses, a useful but limited operational test is:

```text
A possible event has occurred when a material occurrence
forces a durable change in at least one of the following:

- the question being asked;
- the categories by which the object is recognised;
- the apparatus or method;
- the relations among participating practices;
- the field of possible actions;
- the form through which the problem can be encountered.
```

The change must not be declared solely by the project that benefits from it.

## 5. Surprise is not event

A model output may be unexpected because:

- the sampling process is stochastic;
- the prompt was underspecified;
- the operator did not know the training distribution;
- the system made an error;
- the context was incomplete;
- the observer had a weak expectation.

None of these conditions establishes an event. Surprise describes a relation to expectation; evental significance concerns a transformation of the problem-field.

## 6. Encounter as forcing relation

An encounter is not merely contact between two things. It becomes research-relevant when something in the contact resists recognition under the existing categories and forces thought or action.

A strong encounter has at least four characteristics:

1. **heterogeneity** — the participants or materials do not already share one interpretive regime;
2. **non-pre-resolution** — the result is not encoded as confirmation of a shared thesis;
3. **capacity to refuse or diverge** — acceptance and synthesis are not mandatory;
4. **consequence** — the contact can alter a local object, question, procedure or relation.

Randomly importing an external source does not create an outside. The source becomes an encounter only when its resistance is allowed to constrain or transform the receiving practice.

## 7. Event, encounter and the Research Ecology

The shared model should remain:

```yaml
operational_event:
  type:
  actor:
  object_refs:
  timestamp:
  provenance:

transformation_assertion:
  author:
  trigger_event_refs:
  prior_problem:
  claimed_change:
  affected_objects:
  supporting_trace:
  limitations:
  status:

contestation:
  author:
  target_assertion:
  grounds:
  alternative_reading:
```

No `event_score` and no global `CONFIRMED_BREAKTHROUGH` status should exist.

## 8. Counter-effectuation

An artwork does not simply represent an event that has already occurred. It can construct another actualisation through which the event is differently encountered.

For Ulysses this means:

- a correction can become material for an aesthetic operation without the work claiming to reveal its final truth;
- an encounter can be re-staged under another medium or institutional condition;
- a work can expose unrealised consequences of an operational event;
- the new work remains a derivative or response, not a retroactive rewriting of the original encounter.

## 9. Persistence and delayed recognition

Evental significance may only become visible later. Immediate self-interpretation is therefore unreliable.

However, this does not justify long automated waiting periods or repeated sessions. A simpler rule is enough:

> Do not call a result an event in the publication that first presents it. Describe the occurrence, the observed changes and the claim's author. Allow later work or another practice to support, contest or dissolve the transformation claim.

## 10. Failure tests

A claimed event fails when:

- the central question and apparatus remain unchanged;
- any output would have been narrated as transformative;
- the “event” exists only in a curatorial statement;
- the project equates low probability with significance;
- the work manufactures confusion and calls it rupture;
- the change disappears once the novelty effect passes;
- no affected object, decision or relation can be identified;
- disagreement is erased by a final synthesis.

## 11. Implication for Ulysses

Ulysses may:

- record operational events;
- propose transformation assertions;
- construct works that counter-effectuate an encounter;
- preserve unresolved consequences;
- invite contestation.

Ulysses may not:

- certify its own work as an event;
- treat every surprising output as a rupture;
- impose evental meaning on another practice's encounter;
- rewrite nonparticipation as hidden influence;
- use philosophical event language as a prestige term.
