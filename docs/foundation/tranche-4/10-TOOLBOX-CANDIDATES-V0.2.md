# Toolbox Candidates v0.2
## Differential, evental, trace-based and cartographic strategies

## Status

These are **methodological hypotheses**, not automatic methods, creative presets or proof of artistic quality.

Version 0.2 retains the strongest strategies from Tranche 3 and adds strategies derived from difference, event, iterability and cartography. Each candidate must be justified by a concrete problem and can be rejected even when theoretically relevant.

## A. Retained and revised strategies

### 1. Adversarial premise test

**Basis:** An inquiry differs from a demonstration when material can contradict its central interpretation.

**Operation:** Formulate one load-bearing premise that a bounded corpus, material test or independent procedure can weaken.

**Use when:** factual, historical, classificatory or apparatus claims are involved.

**Do not use when:** the central issue cannot be meaningfully rendered as a premise without destroying the aesthetic problem.

**Failure test:** Every possible result is narratable as confirmation.

---

### 2. Technical-object reversal

**Basis:** A supposedly stable instrument can become the underdetermined object of inquiry.

**Operation:** Turn the adequacy, calibration or boundary of an instrument, classifier, prompt, map or interface into the problem.

**Use when:** a technical object silently authorises the phenomenon.

**Failure test:** The work reveals a known limitation but leaves the instrument and its use unchanged.

---

### 3. Trace-chain exposure

**Basis:** Source, trace, data, model, interpretation and projection are distinct transformations.

**Operation:** Select one load-bearing transition and make its gain, loss or exclusion consequential.

**Use when:** a public claim depends on compression, translation, cleaning, classification or model mediation.

**Failure test:** The result is a metadata dashboard whose form does not alter the encounter.

---

### 4. Differential series

**Basis:** Productive repetition requires continuity, consequential difference and recursive effect.

**Operation:** Repeat a score or apparatus while changing one load-bearing condition. Preserve the sequence and allow each result to alter the next operation.

**Use when:** the changed condition can be attributed and matters to the object.

**Failure test:** A parameter sweep generates variants but no change in problem, category or rule.

---

### 5. External return loop

**Basis:** Models become experimentally stronger when they return to material inquiry.

**Operation:** Let a model propose an observation, intervention or source query; allow the resulting trace to revise or reject the model.

**Use when:** simulation, classification or generative hypotheses have a meaningful external correspondence.

**Failure test:** External material can never change the model.

---

### 6. Agential-cut comparison

**Basis:** Apparatuses produce provisional boundaries of object and agency.

**Operation:** Construct two defensible cuts around the same situation and expose how each produces different phenomena, responsibilities or exclusions.

**Use when:** authorship, dataset scope, encounter membership or platform/work boundaries are disputed.

**Failure test:** The operation produces two rhetorical opinions rather than materially different arrangements.

---

### 7. Transductive interface

**Basis:** A disparity can propagate through interfaces and co-individuate the participating terms.

**Operation:** Construct a sequence where each material or technical passage changes the conditions of the next.

**Use when:** responsive systems, live performance, evolving scores or cross-practice transfers are involved.

**Failure test:** Source, target and rules remain fully fixed throughout.

---

### 8. Singular measure

**Basis:** Repetition can expose the contingency of a standard and the singularity of cases.

**Operation:** Apply a stable measure repeatedly until its own adequacy becomes material.

**Use when:** thresholds, rankings, evaluation standards or temporal/spatial measures have institutional force.

**Failure test:** The cases vary but the standard never becomes questionable.

## B. New Tranche 4 strategies

### 9. Problem-field mutation

**Basis:** A consequential event alters the problem, not merely the answer.

**Operation:** Preserve the initial problem statement, then document the exact point at which evidence, form or encounter makes it inadequate. Recompose the work around the mutation rather than hiding it.

**Use when:** an inquiry reveals a category error, omitted actor or changed unit of analysis.

**Contraindication:** Do not invent a vague “deeper question” after weak results.

**Failure test:** The revised problem is only a more impressive wording of the original.

---

### 10. Context-drift test

**Basis:** Iterability allows a mark to travel while exposing it to contextual alteration.

**Operation:** Re-stage one mark, rule, score, claim or interface element in at least two defensible contexts. Preserve changes in force, interpretation and consequence.

**Use when:** source material travels across practices, model versions, institutions, media or languages.

**Contraindication:** Do not create arbitrary contexts merely to produce difference.

**Failure test:** The work assumes the original intention remains final and treats later uses as corruption only.

---

### 11. Constitutive-trace subtraction

**Basis:** Some traces are necessary to the object's identity; their removal changes the object rather than only its documentation.

**Operation:** Remove or withhold one trace, version, caveat or contextual relation, then expose the resulting change in interpretation or action. Restore it and compare.

**Use when:** provenance, missingness, corrections, translations or version histories are load-bearing.

**Contraindication:** Do not withhold information in ways that create factual deception or harm.

**Failure test:** Removing the trace changes nothing beyond the completeness of the archive.

---

### 12. Series resonance

**Basis:** Heterogeneous series may become related through a mediator visible primarily in its effects.

**Operation:** Construct two independently coherent series and test a specific discrepancy, threshold or mediator through which changes in one become legible in the other.

**Use when:** legal, scientific, aesthetic or infrastructural series intersect without a shared ontology.

**Contraindication:** Do not label an unexplained correlation as a mysterious connector.

**Failure test:** The two series are simply juxtaposed and the relation exists only in the curatorial text.

---

### 13. Counter-effectuation

**Basis:** An artwork can actualise another aspect of an event without claiming to reproduce or complete it.

**Operation:** Begin from a documented event or encounter and construct a new situation that exposes unrealised consequences, tensions or possibilities.

**Use when:** corrections, refusals, silences or unresolved encounters have ongoing effects.

**Contraindication:** Do not overwrite the original participants' account or retroactively insert Ulysses into the event.

**Failure test:** The work merely narrates the event with a new aesthetic surface.

---

### 14. Lens collision

**Basis:** Maps are situated operations; incompatible lenses can produce different objects rather than alternative styles.

**Operation:** Apply two explicit lens manifests to the same records. Expose where entities, relations and exclusions change, and prevent automatic synthesis.

**Use when:** provenance, governance, material, conceptual or affected-party maps disagree.

**Contraindication:** Avoid false equivalence between a documented evidentiary lens and an unsupported speculative lens.

**Failure test:** Both lenses use the same schema and only change visual encoding.

---

### 15. Counter-map with consequence

**Basis:** Critical cartography contests distributions of visibility, authority or possible action.

**Operation:** Identify an authoritative map or schema, construct a situated counter-map, and specify what relation, decision or access condition it aims to change.

**Use when:** institutional, territorial, platform or archival classifications have effects.

**Contraindication:** Do not present an alternative map as politically transformative without an affected relation or public.

**Failure test:** The counter-map has no consequence beyond representing more information.

---

### 16. Restratification check

**Basis:** A line of flight can be captured by a new order.

**Operation:** After a rupture, fork or refusal, identify the new categories, centre, infrastructure and exclusions that emerge.

**Use when:** a project claims decentralisation, openness, refusal or escape.

**Contraindication:** This is not a cynical proof that all change is impossible.

**Failure test:** The project celebrates rupture while its new authority remains invisible.

---

### 17. Non-relation preservation

**Basis:** Silence, refusal, incompatibility and absent relation can be legitimate states.

**Operation:** Preserve a non-connection without converting it into hidden influence, failure or future task. Make the conditions and consequences of non-relation visible.

**Use when:** an offer is refused, vocabularies are incompatible or evidence does not support a relation.

**Contraindication:** Do not aestheticise neglect or use silence to evade responsibility.

**Failure test:** The system secretly reintroduces the relation through ranking, metadata or narrative implication.

Before a strategy enters a score:

```yaml
  selected_strategy:
  why_this_strategy_and_not_another:

  differential_condition:

  possible_problem_mutation:
  aesthetic_form_hypothesis:
  affected_parties_and_power:
  contraindications:
  failure_test:
  stop_condition:
```

## D. Combination rule

Use the smallest number of strategies required. Combining several philosophical terms does not strengthen a project.

A valid combination must state:

- which strategy is primary;
- what each additional strategy contributes;
- where the strategies conflict;
- which one governs if their requirements diverge.

## E. Toolbox failure

The toolbox itself fails if:

- it becomes a menu for autonomous idea generation;
- Ulysses chooses strategies because they match its vocabulary;
- every work can be redescribed as using several strategies;
- sources become retrospective justification;
- the strategies produce a recognisable house style;
- the failure tests never actually kill a project;
- the toolbox replaces close reading, material encounter or artistic judgment.
