# Concept Dossier: Apparatus, Intra-action and Transduction

## 1. Purpose

This dossier compares two related but non-identical theoretical lines frequently used in artistic research:

- Karen Barad's account of apparatuses, phenomena, intra-action and agential cuts;
- Gilbert Simondon's account of individuation and transduction, mediated here mainly through Paulo de Assis and the Transpositions volume.

Both challenge the image of pre-existing subjects acting on passive objects. Neither should become a vague vocabulary for “everything is connected.”

## 2. Apparatus and intra-action

In a simplified interaction model, independent entities first exist and then affect one another.

Barad's term `intra-action` points to a different claim: determinate entities and boundaries emerge through material-discursive arrangements. An apparatus does not merely observe a phenomenon; it helps constitute what can appear as the phenomenon and how agencies are distributed.

For Ulysses this implies:

- a dataset is not a neutral object prior to collection and classification;
- a model output cannot be separated from training, prompt, interface and selection;
- a visitor role is produced by permissions, navigation and available actions;
- the distinction between “machine decision” and “human decision” may be enacted differently across the workflow;
- exclusions are part of the phenomenon, not secondary metadata.

## 3. Agential cuts

An agential cut is a situated boundary-making operation through which components become provisionally distinguishable and accountable.

Useful questions include:

```text
Where does this project draw the object boundary?
What is treated as apparatus rather than object?
Who or what is granted agency?
Which exclusions make the result possible?
Could another cut produce a different phenomenon?
Who remains responsible for the cut?
```

The concept does not mean that boundaries are unreal or optional. It makes their production and consequences available for critique.

## 4. Diffraction rather than reflection

Diffraction is often contrasted with mirror-like reflection. It attends to patterns of difference produced through encounter and overlap rather than asking whether one representation accurately mirrors an original.

For Ulysses, a diffractive reading would not simply compare two theories or sources side by side. It would trace where reading one through another changes the problems, exclusions or agencies that become visible.

Misuse begins when `diffraction` becomes a poetic synonym for any interdisciplinary mixture.

## 5. Simondon and transduction

Transduction describes a process in which a disparity is progressively restructured across an interface or series of interfaces. Form is not imposed on passive matter from outside. A metastable field contains tensions and potentials; individuation resolves some of them while generating a new field of conditions.

De Assis applies this to musical practice by treating work, performer, technical environment and material intensities as co-individuating. The performer is not a sovereign centre applying a fixed form. Nor is the material an autonomous author.

For Ulysses, the strongest operational consequence is:

> Do not begin with fully formed terms and then connect them. Study the interfaces through which the terms themselves become determinate.

## 6. Transduction versus transposition

The concepts overlap but should be separated.

### Transposition

Emphasises movement from one position, medium or regime to another, and the alteration produced by that move.

### Transduction

Emphasises progressive individuation through a propagated disparity and the transformation of the field across interfaces.

A claim-to-interface movement may be a transposition. A sequence in which the claim, interface, visitor role and classification regime all change through iterative coupling may be transductive.

Not every translation is a transposition, and not every evolving process is transduction.

## 7. Machine participation

These theories complicate the phrase “the AI made the work.”

A more precise account asks:

- Which capacities were delegated to which model?
- Which outputs became consequential through human selection?
- Which constraints came from code, provider or source corpus?
- Which agencies emerged only in the coupled process?
- Which decisions remained asymmetrically human and legally accountable?
- Which material or public responses changed the arrangement?

Distributed agency does not imply symmetrical agency.

## 9. Misuse risks

### Generalised entanglement

If everything is entangled, no specific relation or responsibility is described.

### Agency laundering

Distributed agency can hide the decisive human act of selecting, publishing, funding or exposing others to risk.

### Mystified emergence

Calling an unplanned output emergent without reconstructing the material conditions that produced it.

### Retrospective transduction

Describing any iterative workflow as co-individuation after the fact.

### Political flattening

Treating model, artist, participant, platform and affected person as equivalent nodes despite unequal rights and consequences.

## 10. Provisional consequence

Ulysses should treat apparatus analysis as constitutive of the work's foundation, while using intra-action or transduction only where the project can show specific boundary-making or progressive transformation.

The practical test is not whether the vocabulary sounds processual. It is whether the work can demonstrate:

- which terms were produced by the arrangement;
- where the relevant boundary was enacted;
- what propagated across which interface;
- and how responsibility remains assignable.
