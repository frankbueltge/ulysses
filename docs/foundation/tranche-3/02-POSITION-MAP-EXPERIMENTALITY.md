# Position Map: Experimentality
## Experiment, demonstration, simulation, repetition and artistic trial

## 1. Purpose

This map does not define one correct model of artistic experimentation. It marks tensions that any future Ulysses model must hold open.

## 2. Main conflict axes

| Axis | Pole A | Pole B | Consequence for Ulysses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Outcome | demonstration: result structurally known in advance | research experiment: relevant outcome not fully determined | a coded effect that merely illustrates a theory is not enough |
| Object | pre-given object observed by a neutral tool | phenomenon co-produced by apparatus and intervention | prompt, model, data and interface must be treated as constitutive |
| Stability | technical object: repeatable, functional, stabilised | epistemic thing: materially accessible but not yet conceptually determined | both are needed; pure openness is unusable and pure stability is sterile |
| Repetition | reproduction of the same | differential reproduction: enough continuity for differences to become consequential | random variation without inheritance or comparison is not research |
| Indeterminacy | uncontrolled randomness | designed underdetermination inside a bounded system | chance must be situated by a score, rule, material and trace |
| Material record | volatile trace | durable data and model | every conversion adds and removes meaning; the chain must remain inspectable |
| Scale of claim | singular aesthetic event | generalisable scientific result | artistic research may be rigorous without claiming universal law |
| Reflexivity | apparatus hidden behind a result | apparatus exposed or turned into material | reflexivity must alter the work, not only add disclosure prose |
| Relation to theory | theory as template to execute | theory as provisional orientation changed by practice | no work should simply visualise a concept dossier |
| Novelty | statistical rarity or model surprise | difference that persists, changes the system or opens a new problem | rarity, randomness and novelty are not equivalent |
| Evaluation | technical success | epistemic and aesthetic consequence | a correct build can still be a failed work and failed research |
| Agency | autonomous artist or machine acts on material | agencies emerge in a specific human–technical–material arrangement | public authorship must not hide asymmetry and responsibility |

## 3. Four positions in the field

### Position A — Scientific experimental system

A stable arrangement produces traces around an epistemic object. Technical objects, protocols and preparations allow the system to reproduce itself while differences become investigable. The goal is not necessarily immediate confirmation, but the production of phenomena that can later be conceptualised.

**Strength:** strong account of how unknown knowledge becomes materially tractable.  
**Risk in art:** importing laboratory vocabulary and mistaking formal resemblance for epistemic equivalence.

### Position B — Aesthetic experiment as singular and reflexive

Mersch's Duchamp reading places the experiment in a different register. The exact setup does not establish a universal law; it exposes contingency, the arbitrariness of measure and the performance of experimentation itself. Repetition produces singular cases.

**Strength:** preserves artistic specificity and the non-general character of aesthetic knowledge.  
**Risk:** singularity can become a romantic excuse for arbitrary form or lack of public accountability.

### Position C — Apparatus realism and intra-action

Barad's vocabulary, as reconstructed by Arlander, rejects a world of already separate subjects and objects. Specific apparatuses enact temporary cuts and make phenomena intelligible in particular ways.

**Strength:** makes the apparatus, exclusions and responsibilities constitutive.  
**Risk:** loose metaphors of entanglement can make every relation sound profound and erase actual differences in power or function.

### Position D — Transductive and processual individuation

Simondon, via de Assis, shifts attention from form imposed on matter to processes in which form, performer, material and technical system individuate together. A disparity propagates through interfaces and creates a new configuration.

**Strength:** supports practices in which material and method genuinely transform one another.  
**Risk:** almost any evolving process can be retrospectively described as transduction.

## 4. What the positions agree on

Despite their differences, the reviewed lines support several shared constraints:

1. Research cannot be reduced to producing many outputs.
2. An experiment requires a relation between stability and openness.
3. The apparatus is not neutral.
4. Material traces matter; interpretation alone is insufficient.
5. Repetition is productive only when differences can be compared and retained.
6. The unknown must be operationally exposed, not mystified.
7. A result can transform the question, the instrument or the practice.
8. Artistic research must not claim scientific validity merely by borrowing experimental language.

## 5. What remains contested

- Whether artistic works can themselves be epistemic things or only generate epistemic situations.
- Whether nonpropositional insight can be publicly assessed without being translated into discourse.
- How much technical reproducibility an artistic experiment requires.
- Whether a simulation can count as an experiment when all relevant phenomena are produced inside the model.
- Where artistic rigor ends and methodological domestication begins.
- Whether the same work can be simultaneously an aesthetic experiment, a scientific instrument and a public exposition without one register dominating the others.

## 6. Provisional position for Ulysses

Ulysses should occupy neither pole exclusively. The strongest working position is:

> Build a sufficiently stable apparatus to generate and preserve comparable traces, while leaving one load-bearing relation genuinely underdetermined and allowing the material result to revise the initial framing.

This implies a refusal of two false alternatives:

```text
not: strict protocol OR free improvisation
not: scientific result OR ineffable artwork
not: deterministic code OR random generation
not: autonomous machine OR passive tool
```

The relevant question is always more concrete:

> Which conditions must remain stable, which relation is allowed to become unknown, and what evidence would show that the system produced more than a demonstration of its own design?
