# Concept Dossier: Experimental Systems

## 1. Working definition

An experimental system is not a single experiment, instrument or workflow. It is a historically and materially organised arrangement in which:

- technical objects stabilise operations;
- an epistemic object remains incompletely determined;
- repeated interventions produce traces;
- differences can be compared across time;
- results feed back into the arrangement;
- the system can alter the question it was built to address.

Rheinberger's concept emerged from histories of laboratory science. Its transfer to artistic research is therefore a hypothesis, not an identity claim.

## 2. Core components

### Technical objects

Instruments, protocols, standards, code, preparations, storage formats and routines that must work reliably enough to support inquiry.

### Epistemic thing

The materially engaged object of research whose decisive properties are not yet known. It is not simply hidden information waiting to be retrieved; its identity can change through the experimental process.

### Trace production

The interaction between arrangement and epistemic thing leaves marks that can be inspected, compared and transformed.

### Differential reproduction

The system reproduces enough of its conditions for differences to be attributable, while generating enough variation for something unforeseen to occur.

### Recursive adjustment

Traces and provisional interpretations alter later interventions. A productive system is neither a one-shot artwork nor a fixed pipeline.

## 3. Experiment versus demonstration

Rheinberger's sharpest criterion is operational:

> If the relevant outcome is completely determined in advance, the setup demonstrates rather than researches.

A demonstration can still be artistically strong. The distinction concerns the epistemic claim.

Examples of demonstrations:

- a visualisation whose result follows directly from encoded rules;
- a simulation constructed to display an already accepted theory;
- an interface that makes a philosophical claim interactive without placing that claim at risk;
- a generative system where randomness changes surface form but not the problem.

Examples closer to research experiments:

- a repository audit that can refute the project's own diagnosis;
- an instrument whose failure reveals a previously unmodelled distinction;
- a transposition where the receiving medium changes what counts as the object;
- a repeated operation whose deviations force a revision of the score or measurement rule.

## 4. Why stability matters

Pure openness produces no research object because nothing can be compared. A useful system needs stable reference points:

- versioned material;
- repeatable code or scores;
- explicit selection rules;
- preserved failed runs;
- known instrumentation limits;
- a bounded field of intervention.

Stability is not conservative by definition. It provides the contrast against which a difference can become legible.

## 5. Why openness matters

A perfectly closed system only confirms itself. Openness must therefore affect something load-bearing:

- the categorisation scheme;
- the instrument's adequacy;
- the relation between source and representation;
- the problem definition;
- the participating agencies;
- the boundary between technical and epistemic object.

A random number generator does not by itself create this openness. It creates unpredictability inside a predefined space.

## 6. Transfer to artistic research

A cautious transfer is possible when the artistic practice:

1. establishes a concrete situation rather than only a theme;
2. stabilises enough apparatus for traces to be meaningful;
3. leaves a genuine question unresolved;
4. allows material or public encounter to alter the work;
5. keeps a record of the transformations;
6. limits its epistemic claim to what the setup can support;
7. produces an aesthetic form that does not merely report the research.

## 7. Misuse risks

### The unknown as branding

Calling an object "epistemic" because it is obscure, complex or poetic.

### Laboratory theatre

Using sensors, data, code or technical vocabulary to simulate rigor without a real open question.

### Infinite iteration

Assuming that more runs or more variations increase research value.

### Retrospective inevitability

Explaining every result as exactly what the theoretical framework predicted.

### Self-contained novelty

Designing a closed artificial world, defining its novelty metric, and then treating internally generated novelty as external discovery.

## 8. Operational test

Before naming a Ulysses project an experimental system, answer:

```yaml
stable_technical_objects:
  - What must remain reliable and repeatable?

epistemic_object:
  - What exactly is not yet determined?
  - Could its identity change?

trace:
  - What material mark will the interaction leave?
  - Can the trace be separated from later interpretation?

possible_disconfirmation:
  - Which outcome would undermine the starting claim or setup?

feedback:
  - How can the result modify the next intervention or terminate the project?

externality:
  - What resists the system beyond model sampling or author preference?
```

If the `possible_disconfirmation` and `externality` fields are empty, the project is likely a demonstration or speculative work, not a research experiment.
