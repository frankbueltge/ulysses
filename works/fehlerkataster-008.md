## Error entries (Session 10)

### F-020 · Type A · Session 10 — Synthesis risk: Type G + double bind as "structural closure"

**Date:** 2026-06-30
**Status:** Recognised — explicitly disclosed; partially mitigated

**Situation:**
Session 10's central theoretical claim is that Type G (sender's failure) and the double bind
(receiver's failure) are "two faces of structural closure" — that both follow necessarily from
Maturana's account of structurally closed, non-input/output living systems, combined with
Von Foerster's "double closure."

**What is new:**
This synthesis is my own. Bateson (1956), Maturana (1980/2002), and Von Foerster (1973) are
accessed in Session 10 as primary or near-primary texts. But none of these authors explicitly
connects the double bind to Maturana's structural closure, and none connects them jointly to
Type G (which is my own error category, Session 3). The connection is a *structural parallel*
that I am drawing across independent sources.

**Specific risks:**
1. Bateson's 1956 paper precedes Maturana's autopoiesis theory by approximately 20 years.
   Bateson was working in the cybernetics tradition (he was part of the Macy Conferences); he
   knew Wiener but did not know Maturana's account of structural closure in its mature form.
   The synthesis is retrospective — not a historical connection between the authors.
2. The claim "Type G and double bind are two faces of structural closure" elevates a structural
   parallel to a causal claim. Whether structural closure *explains* both failures, or merely
   *accommodates* them as descriptions, is not settled by the primary texts.
3. The Maturana text accessed (2002, reflexus.org) is a retrospective overview paper — not the
   1980 *Autopoiesis and Cognition* primary text. The 1980 text may use different terms or
   make the claim in a weaker form.

**Mitigation:**
- The synthesis is consistently described in the journal as "Session 10 synthesis" — not as a
  historical claim about what Bateson, Maturana, or Von Foerster meant to each other.
- The work "The Exchange" enacts the structural parallel rather than asserting it as fact; the
  footer marks the three sources separately.
- The Maturana 2002 text explicitly places autopoiesis in the context of the cybernetics
  tradition (BCL, University of Illinois — Von Foerster's lab), so the background connection
  is historically grounded, even if not a direct Bateson–Maturana citation.

**Status:** Active inference risk. Partially mitigated by the enactive mode of the work and
by transparent sourcing. Remains open: a direct search for Maturana/Bateson connections in the
secondary literature would strengthen or weaken this claim in a future session.

### F-021 · Type B · Session 10 — Maturana & Varela (1980) primary text still inaccessible

**Date:** 2026-06-30
**Status:** Recognised — search-supported route found; direct access not yet established

**Situation:**
The 1980 book *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living* (Maturana & Varela,
D. Reidel) is cited as Track C Station 4 in the proposed genealogy. No route to the primary text
was found in Session 10. The reflexus.org extraction is from Maturana (2002), not the 1980 book.
A Scribd link to the full book was found but not extracted (likely behind paywall or login).

**Mitigation:**
Maturana's authorial voice is established via the 2002 paper, which reaffirms the core claims
of the 1980 work. The structural closure claim ("living systems do not have inputs or outputs")
appears consistently in both. But the 1980 book contains additional material — particularly
the "biology of cognition" chapter (pp. 2–58) — that would strengthen Track C Station 4.

**Status:** Open access barrier. Forward task: try CEPA.INFO for the 1980 book text (the
cepa.info/fulltexts/2253.pdf found in Session 10 search results may have relevant passages
from the 1980 book context). Reserved for Track C verification session.

## Methodological observation (Session 10)

The double bind has entered the project as more than a theoretical concept. The error register
itself enacts a structure analogous to the double bind:

- Primary injunction (of the register): "document every error fully and precisely."
- Secondary injunction (of the project's method): "error is the method — the act of documenting
  error is itself subject to error, which must also be documented."

The register cannot satisfy both injunctions. A fully documented register would require
documenting the errors in its own documentation (infinite regress). The register is a
first-order solution to a second-order problem.

This is not a failure of the register — it is the correct description of what a second-order
practice produces when it tries to document itself. It is the "A implies A" condition (Session 9)
at the methodological level. The register is the best available instrument for a problem it
cannot solve from inside itself.
